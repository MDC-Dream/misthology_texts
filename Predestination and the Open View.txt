                         Predestination versus Free Will
                                                   Introduction
       As was noted in The Outer Darkness (TOD), Calvinists generally make a technical distinction between
election and reprobation. Election is taken to refer to positive predestination to salvation. Reprobation is taken
to refer to negative predestination to damnation. Arminians base God’s predestination on His prescience. This
latter position has been adopted herein, with Thiessen’s popular exception that God’s predestination for
salvation from Hell (i.e., soteriological election) is not based on foreseen faith and faithfulness but simply on
foreseen faith. This faith is the passive punctiliar appropriation of eternal life by believing in Jesus as the
Messiah. Simply put, God foresaw those who would trust in Jesus for eternal life and predetermined that such
people would go to Heaven regardless of their perseverance in faith or faithfulness. However, two major
objections to this approach need to be briefly addressed.
                                                 Objection One
       In response to Thiessen, Vance uses Buswell as his literary foil to articulate the linguistic objection that
kata (according to) in 1Pet 1:2 does not syntactically base predestination on foreknowledge; rather, it shows
that God’s foreknowledge and predestination are simply in harmony with one another. The biblical text does
not prove dependency of either one on the other. Thus, although most English texts translate the verse as saying
that believers are elected according to the foreknowledge of God, advocates of this alternate view say that it
would be better to understand the verse as affirming election in accordance with the foreknowledge of God.
Geisler, who says that there is no logical order in this verse, has made this position popular. He claims that the
two actions are coextensive, speaking of “God as knowingly determining and determinately knowing” (italics
his).1 Molina, the author of middle knowledge, also held to this view.2 Although I do not share Geisler’s or
Molina’s perspective on this matter and retain the preference for according to, the following models will
accommodate advocates of this alternate position, such as Vance, by presuming that the dichotomy between
these two approaches need not be absolute.3 What God has supratemporally or temporally chosen in accordance

                                                 Objection Two
       The second problem noted by Vance in response to Thiessen is a theological one in that if God foreknows
what is going to happen, then it is going to happen anyway, and foreknowledge is thought to be the same thing
as foreordination. There is no real difference between the two.7 Erickson sums up Thiessen’s understanding of
the Arminian position as saying that God “wills what he foresees will happen,”8 to which he then responds by
claiming, “It should be noted that if certainty of outcome is inconsistent with freedom, divine foreknowledge,
as the Arminian understands that term, presents as much difficulty for human freedom as does divine
foreordination. For if God knows what I will do, it must be certain that I am going to do it.”9
                                              Key to Foreknowledge
       Erickson finds the Arminian solution too simplistic since he believes that it basically has God
predestining what He knows in simple foreknowledge is going to happen anyway.10 Predestination becomes a
rubber stamp of God’s foreknowledge. In contrast, Erickson takes what may be described as a middle
foreknowledge (i.e., middle knowledge) position in which God knows everything that can happen and chooses
which of these possibilities will happen. But Erickson counters the rigid determinism of his fellow Calvinists,
who reject free will and believe that God causes all things (even sin). Erickson does so by advocating a moderate
Calvinistic position which affirms that the will of God must be broken down into will1 and will2 in order to
maintain the distinctive existence of permissive will.11 God will permit certain things to happen that He does
not actually want to happen. God does not sovereignly will or cause sin. He sovereignly permits it, although He
is morally opposed to it. According to Erickson, there is a way to maintain God’s sovereignty and man’s free
will: “The key to unlocking the problem is the distinction between rendering something certain and rendering
it necessary. The former is a matter of God’s decision that something will happen; the latter is a matter of his
decreeing that it must occur” (bold his.) 12 Accordingly, in prescience, will ≠ must. Vance takes the same position
and states: “Those who deny God’s absolute omniscience [prescience] often labor under the misconception that
it is God’s foreknowledge of a future event which renders it certain.”13
       This comment by Vance is well taken herein. But this assessment by Vance unwittingly repudiates his
second criticism of Thiessen’s position. God’s knowing that an event will happen does not make it happen.
Vance then proceeds to quote Craig, a well-known proponent of middle knowledge:

      From God’s foreknowledge of a free action, one may infer only that action will occur, not that it
      must occur….For God’s knowledge, though chronologically prior to the action, is logically
      posterior to the action and determined by it. Therefore, divine foreknowledge and human
      freedom are not mutually exclusive.14

        Again, we encounter this necessary distinction between precognitive will and must with the added insight
that, although God’s foreknowledge is chronologically prior to man’s choice, His foreknowledge is logically
posterior to man’s free choice and therefore determined by man’s choice. Vance then concludes, “God’s
absolute omniscience provides the only key to the reconciliation of God’s ‘sovereignty’ and human freedom.”15
The key, then, to maintaining sovereignty and freedom, according to moderate Calvinists and those (like Vance)
who reject Calvinism altogether, is to acknowledge the duality of God’s will (i.e., the existence of sovereign
and permissive will) by making a temporal and logical distinction between will and must. And such a distinction
is compatible with modern day advocates of middle knowledge. Making this will-must distinction allows us to
reject the second objection against Thiessen’s position. Just because something is certain to happen does not
mean that God has sovereignly decreed that it must happen. In God’s foreknowledge, He sees what will happen
and can even share that information with us in the form of prophecy, but this does not mean that He necessarily
makes it happen. Rather, He allows it to happen; He permits it to happen. Some of the things that are certain to
happen are an expression of God’s permissive will rather than His active will.
        For example, God will cause Satan to be bound and then unbound (Rev 20:2,7). But God will not twist
Satan’s arm to make him deceive the nations after he is released (Rev 20:8). Satan will do this of his own free
will. He will not be able to point his finger back at God and say, “God made me do it.” Neither can we say,
“The devil made me do it,” much less can we claim, “God made me do it.” That God allows us freedom does
not make Him responsible for our abuse of that freedom.
        Those outside the Marrowistic camp may raise an objection at this point: How can the present work
disallow the soteriological distinction between must and will (found in Arminianism and Calvinism) and yet
avow a causal distinction between will and must regarding foreknowledge and predestination? In other words,
how can I insist that will = must in Calvinism and yet logically maintain that will ≠ must in prescience.
        By way of explanation, in the soteriological arena, Calvinists claim that what will happen must happen
for the believer to reach Heaven. In other words, Calvinist assert that believers will persevere because God will
irresistibly cause them to persevere, and this causal perseverance is necessary to reach Heaven. This
perseverance is God’s gift, and thus Calvinistically nonmeritorious. God will make the believer persevere. This
perseverance is claimed by some Calvinists to be a characteristic of those who will reach Heaven, not a
condition for reaching Heaven. Supposedly, this soteric perseverance is a description not a condition. Such
semantic word plays, however, do little to conceal the fact that it is a necessary characteristic. Those who fail
to meet this description will go to Hell. In reality, this Calvinistic characteristic is a condition for salvation
from Hell. In Calvinism, it is necessary to persevere in order to reach Heaven. Even if this perseverance is
reputed not to be necessary for (initial) justification, it is certainly necessary in Calvinism to reach glorification
(and thus for final justification). Those who persevere will reach Heaven’s glorious shores; those believers who
fail to swim the last mile will drown in the Lake of Fire. What will happen—perseverance—must happen to
escape the Calvinistic Hell. For this reason, any such artificial soteriological distinction between the description
and condition is rejected herein. Consistent Calvinists, such as Peterson, acknowledge that perseverance is a
condition for final salvation in Calvinism.16
        If perseverance is required to reach Heaven, then it cannot simply be brushed off as a nonmeritorious
characteristic or description of those who are going to Heaven. The Bible likewise teaches that perseverance is
not a gift since the Scripture conditions rewards in Heaven on meeting this meritorious description. The
potential rewards are many and varied, as seen in the misthological formula to him who overcomes…I will
grant: a white stone, white garments, a new glorified name, ruling authority over the nations, the right to eat of
the tree of life, the hidden manna, a crowning experience of life, to be a pillar in the heavenly community, and
the right to sit on Christ’s throne (Rev 2:7,11,17,26; 3:5,12,21; 21:7). Those who meet this misthological
description of an overcomer will be entitled to these merited benefits. Meeting the biblical description of what
it means to be an overcomer is a misthological necessity and, therefore, a condition of having these rewards.
This description of an overcomer is a condition as to what it means to be an overcomer. You have to overcome
to be an overcomer. This description is a condition, not a foregone conclusion. God does not irresistibly cause
us to persevere; otherwise, the exhortations to persevere would be pointless.
        God is the one who reliably perseveres. The believer may or may not persevere. God perseveres in

        What will happen is not irresistibly caused to happen. This statement is true regarding the distinction
between must and will on both the soteriological battlefield and in the present discussion on predestination. The
fact that God foreknows who will believe does not mean that God irresistibly causes them to believe. That God
foreknows that a certain individual will believe does not mean that the person must believe. If God foreknows
the event will happen, then it will happen. But it does not necessarily follow that the event must happen just
because God foreknows that it will happen. On the contrary, God foreknows who will believe because they will
believe. It is false to say that they will believe because God foreknows they will believe. The causal relationship
is of such a nature that God’s resultant foreknowledge is contingent upon the freewill response of His creatures
within the parameters of His permissive will. The fact that something will happen does not mean that it
necessarily will happen or that it must happen; rather, it is permitted to happen (will ≠ necessarily will ≠ must).17
        Election is based on foreknowledge. Anyone can believe and become one of the elect in doing so. Thus,
you can become one of the elect by believing. It is up to you. Faith is a free, rather than forced, response. To
illustrate syllogistically, a simple argument for unlimited atonement would be:

         1. Christ died for sinners.
         2. I am a sinner.
         3. Therefore, Christ died for me.

Likewise, a similar arrangement for election would be:

         1. All who believe are consequently chosen.
         2. I believe.
         3. Therefore, I am consequently chosen.

We do not believe because we were chosen; rather, we were chosen because we believe. Although God
foreknows who will believe, His knowledge is based on who will believe. His foreknowledge is logically
subsequent to their faith although chronologically prior to their faith. He knows who will believe because they
will believe. Speaking casually, they do not believe because He knows they will believe.
                                             Necessity of Election

they are elected because God foresaw that they would believe. Vance initially responds that such election would
be unnecessary because if the foreknown event is certain to occur, then it is already predetermined to occur
without God deciding that it will happen. To recapitulate Vance’s criticism of Thiessen: If future events will

happen. But a weakness in Vance’s criticism of Thiessen surfaces as Vance and others proceed to acknowledge
that the solution to this dilemma is to insist on a distinction between will and must, with an affirmation of
permissive will.

                                             Natural Knowledge
       From His knowledge of infinite possibilities, God foreknew everything that could happen. Thus, by virtue
of His natural knowledge, He knew all possible worlds that He had the power to create. This could be pictured
as bag of marbles. Each of these marbles represents a sphere of reality that God might choose to actualize.
Therefore, prior to making a decision as to what to make, logically God had to be cognizant of what He could
make.

                                                  Moral Knowledge
       However, God cannot create a world that would be contrary to His own internal standards and nature.
His moral knowledge limited His options as to what He should create. He could not create a world in which
good would be evil or in which evil would be good. His voluntarism is limited by His own nature. In other
words, the reality God chose to create (actualize) is not right simply because God chose to make it. Rather, God
chose to make it because it is right. Certainly, the standard of right and wrong is Himself, not something outside
Himself.23 Consequently, His internal essential standard of right and wrong limited His options. This might be
pictured as taking three marbles out of the bag for active consideration.24 From these last three candidates, God
was morally free to choose any one of the three. And He chose a world in which all the free creatures He created
should love Him and should believe in Him.

                                              Middle Knowledge
      In narrowing down the selection of possible worlds from three to one, God had to consider what would
happen if He chose that one world in particular. His knowledge of what would happen is called middle
knowledge. Of course, middle knowledge comes in the middle of the above chart. God knew what would
happen if He created this world with morally free agents within a certain set of circumstances. The
circumstances would not cause them to respond in a predetermined way. But the circumstances would give
them opportunity to respond. For example, placing Adam in a Garden with the Tree of Knowledge did not force
Adam to eat of the tree. But it did give Adam the opportunity to make a choice as to whether to eat from it. And
God foreknew what that choice would be.25

                                              Volitional Selection

intellectual perception. Next, however, comes God’s volitional selection (VS) as to what reality He would

reality. Regardless as to which type of knowledge is entailed, it cannot be equated with His selection. One is
intellectual; one is volitional. Foreknowledge should not be lexically or philosophically fused with
foreordination as though the latter is part of the former. Though often linked, they are distinct. Even though
God selects which world He will sooner or later create, He does not create the world at this time. The decision
at this point as to what He would create must not be confused with His decision to proceed with the action of
create later. At this point, He decided which world He would ultimately create, thus selecting our temporal
universe with morally free agents composed of angels and men who would have the power of free choice and
not do what they should do by compulsion. This is pictured above by God selecting a particular world to create.

Certainty versus Necessity
      Given the complexity of the above subject matter, a more simplistic summation of the latter part
concerning foreknowledge and what will and must happen may be helpful. Picirilli provides an overview based

on three words: Certainty, Contingency, and Necessity. Certainty of the future simply means that it will occur
(i.e., certainty = will). The future is certain. What will happen will happen. The fact that God foreknows what
will happen does not make it any more certain. What will happen is already certain by virtue of its being the
future. God not only knows what could happen; He knows what will happen. The latter type of knowledge refers
to the actual future as opposed to the merely potential future. God knows what actually will happen. This type
of certainty may be broken up into two subgroups depending on the nature of the cause for the action—
contingent certainty and necessary certainty.
        Contingency means that the free agent causes what will happen to happen by making a choice between
two or more courses of action (i.e., contingency = choice). Thus, a future event may be both certain and
contingent. That is, if it will happen in the future, then it is certain to happen. But if it will happen because of a
choice that will be made, then it is, more specifically, contingently certain. God’s foreknowledge of the future
no more determines the future than our post-knowledge of the past determines the past. Likewise, prophecy
need not be anything more than telling what will happen. Whether what is prophesied simply will happen or
must happen is determined by whether the agent is given a choice. Thus, when Jesus told Peter that he would
deny Him, Peter was not forced to deny Christ. Peter had a choice. Jesus simply knew what choice Peter would
make in those circumstances. Neither Jesus’ foreknowledge nor His prophecy of the circumstances forced Peter
to make the choice that he did.
        On the other hand, if a future event is due to some necessity, then an irresistible force causes the future
event. The object of that force has no choice about whether to act in the manner prescribed by the force acting
upon it (necessity = no choice). The agent is not free to make a choice. Therefore, a future event may be certain
and determined. In such a case, it is necessarily certain the future event will happen in a particular way since
no other way is possible.

Supratemporal Conclusion
       God chose the best course of action (including the best type of creation to create) that would allow Him
to reveal Himself for who He is through this creation to His creatures. This does not mean that God had no
choice because He foreknew His choice. The same argument that allows man to have freedom of choice also
allows God to have free will as well. Although God’s knowledge is chronologically prior to His choice, it is
logically posterior to His choice. His foreknowledge of the choice He would make is logically posterior to the
choice He actually does make. As stated above, what God has supratemporally28 or temporally chosen in
accordance with His foreknowledge is logically chosen according to His foreknowledge. This position
maintains both the free will of God and man.29
       So how do we answer the riddle: Did God do what He did because He knew what He would do, or did
God know what He would do because He did what He did? Some think that they can cut this Gordian Knot by
simultaneously affirming both—God coextensively knew and did that which He did and knew. One possible
answer provided herein, in contrast, is that it depends on where in the sequence of events the question is being
posed. At the most fundamental level, God foreknew what He would do because He would subsequently do
what He did. His foreknowledge of His own actions chronologically preceded His action, but His
foreknowledge of what He would do was logically based on what He would do. He naturally knew all
possibilities and which of these possibilities He could morally and freely choose and which of these He would
choose as most feasible. He also knew what our resultant choices would be and His own responses as well.

Prescient Will ≠ Chronological Must
        For example, God foreknows whether you will finish reading this book. But this does not mean that you
must finish reading it. If you were to finish it, then God would have foreknown that to be the case. If you were
to decide not to finish it, then God would have foreknown that to be the case. God has given you the power of
free choice to decide whether to finish reading it. God did not determine what you would do; rather, you
logically determine what God foreknew would happen as a result of the free will He gave to you. To further
illustrate, let us assume that you will finish reading this book. Even so, it is obvious that the following is false:

       1. If you will read every page, then you will finish reading the book.
       2. You will read every page.
       3. Therefore, you must finish reading the book.

      Naturally, it is false to conclude from these premises that you must finish reading this book. It can only
be concluded that you will finish reading, if such is the case. Likewise, it is false to reason:

       1. If God foreknows you will read every page, then you will finish reading the book.
       2. God foreknows that you will read every page.
       3. Therefore, you must finish reading the book.

      God’s foreknowledge is not causative. It does not cause you to finish reading. The fact that you will
eventually finish reading the book is the reason why God foreknows you will finish. You do not finish because
God foreknows you will finish. The opposite is true. God foreknows you will finish because you finish.
Therefore, the correct conclusion is:

       1. If God foreknows you will read every page, then you will finish reading the book.
       2. God foreknows that you will read every page.
       3. Therefore, you will finish reading the book.

       God foreknows you will finish because you will finish. In other words, although God’s foreknowledge
is chronologically prior to your action, your action is logically prior to His foreknowledge. Therefore, although
God’s foreknowledge of your finishing is chronologically prior to your finishing, your finishing is logically
prior to God’s foreknowledge.
       At this point, it would be very tempting to conclude that our freewill response is the cause for the effect—
for God’s foreknowledge. It is certainly true that whether we believe logically determines whether God
foreknows we will believe. And of course, the cause logically comes before the effect. Our positive or negative
response logically determines God’s foreknowledge of whether our response is positive or negative. But after
providing analogies similar to mine above, Craig goes on to maintain that this does not mean that we cause
God’s foreknowledge:
           The fact that Jones will actually mow the lawn is the reason why God foreknows that Jones
      will mow the lawn. Jones does not mow the lawn because God foreknows; God foreknows because
      Jones will mow the lawn. Now this does not mean Jones’s action causes God’s foreknowledge.
      The word because here indicates a logical, not a causal, relation, one similar to that expressed in
      the sentence “four is an even number because it is divisible by two.” (Emphasis his.)35
       As Craig goes on to explain, in the A-theory of time, things such as backward causation and time travel
are impossible.36 We can neither change nor cause the past. Nevertheless, it is possible for our actions to
logically determine God’s foreknowledge. Although Craig’s preference for the A-theory of time is herein
accepted, perhaps it would be permissible to affirm logical rather than chronological causation. In any event,
our posterior action logically rather than chronologically determines God’s prior foreknowledge.

continue to believe.
Since the Foundation
        As was noted above concerning apo in Rev 17:8, it is easy enough for the OV to establish the fact our names
are written from or since the foundation of the world. (Those in the OV who argue that our names are recorded
from/since the destruction of the world rather than its foundation are arguing for a viewpoint that is as insignificant
as it is improbable: a delay of only six days.) The same apo expression occurs in Rev 13:8. And in Heb 9:26 one
would not say that Christ would have needed to suffer before (apo) the foundation of the world. Rather, “He would
have needed to suffer often since the foundation of the world.” Nor could it be a punctiliar action accomplished at
one point in time, namely at the creation of the world, since the author of Hebrews is entertaining the hypothetical
alternate reality of the suffering having occurred often since that point in time. Thus, the suffering would be linear
and denote continual temporal action from the beginning of the world. Likewise, in Lk 11:50, apo is used in
reference to the deaths of the prophets that occurred in linear realized reality from or since the foundation of the
world. It is asserted by OV that this would provide adequate explanation for Rev 17:8. And granted, the idea could
be that those who have not yet believed and had their names recorded are those who marvel. Thus, the Book of Life
would be considered a dynamic, ongoing, temporal record rather than static atemporal record. Accordingly, your
name would then be understood by the OV as being recorded in the Book of Life at whatever point in time you
believed since the foundation of the world.
Open View Problem
       It is then assumed by the OV that this would be adequate explanation for Rev 13:8 as well. “And all who
dwell on the earth will worship him, everyone whose name has not been written from the foundation of the world
in the Book of Life of the Lamb who has been slain.” At this point, the OV encounters a contextual problem,
however. If the text is saying that everyone who has not yet believed at that point in time will worship the beast,
then evangelism becomes impossible after that point in the tribulation, since those who worship the beast and/or
take his mark are consigned to the Lake of Fire (Rev 14:9-11; cp. 19:20). On the other hand, if the passage is saying
that those who are foreknown to never believe are the ones who worship the beast, then there is still opportunity for
those who have not yet temporally believed to do so. Since there will be those who come to faith subsequent to this

Particular Election
       Similarly, there is no reason to limit the election in Eph 1:4 to corporate election. The Book of Life renders
an exclusively corporate view of election unnecessary. God already knows in advance who will believe. Their
names, and only their names, are already permanently recorded in the soteriological Book of Life. There is little

destination of those who are in Christ and does not choose who would be in Christ is suspect. Rather, God foreknows
who will believe in Christ and chose to place them in Christ and predestined their destination to be with Christ. God
choose to put individual believers as a class in Christ.2 Particular predestination of each person in particular is thus
implicitly affirmed. The reason their names are in the Book of Life from (apo) the foundation of the world

beginning of time (2Tim 1:9). This latter text rules out foreseen works as the basis for this particular selection. It
does not rule out foreknowledge of punctiliar faith as the basis for the selection.
Corporate FG-OV
        On the other hand, even if the OV problem presented above concerning the impossibility of evangelism
during the tribulation were somehow evaded and an exclusively corporate view of pre-temporal election
nevertheless preferred, then it would seem possible for FG to adopt the OV view of the Book of Life in general and
yet still reject the OV Arminian soteriology concerning Rev 3:5. This text might still be seen as misthologically
litotetic in corporate FG-OV. In such a proposal, God does not know who will believe, but He does guarantee that
those who do believe will never perish and thus not have their names removed from the Book of Eternal Life once
those names have been entered. Those names are recorded for eternity once they are entered at some point from the
foundation of the world. Thus, a FG-OV advocate could argue that the biblical reason for stressing from the
foundation of the world rather than before the foundation of the world is to stress dynamic permanence. Not all of
God’s records and decisions were set in stone before the foundation of the world. He is actively making records and
decisions as history unfolds. However, once He makes a decision or promise within the progress of history, He is
obligated to fulfill that promise; not doing so would be a violation of His nature. God can do anything but not
everything. He cannot lie for example. Thus, He must fulfill His promise to every individual believer who is
corporately placed in Christ. This particular believer is temporally but not temporarily placed in Christ and
guaranteed a future with Christ.

Jesus Does Not Know
       In turning to the incarnation for answers to such questions, as to what God is really like and what He is
actually able to do in our temporal sphere of reality, there is an answer that readily presents itself. Matthew and
Mark both inform us that Jesus said that He did not know when He would return: “But of that day and hour no one
knows, not even the angels of Heaven, nor the Son, but the Father alone” (Mt 24:36; Mk 13:32). Jesus said that He
did not know. Axiomatically, this means God does not know everything that God knows. Likewise, Jesus marveled
(thaumazo) when He found a Gentile with great faith (Mt 8:10; Lk 7:9). Jesus was amazed, surprised even. Yet how
could He have been surprised if He knew in advance that this is the type of reaction He would receive from the
Gentile?
       Are we to take this to mean simply that God the Son does not know everything that God the Father does? It
would seem rather apparent that Jesus limited the exercise of His omniscience so that He could experience our
temporal reality from our human perspective. Or as Craig readily admits: “Obviously Jesus in his preresurrection
state did not consciously know all things.”4 Hodges likewise remarks, “However, as inadequate as it is to say it this
way, Jesus had ‘blocked’ this fact from His human mind.”5 Such astute admissions as these, of Jesus’ volitionally
self-imposed limitation, provides the basis for a suitable theory: God is able to limit His knowledge temporally,
at least in the person of His Son.
Jesus is Jehovah
        Trinitarians are well acquainted with the biblical evidence that Jesus is Yahweh. Jesus is Jehovah God. When
Thomas exclaimed that Jesus was God (Jn 20:28), Thomas used the OT designation in which Yahweh is identified
as “my God and my Lord” (Ps 35:23). For a Jew to call someone his Lord and God was to identify that person as
Jehovah. Thomas is not making a mere exclamation since he addresses these words to Jesus and since in response
to this confession Jesus says that now at last Thomas believes. Thomas addresses Jesus with both words, Lord and
God. He thus addresses Jesus as Jehovah. Jesus responds by pronouncing a blessing on those who believe likewise.
In Is 9:6 Jesus is called “Mighty God.” In the next chapter, Jehovah is called, “Mighty God” (Is 10:21; cf. Jer 32:18).
Jehovah’s Witnesses derive their title from Is 43:10, in which Jehovah (Yahweh) refers to Himself as the great I
AM (see also Ex 3:14; 32:39; Is 40:8; 41:4; 46:4). The same designation is applied to Jesus (Jn 13:19). Jesus
blatantly uses this title in claiming to be Jehovah (Jn 8:58). He even told the Jews who were stumbling over His
deity that unless they believed that He is Jehovah they would die in their sins (Jn 8:24).
        In Is 44:8 Jehovah says, “I am the first and the last, and besides me there is no God” (NWT). There is no God
but Jehovah; He alone bears the title, Jehovah, the first and the last. And yet John makes it clear that this is Jesus’
title in Rev 1:17, where he records Jesus as saying, “I am the First and the Last” (NWT). John again makes it clear
in Rev 22:13 that Jesus is “the Alpha and Omega, the first and the last” (NWT). Yet this is how John describes
Jehovah in the very opening of Revelation (1:8): “‘I am the Alpha and the Omega,’ says Jehovah God” (NWT).
Thus, even the JW translation of these passages reveal that Jesus is the first and the last and thus that He is Jehovah.
        In Zech 12:10, Jehovah is speaking, and He is claiming that He is the one whom will be pierced: “They will
look upon Me whom they crucified.” In Jn 19:37, John applies this verse to Jesus. John says that they will look
“upon Him” whom they crucified. John uses “Him” (third person) rather than “Me” (first person) because in his
context he is not recording Jehovah as speaking. In other words, Zechariah (12:10) gives Jehovah’s direct quote,
and John (19:37) gives an indirect quote of what Jehovah said. Not only is Jesus Jehovah, He is the one speaking
in the Zecharian prophecy of His future death.

look for them in the garden. This experiential knowledge might be likened to the NT affirmation that Jesus “learned
obedience from the things which He suffered” (Heb 5:8). He experientially learned what He already intellectually

Now I know
       To move to a little less speculative OT example, the Lord said to Abraham, “Now I know that you fear God,
since you have not withheld your son” (Gen 22:12). Did this mean that God did not know beforehand whether
Abraham would sacrifice Isaac, or does it merely mean that God now had experiential confirmation of what He
intellectually knew would take place? The OV would prefer the former affirmation. My proposal can accommodate
this preference without rejecting the CV that God knew Abraham’s freewill response with absolute certainty. From
His supratemporal, unlimited, intellectual perception, God knew that Abraham would offer Isaac. Yet, if God chose
to do so, He could enter our temporal arena and suppress His knowledge of the outcome so that He could experience
the drama with Abraham. God’s not knowing the outcome was not a façade, but an accurate description of God’s
ability to enter into Abraham’s temporal experience and experience it with him. God was not locked out of the
experience by His omniscience.

         Paralambano
       As for the Greek verb that Jesus uses in this Johannine text for the rapture, it is paralambano (Jn 14:3). It is
the same word that Jesus also uses for the rapture in both Mt 24:40-41 and Lk 17:34-35. The Lord’s purpose in
coming, as Hodges notes concerning this verb, is not to take believers away from Earth but to take “them along with
Him!” (Emphasis his.)28 As a result, they will live with the Lord forever, as confirmed in the Pauline rapture text as
well (1Thess 4:17).
       This same verb which Matthew uses for the rapture (paralambano) is the same verb he uses to describe
Joseph taking Mary as his wife (Mt 1:20,24). I correspondingly propose that when Jesus comes to take us as His
Bride, this action does not necessarily entail taking a trip to the stars. Granted, paralambano can be used in the
sense of taking someone along with them somewhere else, such as on a trip. In fact, in the next chapter, Matthew
records that Joseph took his wife and Child along with (paralambano) him when he fled (pheugo) and departed
(anachoreo) for Egypt (Mt 2:13-14). But when paralambano is used to denote taking someone somewhere else,
this additional action of taking them elsewhere is imported into the proposition by a preposition or verbal addition.
       For example, Matthew also records that the soldiers took Jesus along with (paralambano) them to the
Praetorium (Mt 27:27). In this case, there is no additional verb. The prepositional phrase gives the destination.
Matthew did not have to say that the soldiers took Jesus along with them and took him to the Praetorium. There
was no need to supply a redundant verb. Nevertheless, the addition of the destination makes it clear that two actions
were involved. They grabbed Him and brought Him to the Praetorium. However, these synoptic accounts of the
rapture are strangely silent about any additional destination. Neither Matthew nor Luke says that the Lord will come
and take us along with Him back to Heaven. To be sure, John notes that Jesus is preparing dwelling places for us,
and we may suppose that we will have immediate access to these dwelling places when the Lord returns. Even so,
the focus in Jn 14:3 is on the fact that we will be with Jesus. John does not say that Jesus will take us along beside
of Himself in order to take us off with Him to those faraway dwelling places. If the Lord brings those dwelling
places with Him, so that the Heavenly Jerusalem is located with Him in the clouds, then there is no reason to suppose
that Jesus takes us with Him to subsequently take us on a trip with Him to faraway mansions. It is possible from
John’s account to suppose that Jesus simply comes to take us to Himself without posing that He takes us elsewhere.

                                                 Introduction
      The doctrine of middle knowledge (MK) comes in various forms and is used for a variety of purposes.
Originally, Luis de Molina formulated the middle knowledge view (MKV). His classical MKV (CMKV) affirms
unconditional election and thus denies transworld election. William Craig is one of its principle modern day
proponents of MKV. However, he affirms a transworld MKV (TMKV). Herein, in simple terms, I have made my
own blend of such approaches. A brief survey might help one appreciate the various flavors developed for this
formulation. MacGregor has written a helpful overview of Molina’s life and theology that will suffice for the present
survey.1
                                                      Survey
       Molina was superior to Luther and Calvin regarding the extent of grace in key passages (1Tim 2:4; 2Pet 3:9):
“Unlike Luther and Calvin, Molina affirmed that God gives sufficient grace for salvation to every person that he
creates” (p. 18).2
       Although God’s middle knowledge is the knowledge of what any potential free creatures He might create
would do, His MK is not grounded upon that free knowledge (FK). This observation avoids the grounding objection,
since God’s MK is not dependent upon His creature for His MK. (Otherwise, MK would equal FK.) “Molina’s
doctrine of middle knowledge avoids this objection entirely, since he does not base middle knowledge on God’s
looking ahead and seeing what potential free creatures would do in various circumstances. Rather, Molina bases
middle knowledge squarely on God’s nature, specifically God’s innate and timelessly present attribute of
omniscience. As Molina explains, “God does not get His knowledge from things, but knows all things in Himself
and from Himself” (p. 22; emphasis his). Because God is omniscient, He knows all things. Such knowledge is
deductively intrinsic to His very nature.
       Molina grounded God’s soteriological selection exclusively in God’s free will. God predestined who would
be elected to salvation and reprobation apart from any foreseen human response:

             Remarkably, Molina believed that this face-value reading of Romans 9 was entirely consistent
      with human free will. Because Molina viewed no possible individual as bad enough so that she or he
      would freely spurn God’s grace in every conceivable set of circumstances and no possible individual
      as good enough so that she or he would freely embrace God’s grace in every conceivable set of
      circumstances, God’s possession of middle knowledge logically prior to his making any decisions
      about this world, including who would be saved or lost, provides the key to God’s sovereign individual
      predestination. Thus, for any possible individual, God has the power to elect (save) that individual by
      creating her or him in certain freedom-preserving circumstances where God already knows she or he
      would voluntarily embrace his grace. And God has the power to reprobate (condemn) that individual
      by creating her or him in other freedom-preserving circumstances where God already knows she or he
      would voluntarily spurn his grace. And God has the power not to create that individual at all by
      actualizing other circumstances where the individual does not exist. This choice of circumstances
      (leading to salvation, condemnation, or nonexistence) is unconditioned by anything about the
      individual but depends solely on the sovereign will of God. (Pp. 27-28)

       At the conclusion of a Sunday School class on the topic of election, I remarked to the teacher that God held
an election, but He gave us the deciding vote. The teacher thought I was joking. Not so, but I was abbreviating. Of
course, I was not expressing my full perspective with such a quip. In terms of the present discussion, we might say

       To express the matter a little more fully, (1) by means of His omniscience, God naturally knew the infinite
number of worlds that He could make by a sheer act of His power. Not all these worlds were feasible, however.

of what His options were morally. The best option would be to allow moral evil on the part of morally free creatures
yet curtail the amount of that evil in an optimal balance of good and evil to obtain the objective, namely, making a
misthological Bride for Christ. This was the type of world He should make. (3) His middle knowledge entailed Him
knowing what would happen in each of these feasible worlds from which He had to choose. He knew what freewill

have just as easily have chosen a different world in which these events could have played out differently.) Thus,
UVS is logically deduced. At the same time, God knew what His own responses would be to Jacob’s seeking, Esau’s
despising, and Pharaoh’s hardening. Therefore, CVE is logically inferred as well.
       Rather than thinking in terms of the latter, Paul might have been thinking in terms of the former in Rom 9.
Complexity is increased if Paul thinks in terms of both, which seems probable. For instance, Rom 8:29 could be an
example of the CVE while Rom 9:11-33 might be an example of the UVS. For that matter, Paul might think in
terms of both in the same passage, thus increasing the complexity exponentially. If Paul is a multidimensional
writer, we need to be multidimensional thinkers if we are going to understand the depth of his thought. Since Hebrew
exegesis sees multiple layers of meaning and since Scripture evidences multidimensional perspectives on other
occasions, it should occasion no surprise if one of the deepest-thinking, Hebrew-thinking writers would write at
multidimensional levels on various subjects. Indeed, given his training, culture, and genre, one would be surprised
if Paul did not write at a multitude of multidimensional levels. If justification, sanctification, and glorification are
multifaceted terms in Pauline writing, which they are, why think that election would not be also?
