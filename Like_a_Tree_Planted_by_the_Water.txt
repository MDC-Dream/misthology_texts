                                    Chapter 1. Introduction
                                                     Blessed
       From my youth, I have enjoyed memorizing Scripture. Naturally, those passages that seem to describe the
blessings of memorizing Scripture have been among my favorites. For such reasons, the opening verses of Psalms
have been very precious to me for as long as I can remember:
      1
        How blessed is the man who does not walk in the counsel of the wicked, nor stand in the path of
      sinners, nor sit in the seat of scoffers! 2 But his delight is in the law of the Lord, and in His law he
      meditates day and night. 3 And he will be like a tree firmly planted by streams of water, which yields
      its fruit in its season, and its leaf does not wither; and in whatever he does, he prospers. (Ps 1:1-3)

       I remember singing the hymnal phrase with youthful gusto, “Like a tree planted by the water, I will not be
moved.” Those words have remained a cherished part of my childhood memories and have sustained me many,
many times in my adult years, especially the promise that we will bear our fruit in our season—if we stick to our
scriptural meditation. Oh, the tantalizing thoughts of what blessings that might entail!
                                                       Perish
        Such verses always have ranked very high in my favorites, right up there with Jn 3:16. Yet I never thought
that I should equate those verses from Psalms with Jn 3:16. Upon hearing our pastor’s sermon on Psalm 1, however,
the impression I got was that he was equating the first half of the Psalm (quoted above) with the blessings of going
to Heaven and thus implicitly linking the second half of the Psalm with the doom of those bound for Hell:
      4
       The wicked are not so, but they are like chaff which the wind drives away. 5 Therefore the wicked
      will not stand in the judgment, nor sinners in the assembly of the righteous. 6 For the Lord knows the
      way of the righteous, but the way of the wicked will perish. (Ps 1:4-6)

       Indeed, some schools of thought tend to read such texts through a soteriological lens.1 The passage from
John is a what-do-I-have-to-do-to-go-to-Heaven-when-I-die kind of passage. In theological circles, such passages
are called soteriological. Soteriology deals with the doctrine of salvation in relation to Heaven versus Hell.
Accordingly, this Psalm is likened to the NT sheep-and-goat passage (Mt 25:31-46), describing the fate of those
bound for Heaven as opposed to the fate of those bound for Hell—with the presumption that good people go to
Heaven and bad people go to Hell. The perishing of the chaff in this Psalm is frequently thought to be an OT
anticipation of the NT teaching that the chaff is cast into the eternal fire of Hell (Mt 3:12; Lk 3:17), again reinforcing
the impression that bad people (whether they be described as chaff or as goats) go to Hell, while good people
(described as sheep or as trees bearing good fruit) go to Heaven.
       Thus, if our pastor had pursued that course of interpretation, he would have been interpreting this Psalm from
a commonly held perspective. As it is, though, on this particular occasion at least, our pastor left the interpretation
as to what it meant to perish within this Psalm open-ended, both in this sermon and his follow-up sermon dealing
with the second Psalm, which is a companion to the first Psalm. The conclusion of the second Psalm is particularly
applicable:
      12
        Do homage to the Son, lest He become angry, and you perish in the way, For His wrath may soon
      be kindled. How blessed are all who take refuge in Him! (Ps 2:12)

       The juxtaposition is between being blessed and perishing, just as it was in Psalm one. Might this perishing
be equated with the soteriological perishing in Jn 3:16, thus reinforcing the outlook that bad people go to Hell? The
Psalm itself does not actually say. Like the psalmist, our pastor left it to the congregation’s imagination as to what
the fate of the wicked would be in contrast to the fate of the righteous. Nevertheless, by linking the first half of

                                                      Spiritual Water
       In my childlike simplicity, I never had thought to equate Ps 1:3 with Jn 4:14 as our pastor did. Although I
had memorized both passages many years before, and even today would conclude that both verses are using the
imagery of water to picture spiritual life, yet to equate the passages simplistically was not something I would have
done even in my childlike naivety. To be sure, both passages talk about water. Indeed, both passages probably are
using this imagery to picture spiritual life flowing within the believer. I would share such a conviction with our
pastor.
       My question regarding our pastor’s association of this Psalm with Jn 4:14, therefore, is directed more so to
the logical implications of those who assume a thoroughgoing soteriological interpretation. The proper perspective
gleaned from Jn 4:14 is that in order to go to Heaven, you must have this living water flowing within you. Living
water pictures eternal life. In this verse, Jesus is promising the woman at the well, “But whoever drinks of the water
that I shall give him shall never thirst; but the water that I shall give him shall become in him a well of water
springing up to eternal life.”
       The simplicity of our pastor’s sermon in linking the streams of water in Ps 1:3 with the spring of water in
Jn 4:14 matched the simplicity of my youthful impression, but at the opposite end of the soteriological spectrum.
As a youth, I had been raised to believe in eternal security. As an adult, my convictions have gravitated to a more
robust, industrial strength version of eternal security. This full-fledged form of eternal security is more appropriately
named unconditional security. Not only is the believer’s security pertaining to salvation from Hell eternal, it is also
unconditional. Once a person believes in Christ for eternal life that person is not only eternally secure but also
unconditionally secure. Even if that person were to cease to believe in Christ, that individual would remain saved
from Hell because his or her salvation from Hell is unconditional after the point in time in which he or she first
believed. Or to use Jesus’ analogy, after just one drink of living water, the person would never thirst again for living
water in the soteriological capacity. Those holding this point of view are called securitists.2
       Alternatively, conditionalists3 pose that Jesus was telling the woman that she had to keep on drinking spiritual
water in order not to thirst spiritually. Yet such a claim misses the obvious contrast in the passage between earthly
water and spiritual water. In contrast to earthly water, the water Jesus was offering would satisfy her thirst eternally
by just one drink. As a believer in Christ, I already have taken that drink. I no longer thirst for the gift of eternal
life. That thirst has been satisfied already, both fully and permanently. Such is the message of Jn 4:14.
       My pastor, on the other hand, acknowledges that, given such a perspective of security as mine, he
contrastively believes in conditional security. Defining our terms carefully is necessary though; otherwise, we will
misunderstand one another’s perspective. So allow me a moment of clarification. Lutzer provides his perspective
regarding security as follows: “Unconditional security teaches that the God who chose his people to eternal life will
indeed lose none; they who have been redeemed by the blood of Christ will assuredly be saved.”4 Actually, Lutzer’s
description might suffice for a definition of eternal security, but it is woefully inadequate for a description of
unconditional security.

                                              Calvinists versus Securitists
        From the Reformed perspective, those chosen for eternal life (i.e., the Calvinistic elect) assuredly will be
saved but not unconditionally saved since they must persevere. Indeed, Lutzer himself admits as much and
acknowledges that historic Calvinism teaches that “continual obedience is necessary for salvation.”5 In other words,
according to these historic unconditional securitists (to use Lutzer’s perspective), you must persevere in order to
reach Heaven. Obviously, though, those Calvinists holding this perspective are really conditional securitists. They
condition reaching Heaven upon perseverance.
        To his credit, Lutzer points out several weaknesses in this historic Calvinistic perspective. I will mention but
two such weaknesses from his discussion. Historic Calvinism tends to deny that genuine believers can fall away
from the faith even though the Bible gives evidence that genuine believers can fall away and thus incur severe
temporal wrath and even die prematurely in this fallen state without losing their salvation from Hell (e.g., Heb 6:1-
6; 10:26-31). Additionally, as Lutzer notes, historic Calvinism also agrees with Arminians in conditioning salvation
from Hell on continual obedience in the form of perseverance so that good works are made a necessary part of that
salvation and thus a condition to reach Heaven within both theological systems.6
        In contradistinction, Lutzer describes other Calvinists as recognizing that believers can backslide and fail to
persevere; nevertheless, these backslidden believers are acknowledged to reach Heaven: “True believers can indeed
backslide and even turn away from Christ. The conditional passages are interpreted to refer to loss of blessing and
rewards. Obedience is necessary for rewards.” I might interject a question here: “Could it be that the blessing posed
in Psalm 1-2 for obediently meditating on Scripture is a reward for the obedient believer while the perishing could
be understood as being applicable to the loss of rewards for backslidden believers?” The intellectually virtuous
would have to consider this contemplation in any case.
        For now, let Lutzer continue, “Those who are truly regenerate will be eternally saved, even if they fail to
persevere in holiness, and fall into carnality and failure.”7 By not listing any weaknesses for this perspective and
by offering it as an alternative to the weaknesses of the historic Calvinistic position, Lutzer implicitly identifies
himself as holding this latter position, which is my perspective also. Thus, from my perspective, I would have to
consider the possibility that Lutzer’s comments are applicable to Psalm 1 and investigate the plausibility of applying
the first half of Psalm 1 to spiritual believers and the second half to carnal believers.
        Historic Calvinists would rule out serious consideration of such a possibility as a priori, of course, in that
they, as Lutzer points out, disallow the possibility of carnal believers. On the other hand, for securitists, such as
Lutzer and myself, the existence of carnal believers cannot be ruled out categorically. Consequently, a passage such
as the one before us must be examined with such a possibility in mind.
                                                Mediating Position
       In a discussion over breakfast with my brother and our pastor on such matters, our pastor indicated a
preference for a mediating position. He would allow the possibility of backsliding. He and I both have a Baptist
background. In fact, we both attended the same Southern Baptist Seminary. A strength among Southern Baptist is
that many within Southern Baptist circles have a history of allowing the possibility of protracted backsliding without
necessarily denying the eternal security. Some holding this perspective maintain a soft form of conditional security,
while others embrace unconditional security. Our pastor falls into the first category, while I fall into the latter.

        One reason I describe unconditional security as going further than conditional security in acknowledging the
logical implications regarding backsliding is that once it is admitted that believers can be backsliders and even die
in a backslidden state without forfeiting their eternal security, it becomes immediately transparent that the security
of those backsliders is not conditioned on their perseverance in faithfulness.8 Consequently, the most a conditionalist
could hope to insist upon in that case is that the retention of regeneration is conditioned on perseverance in faith.
For that reason, soft conditionalists will insist that they affirm salvation by faith alone although they condition that
salvation on perseverance in faith. Reformed Arminians, for example, advocate such a position. Nevertheless, even
this soft conditionalism suffers some major setbacks.
        For one thing, even soft conditionalism is still conditioning salvation on performance since perseverance in
faith is rewardable and thus a work. For rewards are always based on works. More germane to the present discussion,
nonetheless, soft conditionalism undermines its own position when it acknowledges that backsliding is possible but
not necessarily detrimental soteriologically; for then the soft conditionalist would have to admit that a significant
number of classic proof texts thought to support conditional security are no longer usable as part of the
conditionalistic arsenal.
                                                     Faith-Works
       As a case in point, during this breakfast discussion, I mentioned to our pastor that I would be teaching on
James 2 that coming Sunday night at a Bible study at the nearby university. I briefly summarized my interpretation
by pointing to the context. In Jam 2:13, the issue is whether Christians will be shown mercy when they stand before
the Lord in judgment. This warning is used to introduce a discussion throughout the remainder of the chapter in
which Christians are exhorted to show works of mercy in order to keep their faith alive so that they may be justified
by faith-works before the Lord at judgment. In short, if believers have performed works of mercy, they will be
shown mercy when their works are judged. Otherwise, judgment will be merciless for believers.
                                                         Rewards
       Our pastor picked up on the obvious fact that I had allowed a faith-works association made popular by John
MacArthur. Unlike MacArthur, however, who would make such works necessary to reach Heaven, I regard such
works as necessary to receive rewards in Heaven. Being shown mercy as a result of showing mercy is a reward
issue. James is expounding upon a truth already taught by Jesus: “Blessed are the merciful, for they shall receive
mercy” (Mt 5:7). This blessing regarding mercy is part of a list of blessings, known as the Beatitudes, that are
potentially available for believers. Jesus instructs believers to have this attitude concerning such blessings, “Rejoice,
and be glad, for your reward in heaven is great” (Mt 5:12). Reward or loss of reward is at stake. The Greek word

       I live on a mountain, in the white zone, as it is called. In the winter when the tops of the mountains turn white

                                                  Soft Conditionalism
       For a hardcore conditionalist, the matter is rather simple: Condition the believers’ salvation from Hell on
their moral and intellectual virtue. For the softcore conditionalist, though, not even this problematic solution is
possible because the softcore conditionalist acknowledges that believers may backslide into carnality and even die
in a backslidden state. Thus, conditioning the salvation of believers from Hell on their virtue is not a viable option
for the softcore conditionalist. Therefore, for soft conditionalists to be intellectually virtuous, they must
acknowledge that the judgment being described by James is not dealing with salvation from Hell but with salvation
from a merciless and strict judgment of believers in terms of loss of rewards.
       Yet once that concession is made, for all practical purposes softcore conditionalism collapses into soft-shelled
unconditional security. For in that case the conditionalist would have to admit that believers can reach Heaven with
a dead faith—a faith they failed to keep alive by their works. This assessment is much more consistent with the
securitist camp, however. According to securitists, a dead faith may save a believer from Hell but not from loss of
rewards. Since these Jacobean believers did not work to keep their faith alive, their continuation in what faith they
had could not be attributed to themselves. They did not persevere in the faith; they merely continued in the faith.
Their faith is dead, not nonexistent. It is dead in the sense of being nonproductive. Soft-shelled securitists are of the
opinion that believers will continue in the faith. God preservers them in the faith. Preservation in the faith is not to
be confused with perseverance in the faith.
       The above discussion shows a practical difference between preservation and perseverance.10 If soft
conditionalists are correct in acknowledging that backslidden believers can still make it to Heaven, then they are
incorrect to think that their continuation in the faith is necessarily attributable to their performance. God would get
the sole credit for backslidden believers remaining in the faith. So even if the premise is granted that continuation
in the faith is necessary to reach Heaven, God would be the sole agent causing them to reach Heaven. They would
have reached Heaven despite their performance, not because of it. Yet this perspective effectively moves into the
field of unconditional security.
       Softcore conditionalism, therefore, suffers a decisive setback when the Jacobean context is properly
understood as allowing the possibility of backslidden and backbiting believers suffering loss of reward rather than
loss of regeneration. By pointing out that regeneration is a gift (Jam 1:17-18), James already had prefaced his
discussion by removing loss of regeneration from consideration. Because regeneration is a gift rather than reward,
regeneration, along with the imputational justification by faith (Jam 2:23-24), is not conditioned on performance
and therefore is not susceptible to loss because of poor performance. Justification by works, on the other hand, is a
misthological issue for those who already have obtained the gift of soteriological justification (and regeneration) by
faith. To concede this point effectively removes any roadblocks preventing one from embracing a soft securitistic
view of security. Indeed, this perception substantially blocks one from maintaining a soft conditionalistic
perspective.

       In the Gospel of John, Jesus is referring to two different forms of life and to two different aspects of the latter
form. To unpack that statement by means of pictures, first consider the graph of eternal life above. Eternal life is
graphed from the perspective of unconditional security in that the gift aspect of eternal life is clearly distinguished
from the reward aspect of eternal life. The gift aspect of eternal life unconditionally guarantees the person who has
believed in Jesus for eternal life that he or she will enter Heaven as a free gift. Because eternal life is eternal, the
quantity of this life goes on and on forever. The curvy line denoting one’s possession of eternal life is unbroken.
You cannot cease to have eternal life; otherwise, the life you were given when you believed would not be eternal.
Your possession of the gift dimension of eternal life is static in that it is not something you can have one day and
not the next day. Once you have it, you have it permanently.
       On the other hand, your enjoyment of eternal life can vary drastically. Having eternal life does not mean that
you will not have good days and bad days. Your experience of eternal life is dynamic. In fact, when Jesus died on
the cross, His experience of eternal life was so dynamic that it was traumatic. Nevertheless, not even His experience
of eternal life could dip below the ontological threshold. He could not lose eternal life to the extent that He no longer
had eternal life. The same may be said of any believer. Once you have eternal life, your possession of that life
cannot dip below the soteric threshold. You are still assured of entering Heaven.
       Being in Heaven is a gift, not a reward. Yet eternal life has a far greater potential than simply ushering the
believer into Heaven. The crown of life pictured in the chart refers to a reward dimension of eternal life to which
believers can aspire by means of lovingly persevering (Jam 1:12; Rev 2:10). If you want to really live it up when
you get to Heaven, then go for the crown. To help picture this duality, I will enjoin the above chart with a common
frog-horse picture.

        A rudimentary, yet crucial, observation must not be lost sight of; namely, a frog is not a horse. In terms of
the graph, the distinction being made is that a gift is not a reward. When the Bible talks about eternal life as a gift,
it is not confusing the gift of eternal life with eternal life as a reward. The law of non-contradiction is not being
violated: A is not non-A. The same thing is not being said to be two different things in the same way at the same
time.
        Biblical interpreters may speak contradictorily, but the Bible does not. When one encounters contradictory
interpretations, red flags should go up and sirens should sound in the hearts and minds of those who are seeking to
be faithful to the Bible. Passages describing eternal life as a gift can be harmonized quite easily with passages
offering it as a reward. Could such an observation provide insight into a passage like Psalm 1? If so, what would it
look like? Might a believer who meditates on God’s word be pictured as a vibrant fruit tree firmly planted by streams
of living water, dynamically drinking in the water and actively producing fruit in the form of eternal life as an
enhanced capacity to enjoy life, as a reward for meditation on Scripture; whereas those who do not, do not?
        Pause here. Go back and read Psalm 1 with that question in mind. That opening chapter of Psalms is provided
on page one of this book after all. Meditate on the passage for a few minutes. Think about it in relation to the above
frog-horse illustrations. If you do so, you might be surprised to find that you are able to see a frog-horse staring
back at you from within that passage. Go back and look. Is it your mind playing tricks on you, or is the Bible starting
to open up for you in a way it had not before?
        For those who have been able to see only a frog or a horse when reading that passage, the time has come to
consider the possibility that the Spirit intends both. If a skillful artist can draw a horse so as to cause you to see both
a frog and a horse, do not write off the possibility that the Spirit can skillfully cause the psalmist to provide a passage
that suggests images of soteriology and misthology. The Spirit has done so elsewhere in the Bible. Why not here?

                                        Illustration 6. Principlistic Application




       Principlism might be pictured as the mountain over which the plane of application would fly.7 The underlying
principles for specific statements made back then in the OT text move up the scale, in terms of general applicability,

the church age (now) or to the future millennial kingdom (then). If a future (i.e., eschatological) judgment is entailed,
then one has to determine if the nature of that judgment would be applicable to believers or unbelievers.

       As Keathley accurately has noted, being transplanted into Christ is a gift by grace through faith apart from
works. Consequently, remaining in that transplanted position is unconditional. Thus, becoming a child of God,
being adopted into His family, becoming a member of God’s family, having the Lord not impute our sin against us

gift (Rom 3:24; 5:15; 11:6; Eph 2:5,8; 3:7). Grace is the (G) grounds, and faith is the (M) means to being justified
freely as a (R) result. Faith is not a gift but the means to receiving the gift of justification. In such passages, being
saved from Hell is a gift from start to finish. In the moment in time in which a person trusts in Christ for eternal life
that person receives the gift offered by grace and thus moves into a saved state. Thereafter, the person is saved from
Hell because of the gift that already has been received. The line in the above illustrations represents being moved
from one place into another: being transplanted into Christ. It does not represent linear faith. Saving faith is
punctiliar. In the split second you believe, you are transplanted: removed from the world as the sphere of your
ontological spiritual identity and placed into Christ as a new, born again creature (2Cor 5:17).

       Perhaps you have seen the brain teaser in which two twisted nails are linked together, and you are supposed
to see if you can figure out how to separate them. When it comes to the gospel and interpreting such texts as this
one, the problem that stumps many exegetes is that they perceive the free aspect of the gospel (i.e., the gospel of
grace) and the costly aspects of the gospel (pertaining to the gospel of the kingdom) and fail to perceive how these
aspects can be distinguished, much less separated, from one another. So they end up making salvation from Hell a
costly-free proposition, which is a contradiction.
       Rather than acknowledge that their proposal results in a contradiction, they seek various ways to deal with
the tension. Some do so by pretending that costly is not costly. Herrick tried this approach and failed for obvious
reasons. Some make believe that the costly aspect is not conditional and thus claim that they cannot be charged with
conditioning salvation from Hell on performance. Lange’s attempt at this solution was futile. The passage is
certainly conditional. Some interpreters pretty much just ignore the contradictory nature of their position. Henry
seems to be a prime candidate for this approach. Other interpreters simply embrace the tension and call it a paradox.
Some give attempts at resolving the tension by denying works as a basis while affirming works as a means of
reaching Heaven. This means you have to work your way to Heaven. Still others make faith the root (or in this case
the leaves) of the tree and good works the fruit. Fruit is said to prove root. Even so, salvation is conditioned on both
the root and fruit in this scenario, even when the fruit is downplayed as mere evidence of the root. All these
approaches dovetail into conditioning salvation from Hell on performance and thus fail to separate costly from free
so as to allow the believer freely into Heaven.

for believers to rewards in Heaven. Keathley adopts this approach with considerable success by making
primary application to unbelievers and secondary application to believers. He did well to reiterate the
applicability to carnal believers in misthological terms: “A life lived for treasures on earth will perish, that is, it will
result in the loss of rewards.” The costly aspect for believers is limited to rewards. Perishing like chaff would be a
picture of misthological death for the believer in terms of rewards. Death is the forfeiture of life. Forfeiture of the
tree of life and the crown of life would be a form of misthological death.

Acknowledging that they are not disconnected with this mountain begs the question as to how they are connected.
How is the mountain home of the gods in the farthest recesses of the north connected with earthly Mount Zion?
        My answer is that these mountains are certainly connected historically (in the Adamic distant past and
Isaiah’s present), to a limited extent geographically and topologically, obviously poetically, and to a fuller extent
prophetically and typologically. The historical, geographical, and topological parallels are the seed bed for the
poetical, prophetical, and typological connections. Both Isaiah and the Canaanites, and by implication David also,
are drawing upon a historical reality that predates the flood, when the heavenly mountain city of God (known as
Mount Zion, Zaphon, Olympus, Meru, Alburg, etc.) was indeed to the north of this planet. My hypothesis, based
on an extrapolation of the OT evidence, as seen through the eyes of the NT, and probably through the eyes of David
as well, is that many years before these images were committed to Scripture, the people of this planet were aware
of this mountain city and probably even saw it. Abraham endured many trials and the testing of his faith for he was
looking for this Reward City (Heb 11:8). How could he look for it if he knew nothing about it? In NT times, this
city is called various names: Heavenly City, Heavenly Jerusalem, New Jerusalem, and Mount Zion. In deference to
the NT teaching and imagery, one might also call it the Bridal City—a reward for bridal saints. Isaiah also describes
this city in bridal terms. So this perspective is not unique to the NT. Because Abraham was looking for this Heavenly
City as his reward and because David talks about the possibility of earning the right to ascend to this city, the
probability should be granted that Abraham and David share a similar perspective about the city—a perspective
grounded in factual history, not pagan mythology.

had been distorted by pagan myths, the biblical record suggests a common heritage. One need not pose that the

the Greek myths are but a distorted picture of the reality to which the Bible accurately points.

                                The Better Resurrection and Davidic Misthopology
        The writer of Hebrews affirms that OT saints endured torture so they could obtain (i.e., earn and thus be
rewarded with) a better resurrection than they would have had if they not endured: “Women received back their
dead by resurrection; and others were tortured, not accepting their release, in order that they might obtain [tugchano]
a better resurrection” (Heb 11:35). Although tugchano may simply mean to experience, it could just as easily mean
to earn or be rewarded. The context in Greek (as in English) determines the meaning of the word. Accordingly,
tugchano in Heb 11:35 should be translated as saying that they endured torture so that they might earn, that is, be
rewarded with a better resurrection (Heb 11:35). In the immediate context, BBE appropriately translates the
compound cognate epitugchano as got their reward (Heb 11:33). The resurrection is a reward in these contexts.
Lexical considerations for the Lukan phrase, especially for worthy (kataxioo), cause me to opt for the contextually
decisive parallel in Hebrews as being appropriate to Jesus’ statement in Luke. BBE is, therefore, correct to render
the phrase considered worthy to attain (kataxioo tugchano) in this neutral Lukan context as given the reward
(Lk 20:35), specifically, of the world to come (cp. Heb 2:5). My preferred translation would be: “Those who are
considered misthologically worthy of that world and the resurrection out from the dead, neither marry, nor are given
in marriage” (TM). The world to come will be their reward—if they are worthy. So they will rule the world, but
from where? From Zion of course (Ps 110:2).
        This better resurrection entails not merely the right to rule the world but to do so from Zion. The writer of
Hebrews promises not only the subjection of the world to come to those who are worthy but also the city which is
to come, that is, the Heavenly City, Mount Zion (Heb 11:10,16; 12:22; 13:14). (1) Rulership of the world to come
(2) from the city to come is at stake. Hebrews states that the OT saints were looking for both, thus for rulership
from the city. The present inquiry is focused on the second aspect of the promise—the city. Specifically, Abraham
was looking for the Heavenly City as his reward (Heb 11:10). The OT worthies were looking for a better
resurrection (Heb 11:35) that entails a better country, that is a heavenly one that involved ruling the world from
this city (Heb 11:16).
        David knew that he would die. As one may deduce from Hebrews, David also knew that he would be
resurrected.1 In that resurrection, he wanted something better than to dwell in the world to come. He wanted to rule
in the world to come. This rulership would require that he be able to dwell in Mount Zion, the Heavenly City.
Abraham was not the only OT worthy looking for the Heavenly City. He was seeking to obtain the right to dwell in
this city by the way he lived (Heb 11:9). Living by faith, not mere justification by faith, is required for this reward.
Hebrews explicitly affirms that what Moses and (by implication) the other OT worthies were seeking was a reward
(Heb 11:26). As NT believers, we too are to seek this city by means of our suffering with Christ (Heb 13:13-14).

Out Resurrection
       Paul refers to this better resurrection as the out-resurrection (Phil 3:11). By the title worthies, I am referring,

Misthological Support of OT Background
        Whereas Paul may be focusing on a limited application of Is 25:8 in 1Cor 15:54, by making it applicable in
a limited sense to all transformed believers, in Revelation John gives the fuller intended application of the verse
from its OT background by litotetically limiting its full applicability to overcomers. By limiting its scope only to
embrace overcomers, John deepens its perspective. In Is 30:19, it is those who dwell in Zion who will weep no
more. The promise is for city dwellers, not kingdom inhabitants. Is 35:10 promises eternal joy, no sorrows, for those
in Zion (cf. Is 51:11). Is 57:18 contextually promises comfort for those who respond to His chastisement, not for
those who continue to walk in wickedness (cf. Is 57:20-21; 59:20). The promise is for those who take refuge in the
Lord and who will thus inherit the land and possess His holy mountain, that is, Mount Zion (Is 57:13). The NAS
expresses it this way: “He who takes refuge in Me will inherit the land and will possess My holy mountain.”
Conversely, the NKJ transposes the word order: “He who puts his trust in Me shall possess the land, and shall
inherit My holy mountain.” It makes little difference. The two words are synonymous and therefore may be
translated interchangeably. Those who inherit the land will be the ones who possess the city. Overcomers (i.e. bridal
saints) will possess dwelling places in the Bridal City (Jn 14:2-3). Subcomers will not.
        The Lord promises to revive those who are contrite and poor in spirit (i.e., those who have a contrite and
lowly spirit). Compare the misthological nature of this promise in Mt 5:4. Significantly, Is 60:20 depicts no
mourning in the glorified Zion. Its inhabitants not only dwell in the land, they possess it (Is 60:21). No more
mourning is a promise for those who possess the land. Inheriting and possessing the land are misthological concepts
that are limited correspondingly to overcomers. Is 61:2 says that the Lord will “comfort all who mourn.” Does this
mean that He will comfort everyone, even those in Hell? Not hardly. The context rules out those who are confined
to Hell. It also rules out comfort for those who are confined to the outer darkness, since the very next verse defines
the comfort as “the oil of gladness” and limits the comfort to “those who mourn in Zion” (Is 61:3; cp. Lk 6:25).
Their comfort consists of wearing a garland on their head as a “crown” (Is 61:3 NIV; cf. 51:10), being praised

believers, not gifts for all believers, as 61:8 goes on to explain: “For I, the Lord, love justice; I hate robbery and

it is in Mt 5:4. Jeremiah also says that the cessation of weeping is due to rewarded work (Jer 31:16). It is a reward
experienced by those in Zion (Jer 31:12-13).

                                           Bridal Righteousness in Isaiah 61:10
        Many interpreters try to shirk away from the misthopological overtones of the passage by making a pure

the host (Is 61:10). However, Isaiah merely affirms: “I will rejoice greatly in the Lord, my soul will exult in my

bridegroom decks himself with a garland, and as a bride adorns herself with her jewels” (Is 61:10). Did you see the
word freely in the biblical text? Neither did I. Such interpreters interject it. Martin appropriately rejects inserting
the word freely (BKC). The OT context is decisively in favor of Martin’s rejection.
        To be sure, a favorite OT text found chapters away affirms: “All our righteous deeds are like a filthy garment”
(Is 64:6). Fair enough. This text establishes the need for imputed righteousness. Still, it does not nullify the need of
imparted righteousness for misthological themes. For those who have been made imputationally righteous, the
possibility of imparted righteousness becomes a valid possibility. In the context of Is 61:10, imputational
righteousness is not the theme.

misthological righteousness of His robe, the nature of which is shared with His Bride (Zion). As Lang points out,
“the connection is not that of a criminal being accounted righteous but that of a bridegroom and bride decking for
the wedding....it is not a question of a person escaping penalty in a court of law.”9

will cause righteousness and praise to spring up before all the nations” (Is 61:11). Imputational righteousness is no
more intended than imputational praise. Actual praise and manifest righteousness are the obvious subject matter:
righteousness you can see, and praise you can hear. The next two verses confirm this point: “For Zion's sake I will
not keep silent, and for Jerusalem's sake I will not keep quiet, until her righteousness goes forth like brightness, and

of the psalmists leads to the conclusion that they saw an extended interlude. Their foresight would even seem to
have applicability to the NT Bride, the rapture, and the timing of the Day of Wrath (DOW).

      Chisholm notes two opposing points of view regarding the OT belief in the resurrection:

             Because the Davidic ideal portrayed in the royal psalms is fully and finally realized

     it suitable for coronation ceremonies as well.

Direct-Yet-Inclusive Prophetic Psalms
                                              Davidic-Messianic Rulership
       A second quandary, not considered by Chisholm, perhaps because he is blinded by his BAAB hypothesis, is
whether the direct-versus-indirect models are necessarily mutually exclusive. Chisholm, Constable, and Spurgeon
seem to assume an either-or mentality: (1) Only if it is indirectly messianic is it inclusively messianic (so that
indirectly = inclusively). Otherwise, (2) if it is directly messianic, then it is exclusively messianic. But are we really
limited to these two options, to take the psalms as either inclusively (thus indirectly) messianic or exclusively (thus
directly) messianic? This premise of mutual exclusivity is certainly open to question.
       A mediation seems more prudent. My proposal is that David saw both an immediate and ultimate fulfillment.
He saw a direct application to his immediate situation but an additional inclusive applicability to the Messiah. David
saw that he was only the shadow of ultimate messianic fulfillment. A direct-yet-inclusive model (DYIM) is to be
preferred. Kaiser challenges those like MacKay (and thus those like Chisholm, Constable, and Spurgeon) who take
an either-or approach to the psalms:

                                Excurses on Bridal Mystery
       In a compilation, Bullinger, Fry, and Welch argue that the Bride of Christ is not the epitome of misthology.
Rather, they contend that there are three ascending levels of reward: (1) terrestrial glory (here on Earth—for faithful
Jews, thus God’s Wife), (2) celestial glory (Heavenly Jerusalem on Earth—for Christ’s Bride, that is, overcomers),
and (3) cosmical glory (the heavenlies—for Christ’s Body, that is, the Church of the Firstborn).1
       From this they will argue that the Church is a mystery and therefore composes a level of revelation and glory
superior to the Bride. Their argument goes like this:

    1. A NT mystery refers to something completely unknown but now revealed.
    2. The bride of Christ was known in the OT.
    3. Therefore, the bride is not a NT mystery.

       Dispensationalists like Walvoord would likewise conclude that a NT mystery does not refer to something
partially revealed in the OT.2 If the Bride of Christ is hinted at in the OT, however, then Walvoord’s thesis fails, as
does the syllogism by Bullinger. The major premise of their syllogism appears to be flawed, leading to Bullinger’s
strained analysis. The fact that we can look back in hindsight and see the NT Bride in the OT does not mean that
the NT Bride was not concealed in the OT. This assessment is consistent with how the NT uses the word
mystery/mysteries in Mt 13:11; Mk 4:11; Lk 8:10. Jesus presented the message of the kingdom in mysterious
parables to conceal and reveal kingdom truth. The Lord’s presentation of the kingdom in mysterious teachings does
not mean that the kingdom truths were completely unknowable, however. A better syllogism would be:

    1. A NT mystery refers to something concealed yet revealed.
    2. The Bride of Christ was concealed in the OT but revealed in the NT.
    3. Therefore, the Bride is part of the NT mystery to which Paul alludes in the Ephesians.

       This conclusion is consistent with Revelation picturing the Bride and New Jerusalem as the epitome of
misthology. In relegating these items to a mid-tier level reward, the counter argument by Bullinger and company
falters badly in this regard. Their discussion is nonetheless very commendable in recognizing the City and Bride as
reward issues. And their analysis is intriguing in picturing God’s Sinai wife as being limited to the earthly realm
