                                                  Key Terms
        Misthologist: refers to an advocate of unconditional soteriological security who has a sufficient degree of
 misthological competency so as to recognize that the biblical teaching regarding the outer darkness pertains to
 misthological themes so that salvation from the outer darkness depicts conditional misthological security.
        Misthology is a coined term derived from the Greek words misthos (reward) and logos (teaching). It and its
 derivatives will be used to refer to matters pertaining to the doctrine of rewards.
        Mistholic is an abbreviation for misthological (that has been shortened syllabically to match its counterpart
 soteric, which, in turn, is the abbreviation for soteriological).
        Soteriology is based on two biblical Greek words, soteria (salvation) and logos (teaching), and correspondingly
 refers to biblical teaching about salvation. Although the biblical concept of salvation includes many dimensions other
 than deliverance from hell, it is this aspect of salvation to which writers refer frequently when they use the word
 soteriology. Thus, salvation from hell (or more specifically, from eternal damnation in the Lake of Fire) will be referred
 to as soteriological salvation.
       Overcomer is used herein to refer to faithful believers.
       Subcomer is a coined term used herein to refer to unfaithful believers.
        Unconditional security is the conviction that once a person believes in Christ for eternal life, that person is
 unconditionally secure in Christ and unconditionally assured of reaching Heaven unconditionally. Technically, this
 articulation is superior to saying that believers are unconditionally assured of reaching Heaven. In Calvinism, God
 unconditionally elects certain individuals to reach Heaven through means of their perseverance. The elect not only will
 persevere, they must persevere in order to reach Heaven. Within Calvinism, reaching Heaven is conditioned on the
 perseverance of the elect. In contradistinction, unconditional security does not condition reaching Heaven upon the
 believer’s perseverance. Accordingly, unconditional election is not to be confused with unconditional security.

                                                      Introduction
       Many Christians have the mistaken notion from Jn 14:2 that Jesus is building a heavenly mansion in New
Jerusalem for all believers regardless of the way they live. As a result, those adopting this perspective tend to think that
Heaven is pretty much the same for everyone. Overcomers and subcomers are treated roughly identically in terms of
their housing.1 Additionally, the parable of the laborers (Mt 20:1-16) is thought to prove that everyone gets the same
paycheck in terms of rewards.
                                         Illustration 1. Communistic Paycheck




       According to this perspective, the communistic God gives the same paycheck to overcomers as He does to
subcomers. The payment amount on their heavenly paycheck might as well read ditto. The emblem of Christian
communism is a fitting logo for this view of Heaven.2 The present study will demonstrate that logical incompatibility
with supposing that every believer has a heavenly mansion in that Paul teaches that the houses being built by some
believers will be completely burned down to the ground. As demonstrated in 4D Forgiveness, the heavenly mansion
promised by the Lord in Jn 14:2 is contextually and culturally limited to overcomers. Subcomers do not qualify. The
present book will focus its rebuttal of the communistic notion by comparing Paul’s teaching of the workmen with
Jesus’ parable of the laborers.

                                                  Be Punished (zemioo)
         Paul explicitly teaches that some believers will experience misthological suffering in Heaven as a result of the
Judgment Seat of Christ (known by its Greek name as the Bema). By far, most translations translate this verse as
asserting Bema suffering: “If any man’s work is burned up, he shall suffer loss; but he himself shall be saved, yet so as
through fire” (1Cor 3:15). Although the nature and duration of this suffering is debated, the reality of this suffering
should be accepted. It is biblical. As Alcorn states concerning this verse: “Paul spoke of loss of reward as a great and
terrible loss. The fact that we are still saved is only a clarification, not a consolation.”4 In 2Cor 5:10, Paul also
clarifies that some believers will be recompensed in like kind for the bad and good they do. In other words, if they do
bad on Earth, they will reap bad consequences in Heaven. He also indicated the same thing in Col 3:25: “For he who
does wrong will receive the consequences of the wrong which he has done, and that without partiality.” Jesus presents
this contrast pictorially in parables. The didactical and parabolic material are in full agreement. The parables are
pictures of this misthological suffering that unfaithful believers will experience in Heaven. The doctrine pictured by the
outer darkness is not limited to three parables. The nature of this misthological suffering receives some clarification
from Paul’s own context.
        I have translated this key verse as: “If any man’s work is burned up, he will be punished [zemioo]; but he
himself will be saved, yet so as through fire” (1Cor 3:15; TM). BDAG states that zemioo generally means “to cause
injury or inflict punishment.” The verb is used in the passive in our literature and thus means to “suffer damage or loss,
forfeit, sustain injury.” More specifically, as BDAG points out, in 1Cor 3:15 it means to “be punished.”
        One definition L-N offers for zemioo in 1Cor 3:15 is: “To be punished, with the implication of suffering
damage–‘to be punished, to suffer punishment.” Additionally, it provides an alternative definition: “To suffer loss.” But
L-N qualifies this alternative by saying that the implication is “that the loss involves considerable hardship or
suffering.” An expanded translation thus would be: He will suffer the punishment of painful loss. This is the
translation being used herein.
        Zemioo is used in six passages in the NT. In Phil 3:8, Paul said that he had “suffered the loss of all things” in
order to gain Christ. Gaining Christ was not a painless experience: No pain, no gain. In 2Cor 7:9, Paul is relieved that

                                                           Fire
       To be sure, some passages indicate that those believers whose lives are characterized by moral failure will have
their works burnt up: “If any man’s work is burned up, he shall suffer loss [zemioo]; but he himself shall be saved, yet
so as through fire” (1Cor 3:15). “But if it yields thorns and thistles, it is worthless and close to being cursed, and it
ends up being burned” (Heb 6:8). For sake of argument, assume for the moment that this just pictures God depriving
you of rewards for poor service, as advocates of the awards podium view of the Bema insist. Is that not enough to
conclude that a punitive aspect is intended? Awards-podium advocates seem to picture God as saying, “Please get
outside while I burn your house down, and please don’t mistake this as being an indication that I am upset with you or
are punishing you. It’s just that your house is not up to code for this neighborhood, and I am responsible for enforcing
that code.” The Lord stands there emotionally neutral while burning your house down. Such impassivity is not a
suitable picture of this excruciating activity, however.
       Moreover, the biblical imagery does not necessarily prove that this burning has nothing to do with the believers
themselves. To be sure, the Bema fire does not necessarily singe the believers literally. “Shadrach, Meshach and Abed-
nego came out of the midst of the fire. And the satraps, the prefects, the governors and the king’s high officials
gathered around and saw in regard to these men that the fire had no effect on the bodies of these men nor was the hair
of their head singed, nor were their trousers damaged, nor had the smell of fire even come upon them” (Dan 3:26-27).
       These particular believers passed through the fire unharmed and without suffering because they were faithful. In
contrast, the believers in 1Cor 3:15 and Heb 6:8, who suffer as a result of the flames, are unfaithful. God burns the
believer’s house down (so to speak) while the believer is still in it. God does not tell the believer to step outside while
He burns down his or her house. God sets the house aflame while the believer is still morally asleep in it. Let the fire be
the wakeup call! The believer escapes, but one would suspect that he or she does so with burned hair and clothing, and
perhaps second and third degree burns. Similarly, in the second passage, the field lays charred. They did not cut the
thorns down and cart them off to the fire. They burned the thorns while they were still in the field. God’s fiery wrath is
not limited to the believer’s works. It touches the person in the house and scorches the field as well. The believer does
not escape unscathed.
       “If anyone does not abide in Me, he is thrown away as a branch, and dries up; and they gather them, and cast
them into the fire, and they are burned” (Jn 15:6). The branches are identified in the context as believers. Parabolically,
believers themselves are thrown into the fire. Also, the context of Heb 10:27 addresses this warning to believers: “A
certain terrifying expectation of judgment, and the fury of a fire which will consume the adversaries.” The adversaries
themselves experience the fury of the fire.
       The NT speaks metaphorically of such believers experiencing misthological fire (Mk 9:49; Jn 15:6; 1Cor 3:15;
Heb 6:7-8; Jam 5:3; Jude 1:23) and misthological darkness (Mt 8:12; 22:13; 25:30). Believers will come into
misthological judgment but not soteriological judgment (Jn 5:24). They will never come into a judgment where the
issue is the Lake of Fire versus the kingdom of Heaven. They may be cast out misthologically (Jn 15:6), but they will
never be cast out soteriologically (Jn 6:37). They can be cast out of the banquet; they cannot be cast out of the
kingdom. They will be least in the kingdom, but at least they will still be in the kingdom. Since Paul says that unworthy
believers who have built upon the foundation of Christ will be saved in 1Cor 3:15, unworthy believers will not undergo
the same punishment that unbelievers go through who do not build upon the foundation. Even if Gehenna has
secondary, correlative applicability to unfaithful believers, unfaithful believers will never burn in the literal fires of
Gehenna.

        Certainly, God’s people can misthologically put themselves in an adversarial position. The application of God’s

misthologists may prefer a temporal understanding of God’s fiery wrath. But the context teaches that the misthological
results will be eternal in both John (12:25) and Hebrews (3:11; 4:3,5). Misthologically, Dillow is quite justified to link

works are simply a metonymy for the believer himself.”9
        Some have sought to find justification in Dillow’s statement for concluding that the sin nature of a believer is
burned up and consumed by the fires of the Bema. To the contrary, although 1Cor 3:15 is “a metonymy for the believer
himself,” the verse is not teaching an annihilation of that part of the person producing that defective behavior. The
purpose of the passage is to show penalization rather than purification. A trope is being used for the painful punishment
inflicted upon believers as they are deprived of their trophy. The person him or herself, not some part of the person, is
saved. Paul is making a soteriological distinction between the believer and his or her works to show that a person is not
saved from hell by works. At the same time, Paul is making a misthological association between the workers and their
works (both undergoing the fire) to show that faithful believers are saved by works from painful, personal loss at the
Bema. The identification of believers with their works is a misthological metonymy, not a soteriological metonymy.
Paul’s point in 2Cor 5:10 is not merely that the works are judged, but that the workers receive the results of their
judged works.

Material
       The nature of the works also bears elegant testimony to the dualism. Two distinct classes of works clearly are
entailed: (1) gold, silver, precious stones, on the one hand, versus (2) wood, hay, and straw, on the other hand. The
worker in the first category builds with expensive, hard to obtain, non-combustible material. Whereas the second type
of worker builds with cheap, easy to find, easily burnt material. One builder builds with one kind of material, while the
other builder builds with an entirely different type of material. This passage presents two different types of builders—
one good, the other bad—not one builder who uses both types of material. The use of work (ergon) in the singular
throughout the passage reinforces this impression. Each builder will have his particular building put to the test by the
building inspector. Two builders and two buildings are under review. Each singular building will be composed of
various materials. But the building either passes or fails the inspection. This test is strictly pass or fail. No gradients are
entertained. Thus Thiselton appropriately concludes: “The overwhelming majority of commentators rightly perceive a
qualitative difference between only two types of material, as against six.”12 Of course, everyone will build with some
gold and some wood at some point in their lives, that is, with all six materials, but that is not the point Paul is making in
this analogy. Quite the contrary, he has polarized the material into only two types used by a good worker versus a poor
worker.
Results
       This understanding of the text is unmistakably correct in view of the results of the judgment. Only polarized
ends of the misthological spectrum are entertained. (1) The good worker receives a reward. (2) The other worker
suffers the punishment of painful loss—no reward. An all-or-nothing dichotomy is obviously at work. Either your
work remains and you experience a positive outcome, or your work does not remain and you suffer a negative outcome.
The purpose of this fire is not to purify but to reveal the quality of each building so that its builder may be dealt with
accordingly. Those commentators who insist that a punitive aspect cannot be entailed because the purpose of the fire is
to reveal the quality of the workmanship inject a false dichotomy. The purpose of the fire is not just to see if the
building stands or falls. Much more is at stake. Something positive or negative is done to the builder after his building
is judged. The good builder gets something in addition to having his building stand. He gets a positive reward. The
poor builder gets something in addition to having his building burned down. He gets a negative reward—a punitive
response.
       So whereas the good builder gets a double positive, the poor builder gets a double negative. On the positive side,
the people whom the good builder has helped will be able to welcome him into their heavenly mansions, for example
(cp. Lk 16:1-9). He will not only have his own mansion but find welcome in theirs. On the flipside, the poor minister
who winds up in the outer darkness along with his congregation will have neither his own mansion nor mansions to be
welcomed into.

        In 1Cor 3:10-17, Paul implements two thresholds. Being placed upon the foundation of Jesus Christ is a free gift
given to all those who simply believe in Jesus for eternal life. Those placed on the foundation apart from works can
start working and thereby pass over the first threshold from being a mere believer to being a believing worker. As a
believer, all the benefits freely available to you in Christ are yours permanently, such as bare minimum salvation from
eternal damnation, which Paul pictures as being saved through fire by the skin of your teeth. As a worker, however, you
can produce quality workmanship that has the potential to result in reward. If you qualify for such reward, you will
have crossed a second threshold dealing exclusive with rewards in Heaven. Entrance into Heaven was assured already
by virtue of having been placed on the foundation. The notion that the destruction in 1Cor 3:17 can nullify the security
conveyed by the foundation is nullified by observing that Paul acknowledges that even he could be disapproved at the
Bema if he performed poorly as a preacher. Yet this poor performance, as he acknowledges in 1Cor 3:15, does not
result in loss of salvation relative to being on the foundation. Additionally, in the parallel passage in 2Tim 2:14-21,
Paul makes it clear that the shame and honor resulting from the Bema are experienced in the Father’s large house (i.e.,
within Heaven) and that the foundation stands firm in its saving sufficiency regardless of the nature of the construction
or destruction taking place upon the foundation.
        In Mt 20:1-16, Jesus pictures all the workers as faithfully discharging their responsibilities and seemingly being
rewarded equally as a result. Some interpreters have abused this passage greatly so as to picture a communistic Heaven.
Being ignored in such misrepresentations is the fact that the parable is only dealing with faithful workers. Since
unfaithful workers are not in view, drawing the conclusion that all workers, faithful and unfaithful alike, receive the
same reward is completely erroneous. Moreover, the parable itself is crouched in a relative ranking of the faithful
workers pictured in the parable. Whether the relative ranking of first to last deals exclusively with the faithful believers
is open to debate perhaps. My perception is that this first-last ranking is dealing exclusively with faithful believers who
qualify to inherit the kingdom. Regardless, this relative ranking certainly deals with these workers inclusively. A
communistic interpretation of the parable is impossible, in any event, given the very clear passages in the NT affirming
misthological ranking. Having provided an overview of the nature of the binary and fuzzy issues pictured by the
parable, a more detailed discussion can now be undertaken in the following chapters.
               Chapter 2. First and Last Relatively Speaking
                                                     Introduction
       The most common parabolic objection to the doctrine of rewards is derived from the Workers in the Vineyard in
Mt 20:1-16 who receive the same reward. Different groups work a different number of hours, being called at different
times of the day. Yet at the end of the day, they all receive the same wage. Judging from how some interpret this
parable, you would think that God is a communist in regards to how He governs Heaven and runs its economy. Indeed,
Blomberg uses this parable to begin his article, in which he denies that believers get different rewards in Heaven. He
assumes that the parable teaches that all genuine believers work and get the same reward since false believers are
excluded from the kingdom. Thus, he takes the reference to having last place in Mt 20:16 to mean “excluded from the
kingdom” so that there is no ranking “among genuine believers.”24 This assessment is rather strange for a verse that
concludes a kingdom parable by saying, “Thus the last shall be first, and the first last.” One would be inclined to
suppose a capitalistic ranking within the kingdom according to this parabolic conclusion. A social stratification is
certainly implied. Even broadly speaking, Blomberg’s communistic understanding of how the word last is used is open
to serious question. My thesis is that God does not limit entrance into Heaven to those who work for it. Nor does this
parable intend to teach that everyone in Heaven gets the same paycheck.
                                                        First versus Last
        A more careful observation of how the word last (eschatos) is used parabolically could lead to the conclusion
that those who are last are misthologically last at the table in the kingdom. Those paid last in this parable are given the
same reward as those hired last. Both groups work faithfully and are paid the same accordingly. The natural inference,
therefore, is that the first-versus-last distinction that concludes the parable is a ranking of faithful workers. This ranking
is relative to the table (i.e., the kingship) rather than to the kingdom per se.

         Contrary to Blomberg, to have last place should not be construed as having no place. Last place believers within

categorical reward as a result of their actions but a different subcategorization of their rewards based on their
attitudes.25 The inclusio in 19:30 and 20:16, about being first and last, certainly suggests the subcategorization amidst
the parabolic standardization in which each laborer receives the standard wage for a day’s wage.

therein is based on faithfulness and not on the timing of one’s entrance into Christian service. Believers who come to
Christ (1) late in their lives or (2) late in the Christian era have the opportunity to earn the same reward as do those who
come to Christ early in their lives or early in the church age. Rewards are based on faithfulness subsequent to coming to
Christ, not on how long one has been a Christian or when one became a Christian. Being rewarded according to one’s
faithfulness certainly explains the standardization.

need very much faith. They had an implied contract. The second group needed more faith that their faithfulness would

faith! Or as Gregg expresses it, “Laboring faith is especially rewarded by the Rewarder of this service.” His thesis “is
that the degree of faith exercised, and not the grief endured, is the overarching principle in God’s compensation of his
servants….Greater trust in His just reward is justly rewarded with greater honor in the end….the greater faith of the

                                                        Affirmation
        The parable announces its misthological nature in the opening verse: “For the kingdom of heaven is like a
landowner who went out early in the morning to misthologically hire [misthoo] workers for his vineyard” (Mt 20:1;
TM). Misthoo is the verbal root for misthos, from which the term misthology is derived. When the landowner asked a
group later that day why they were not working, they respond: “Because no one misthologically hired [misthoo] us”
(20:7; TM). Later, when it is time to pay (apodidomi) the workers (ergatns), he tells his foreman to pay the workers
their misthological wage (misthos). This parable is dripping wet with misthological details. The nominal and verbal
forms of misthology are used to indicate that it is dealing with something that is paid to those who work.
        For LS soteriologists, such as Pagenkemper and Keathley, to come along and try to turn this parable into a
description of a gift freely given to those who do nothing to earn it (except work for it!) is to ignore just about every
detail in the parable. Misthologists need only point out the numerous details, which stress through redundancy that the
payment is paid to those workers who work. Soteriologists, on the other hand, in trying to ignore the details in order to
derive one major soteriological truth, miss the richness of this misthological vineyard. There is a major truth, to be sure,
but it is misthological in nature. Properly inducing the fact that this parable is conveying misthological truth will allow
us to compare it deductively with other misthological parables, as well as with didactic material, in order to determine
what major misthological truth is being expressed in comparison with and in relation to other such illustrations.
                                           Theory of Misthological Relativity
Exegetically Fruitless
        Does the parable of the workers in the vineyard necessarily teach that all believers will work, that is, produce
good works in terms of good fruits? No. Various degrees of carnality are portrayed in other parables.34 Jesus indicates
that some carnal believers bear no fruit (Lk 8:13); these are contrasted with believers who bear no fruits to maturity
(Lk 8:14). The immature believer fails to produce mature fruit. He fails to produce fruit to the same quality and
quantity as the mature believer (Lk 8:15). In another parable, Jesus represents believers as vines who may produce no
fruit, some fruit, or much fruit (Jn 15:2). Despite the Lord’s persistent care and discipline, no fruit may be produced
(Lk 13:6-9). The Lord may not be able to get any interest back on His investment in the lives of some believers
(Mt 25:26-27). This unfaithful slave does not bear any fruit in terms of producing even one talent.
        Likewise, in the parable of the minas, one group of believers will be very faithful, others partially faithful, and
still others will be totally unfaithful and not even yield an increase of one mina (Lk 19:11-27). Some believers produce
zero fruit, minas, or talents. Certainly, many believers will serve money instead of Jesus (Mt 6:24). According to John,
a number of believers have a dead love that does not express itself in good works (1Jn 3:17-19), and James concurs that
believers may have a dead faith that fails to express itself in good work (Jam 2:14-27). Peter indicates that believers
may lack good works (2Pet 1:8-9). The Greek word that Peter uses to describe the reality of unfruitful (akarpos)
believers in this passage is the same word that Paul uses to describe the possibility of unfruitful believers in Tit 3:14,
which in turn is the same word that Jesus used to describe believers who become unfruitful after initially bringing forth
immature fruit (Mt 13:22; Mk 4:19).
        In fact, akarpos is the same word Paul uses to tell believers, “Stop participating in the unfruitful deeds of
darkness” (Eph 5:11; TM). If believers cannot be unfruitful, then why does Paul command them to stop being
unfruitful? Lest anyone say this is just a temporary condition, Paul teaches that it is as equally possible for a believer to
not have any works worthy of reward at the conclusion of his or her life as it is for another believer to have many works
worthy of being rewarded (1Cor 3:11-15). Equal possibility rules out mere hypotheticality. In this passage, a believer
can be considered barren even when his or her whole life is under review. The writer of Hebrews also relates the
possibility of believers yielding thorns rather than fruit and consequently experiencing a fiery judgment as their
concluding result (Heb 6:8). According to Jesus, Peter, Paul, John, James, and the author of Hebrews, believers may be
unfruitful.

                                                   Positive Expectations
       A favorite ploy among Lordship Salvationists is to represent themselves as merely expecting some change in a
person’s life. Demanding changes in a person’s life is quite different from expecting them, however. LS exegetically
and pragmatically demands radical changes. Those in FG generally expect fruits (i.e., positive changes) in the lives of
the regenerate, but FG does not confuse expectations with conditions by demanding works as conditions or as
necessary proof of regeneration. The general expectation that regeneration makes a positive difference in a person’s life
is not the point in question. The point of contention is not with this general expectation but with turning this
expectation into a scriptural condition for salvation. FG theology does not turn what a person does into a litmus test for
regeneration. Good works, or lack thereof, are not infallible indicators of regeneration. Moreover, the Bible provides
numerous exhortations to do good works. These are exhortations, not promises.
       When those in FG encounter a professing believer who is living a life that is substantially substandard, the FG
advocate would do well to question the salvation of the professing believer on the basis of general positive
expectations. The primary difference between LS and FG would be what happens at that point of examination. Suppose
the individual being questioned gives clear articulation of the fact that he or she indeed has trusted in Christ alone for
eternal life. LS would continue to doubt, if not outright deny, such a person’s salvation due to the LS insistence on a
visible turning in a saved person’s life; whereas, those from FG would more readily accept the person’s testimony.
Secondarily, the FG advocate would not encourage such individuals to base their soteriological assurance on their
performance but to be assured that they will be judged severely at the Bema due to such performance. The FG message
to such believers is to repent, not to become believers since they already are believers.

      obligatory nature of the reward. Quantitatively, every reward is merited. Solely on the basis of God’s gracious

that merit is entailed, one should note the interplay between condign and congruent merit.

                                                       Introduction
       The inclusio between Mt 19:30 and 20:16 about the potential role reversals between first and last is often
perceived by commentators to encompass a warning to the twelve.58 As the rich young ruler walked away from the
opportunity of earning treasure in Heaven by selling everything and following Jesus, Peter inquires: “Behold, we have
left everything and followed You; what then will there be for us?” (Mt 19:27) Contrary to the opinion of some, Jesus
does not rebuke Peter for being selfish in seeking a reward, yet the Lord does give a warning that there will be surprises
regarding rewards: “Many who are first will be last; and the last, first” (Mt 19:30). The parable of the laborers in the
vineyard, which follows this cryptic saying, likewise concludes: “Thus the last shall be first, and the first last”
(Mt 20:16). To make the matter even more obscure, the intervening parable pictures all the participants as getting the
same reward regardless of how long they labor. So what happened to the first versus last distinction?
                                                        Leftovers
       Peter, as one of the twelve, had just been promised rulership for following the Lord. The twelve were in the
position to earn the twelve thrones governing the twelve tribes: “And Jesus said to them, ‘Truly I say to you, that you
who have followed Me, in the regeneration when the Son of Man will sit on His glorious throne, you also shall sit upon
twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel’” (Mt 19:28). Seemingly, the twelve were at the front of the line for
the dispensing of the most prestigious rewards within the kingdom. Everyone else would have to be satisfied with
leftovers—what rewards would be left over after the twelve got the top spots. Latecomers to the party, such as the rich
young ruler, could only hope for second-rate rewards. Oh, to be sure, such stragglers might inherit the kingdom, but co-
heirship with Christ surely would be beyond the range of possibility for subsequent disciples since the top spots already
would have been given to the twelve.
       Such erroneous thoughts surely would have been a temptation for the twelve. They were prone to misthological
miscalculations in their zeal for top positions within the kingdom (Mt 20:20-28). Ruling in the kingdom was not
enough for their ambitions; they argued among themselves as to which of them would be the greatest (Lk 9:46; 22:24).
Jesus’ parable of the workers in the vineyard is intended to confound any such misguided misthological speculations.
                                                    Unsought Rewards
       The nature of the role-reversal warning is misconstrued frequently because of a colossal misinterpretation as to
the nature of the intervening parable of the workers (Mt 20:1-16). Turner is representative of this misrepresentation:
“Although Peter and the disciples will be rewarded, they must not presume upon God’s grace and seek rewards.”59
Although Matthew has provided numerous examples of Jesus encouraging His followers to seek rewards and even
though Jesus has just laid out the possibility of reward to the rich young ruler, the best Turner can do is to conclude that
Jesus is saying that His followers should not seek rewards! Turner, in effect, rebukes Jesus. While Jesus is teaching His
followers to work for rewards, Turner is saying, in effect, “Not so, Lord.” Yet Turner’s assessment is shared by an
unending parade of commentary parody. Wilkins, for example, thinks that the parable means that those who follow
Jesus’ repeated admonitions to seek for rewards in Heaven and to lay up treasures therein will come in last place for
doing what Jesus has instructed!60 Some commentators have the audacity to say that it is unchristian to follow Christ’s
injunction to seek for rewards and even assert that those who do so are not Christians! In reality, such commentators
are being unchristian.

                                                      What is Right
        The promise of remuneration the landowner provides to the second group he hires is this: “‘You too go into the
vineyard, and whatever is right [dikaios] I will give [didomi] you’ And so they went” (Mt 20:4).84 This statement is
repeated to the third and fourth groups. So why does the landowner not give less than a full day’s wage, when he had
promised to give them what was right? Legally, he would have been expected to give them less than a denarius since a
denarius was the wage for a full day’s work. A partial day’s work would not have resulted in the payment of a full
denarius. The stock assumption among commentators is that the statement anticipates an appropriate reduction in pay.
Probably, in the minds of the parabolic workers, such a reduction is expected. Instead, however, each group is given a
full day’s wage for a partial day’s work. Why was this full wage the right wage? Because a full day’s wage is what was
promised. The parabolic reader should anticipate as much.
        Obviously, the parabolic master had not made such a promise, but the contextual Master had. Jesus had just
promised that everyone who serves Him faithfully will be rewarded fully: “Everyone who has left houses or brothers or
sisters or father or mother or children or farms for My name’s sake, shall receive many times as much, and shall inherit
eternal life” (Mt 19:29). The point of the parable is just that: Everyone who serves Jesus in this manner will be
rewarded in that manner. If you serve the Lord faithfully, you will inherit the kingdom. Last place believers do not have
to be anxious with the thought that nothing will be left over for them or that the only thing that will be left over for
them is leftovers. God has the right to be gracious in granting a full inheritance. The Master will be faithful to His
promise. The parabolic master graciously gave according to their need; the contextual Master graciously gives
according to His promise.
                                                           Conclusion
        The parable of the laborers in the vineyard focuses on surprising the role reversals that will take place among
kingdom rulers. Condign merit is integrated with congruent merit so that even those believers who qualify for rulership
late in life, or late in the dispensation, or last in opportunity or ability need not worry about being treated second rate in
terms of the positions for which they qualify. Their trust in the gracious nature of the Lord will not go unrewarded.
Those serving the Lord faithfully because they trust in the Lord graciously rewarding them with a great degree of
reward, even when it seems like the degree of temporal results they are generating is comparatively small, will not be
disappointed by the outcome of the Bema. Contrastively, some of those who are generating the most impressive
temporal results may be disappointed by their comparative misthological results. Those serving in obscure limited
capacities should take great encouragement from this parable. Although they may not generate as great a return on the
Lord’s investment in their lives because of their circumstances, they will fully inherit the kingdom. They will cross the
misthological threshold just as fully as those who obtain the highest ranking positions.

82
   Congruent merit points to the excessiveness of the reward. Qualitatively, the reward far exceeds what we deserve.
Condign merit points to the obligatory nature of the reward. Quantitatively, every reward is merited.
83
   If one were to look at the parable strictly in terms of the parable itself, without contemplating on the nature of the
reward being represented, then perhaps the argument could be made that the contrast is between congruent merit and
strict merit. However, given the theological and misthological context, the plausibly strict merit parabolically pictures
condign merit. Presumably, this parabolic master could have allowed his servants to do the reaping and thus avoided
paying extra workers. Instead, he chooses to be generous, as the parable itself attests, and to provide employment
opportunities for those who need it. Obviously, his gracious giving is motivated more by their need rather than his own.
Additionally, they did not deserve the right to work, only the right to payment once they had worked. Therefore, even
the parabolic details themselves suggest that condign merit be given preference over strict merit as describing what is
taking place, even in the case of those who worked all day. Although not emphasized on the two-dimensional graph,
they each were given the same gracious opportunity: to work for a full-day’s wage, a denarius. In that aspect, they were
each shown the same grace and the same amount of grace. Although the payment for the job was earned by various
degrees by each group, the giving of a job was not deserved by any group. Even the basis (in terms of grounds) for the
offering of an opportunity of strict merit afforded to the first group hired was on the basis of grace and better
considered a parabolic example of condign grace.
84
   Give (didomi) can also be translated pay (as is done by this same translation, the NAS, in Mk 12:14-15 for example).
Didomi is translated as pay in Mt 20:4 by various translations as well: CJB, NIB, NIV, NLT, NRS. Apodidomi in
Mt 20:8 is rendered as pay by the NAS: “Pay them their wages.” They are given what is right (dikaios) as their
payment. This word is also translated as righteous. A play on words may be intended. They are given misthological
righteousness before God.
