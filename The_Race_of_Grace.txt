                         Technical Terms
       Conditional security is the belief that perseverance is necessary to
enter heaven. (Both proveitism and forfeitism advocate conditional security.)

       Conditionalist refers to an advocate of conditional security—the
conviction that once a person believes in Christ for eternal life, that person is
conditionally secure in Christ and must persevere in order to reach heaven.

       Forfeitist refers to an advocate of conditional security who believes
that those who do not persevere forfeit (i.e., lose) the gift of eternal life.

       Legalism refers to an unlawful use of the law to obtain something that
is not intended to be obtained by keeping the law. Broadly speaking
soteriologically, it may refer to the attempt to gain entrance into heaven (or
assurance of entrance into heaven) by means of God-pleasing performance.

       Misthology is a coined term derived from the Greek words misthos
(reward) and logos (teaching) and refers to the doctrine of temporal and
eternal rewards but is used primarily in reference to eternal rewards.

       OSAS is the popular acronym for Once Saved Always Saved (i.e.,
unconditional security). Technically, OSAS is not the same thing as
unconditional security in that many proveitists believe in OSAS and yet
insist that one must persevere in order to reach heaven. Thus, reaching
heaven is conditioned by the proveitist camp on perseverance. This
proveitist hybrid is not a valid form of OSAS. The only form of OSAS
considered legitimate herein is the unconditional affirmation of OSAS even
if one does not persevere.

      Performatist is someone who believes that God-enabled, good
performance on the believer’s part is necessary for entrance into heaven.

        Proveitist is an advocate of conditional security who believes that
fruit is necessary proof of salvation: If you do not have this fruit, then you
were never saved to start with. Perseverance is necessary to prove that you
are saved and to enter heaven. Thus, entrance into heaven is conditioned on
the fruit of perseverance.

       Regeneration is the theological term used to describe the impartation
of eternal life as a free gift (to one’s spirit) which occurs when a person
believes in Christ for eternal life. Such a person is said to be born again (i.e.,
given spiritual life in the form of eternal life as a free gift).
                                                                        Page vii


       Saving faith is a theological term used to refer to the faith which
results in salvation from hell. For an unconditional securitist, such faith is
regarded as punctiliar (z). For the conditional securitist, such faith is
regarded as linear (£). In other words, securitists believe that initial faith is
sufficient for salvation from eternal damnation. Insecuritists, in contrast,
teach that initial faith is insufficient since it must be followed by
perseverance in faith in order to qualify for heaven.

      Securitist refers to an advocate of unconditional security—the
conviction that once a person believes in Christ for eternal life, that person is
unconditionally secure in Christ and is unconditionally assured of reaching
heaven unconditionally.

       Soteriology is based on two biblical Greek words, soteria (salvation)
and logos (teaching), and correspondingly refers to biblical teaching about
salvation. Although the biblical concept of salvation includes many
dimensions other than deliverance from hell, it is this aspect of salvation to
which writers refer frequently when they use the word soteriology. Thus,
salvation from hell (or more specifically, from eternal damnation in the Lake
of Fire) will be referred to as soteriological salvation or eternal salvation. A
soteriological interpretation of a passage thus would treat the passage as
dealing with eternal salvation from the Lake of Fire (Rev 20:14-15).

       Unconditional security is the belief that entrance into heaven does
not require perseverance.

       Works-righteousness is a derogatory label applied to the teaching
which asserts that we are saved from hell on the basis of “works of
righteousness which we have done.” Such a teaching rejects the clear
biblical summation that teaches that we are not saved from hell on the basis
of “works of righteousness which we have done” (Tit 3:5). Biblically, by
faith, we must rest our justification pertaining to getting to heaven on the
imputational/legal righteousness of Christ apart from any of our works
(Rom 3:28; 4:6). Those trusting in impartational/practical righteousness in
order to reach heaven are trusting wrongly in works-righteousness for a
benefit it was never intended to bestow.

       The Crown and Hope of Righteousness
      At the end of his life, the apostle Paul likens his life as a Christian to
having run a race successfully:
      7
        I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have
      kept the faith. 8 In the future the victor’s crown of
      righteousness is reserved for me, which the Lord, the
      righteous Judge, will award to me on that day; and not only to
      me, but also to all who have loved His appearing. (2Tim 4:7-8;
      TM)

Unfortunately, erroneous legalistic conclusions frequently are drawn from
this powerful imagery. Biblically, such errors are grievous enough to
disqualify one from winning the race. Earlier in this same epistle, Paul
warns: “If anyone competes as an athlete, he does not win the prize unless he
competes according to the rules” (2Tim 2:5). In a prior epistle, Paul clearly
asserts that although some runners were doing their best to live the Christian
life, they nevertheless were disqualified from winning the race:
      4
        You have been severed from Christ, you who are seeking to
      be justified by law; you have fallen from grace. 5 For we
      through the Spirit, by faith, are waiting for the hope of
      righteousness. 6 For in Christ Jesus neither circumcision nor
      uncircumcision means anything, but faith working through
      love. 7 You were running well; who hindered you from obeying
      the truth? (Gal 5:4-7)

       When it comes to winning this race, legalistic* performance counts
for nothing toward winning this crown/hope of righteousness. These
legalistic believers/runners were disqualified from the race because they
were using the law unlawfully in trying to be justified (in terms of going to
heaven) by their performance. Thus, Paul reminds them that “the Law has
become our tutor to lead us to Christ, that we may be justified by faith”
(Gal 3:24).† This type of justification is by faith, not by performance.

*
  The term legalism is derived from the unlawful use of the law in passages such
as this one. Conversely, “the Law is good, if one uses it lawfully” (1Tim 1:8).
†
  Also see Gal 2:16; 3:8,11.

        To be sure, these Galatian believers realized that they were justified
initially by faith apart from works. Paul would have drilled that into their
heads (Rom 3:28; 4:6). That is the heart of the gospel of grace after all.
Justification qualifies the believer for heaven—unconditionally and
permanently. Yet someone subsequently led them astray into thinking that
the final realization of that initial justification would be conditioned on their
subsequent performance (Gal 5:10). They came to conclude that, in the end,
salvation from hell ultimately would be based on their postconversional
Christian performance. Legalistically, they had turned winning the Christian
race into a rat race to escape hell.
                     Illustration 1. Hope of Righteousness




        In reality, the Christian race has nothing to do with escaping the
flames of hell. Instead, winning this race (i.e., winning the hope of
righteousness, Gal 5:5) has everything to do with what Paul later clarified at
the conclusion of his own personal race as winning the crown of
righteousness (2Tim 4:8). What is at stake at the end of the race is the
reward of righteousness, not the free gift of righteousness. Those who are
seeking final justification as if it were initial justification have turned the
Christian race into a rat race.1 The final justification that one gains by
successfully winning the race pertains to rewards in heaven (not entrance
into heaven), and these rewards are to be pursued by faith working through
love.
        In fact, Paul will conclude this very chapter to the Galatians by
warning these unloving, Christian legalists that those who behave unlovingly
will not inherit the kingdom of heaven (Gal 5:21). Obviously, to inherit a
kingdom refers to more than just entering a kingdom. The only way to
inherit a kingdom is as a ruler of that kingdom. Not all who live in a
kingdom are rulers of that kingdom. Paul, among others, explicitly teaches
that this type of inheritance is a reward (Col 3:24). Christian, if you want to
be a kingdom ruler, then be loving.2 Run the race. This is a race of love: a
love race, not a rat race.3

         Two Errors
      The aforementioned legalistic errors mistakenly make getting into
heaven a matter of successfully living the Christian life—a matter of

perseverance in the race. As a result, getting into heaven wrongly becomes
conditioned on the way a person lives. Yet such errors can be avoided easily
by just maintaining the distinction between a gift and a reward.
       As to one form of the error, many people interpret the passage to
mean that if you are a genuine believer, then you will prove it by
successfully running the race. Conversely, if you do not successfully run the
race, then this proves that you never were a genuine Christian. Such
interpreters accordingly may be called proveitists. They insist that only
successful runners are genuine runners. Proveitists limit entrance into
heaven to successful runners. Among other things, one might well wonder
though: “How does not finishing the race prove that one never was running
in the race?” Concluding that one who stops running was never running is
certainly counterintuitive.

         Proposed Solution
        In contrast to both of these conditionalistic perspectives, securitists
do not believe that entrance into heaven is conditioned on successfully
running the race or on holding fast the Christian faith. Keeping the free gift
of eternal life is not conditioned on perseverance (in either faith or
faithfulness). A person who becomes a Christian has unconditional security.
Accordingly, those adopting this perspective avow unconditional security.
What is at stake for successfully completing the race is a reward. Being
awarded the crown is a reward—not a gift. What we win by our God-
enabled personal performance is a reward. Winning this race is costly—to
the runner. Yet the prize makes it well worth the God-enabled effort.
        Paul also described this race and his participation in this race earlier
in his life:
      23
         And I do all things for the sake of the gospel, that I may
      become a fellow partaker of it. 24 Do you not know that those
      who run in a race all run, but only one receives the prize? Run
      in such a way that you may win. 25 And everyone who
      competes in the games exercises self-control in all things. They
      then do it to receive a perishable wreath, but we an
      imperishable. 26 Therefore I run in such a way, as not without
      aim; I box in such a way, as not beating the air; 27 but I buffet
      my body and make it my slave, lest possibly, after I have
      preached to others, I myself should be disqualified. (1Cor 9:23-
      27)

       Winning is conditioned on running. Biblically, the runner is to be
motivated to run well by the dual prospects of winning and losing.5
Disqualification is possible even for the most accomplished runner. Even
Paul would be disqualified from winning the race if he ran poorly. From a
proveitistic perspective, this means that Paul was in danger of proving that
he was never saved. Indeed, some proveitists follow the logical implications
of their perspective to that very conclusion. Yet who reading Paul’s
conversion account on the road to Damascus (Acts 9:1-30; 22:6-21) could
take such a suggestion seriously if they were not blinded by theological
presuppositions? Forfeitists reason instead that Paul was in danger of
actually losing the free gift of eternal life and thereby being disqualified
from entering heaven. However, this supposition logically rejects the
repeated emphasis in Paul’s epistles that salvation from hell and the
possession of eternal life as a free gift are not based on one’s performance
(Rom 6:23; Eph 2:8-9).

       The most natural conclusion is that entrance into heaven is offered
freely to those who simply believe in Christ for eternal life. Rewards in
heaven, however, are costly and forfeitable. In fact, Jesus says as much: “I
am coming quickly; hold fast what you have, in order that no one take your
crown” (Rev 3:11). This crown is limited to those believers who persevere
(Rev 3:10) to the end of the race and overcome (Rev 3:12) every obstacle in
their way of winning the race. Otherwise, believers will be disqualified from
winning the crown for which they otherwise would be qualified.
       For such reasons, biblically consistent securitists maintain that
entrance into heaven cannot be conditioned on perseverance. Rather,
perseverance is required for rewards in heaven. A free gift cannot be
conditioned on performance. When it comes to answering the question as to
what God requires for entrance into heaven, securitists are not performatists.
Nevertheless, Paul’s gospel does include a strong emphasis on rewards and
on the importance of performance. He runs to win these rewards lest he
should be disqualified from the rewards of which he speaks in his gospel.
       The writer of Hebrews, likewise, enjoins believers: “Therefore, do not
throw away your confidence, which has a great reward. For you have need
of endurance, so that when you have done the will of God, you may receive
what was promised” (Heb 10:35-36). What is received in response to
endurance/perseverance is a reward, not a gift.

         Repentance
       Many conditionalists say that repentance is required for the free gift
of eternal life. To the contrary, it makes no sense to say that God conditions
a free gift on changing one’s performance. Repentance is a change of
perspective about one’s performance so as to change that performance
(Acts 26:20; Rev 2:5). Repentance is tied inextricably to rewards in the
context of the latter passage (Rev 2-3). Like perseverance, repentance is a
performance issue that results in rewards. Repentance is not a non-
performance issue that results in a gift. Consequently, the gift of eternal life
cannot be conditioned on repentance.
       Conditionalists will object by claiming repentance was a condition for
receiving the gift of the Holy Spirit on at least one occasion: “And Peter said
to them, ‘Repent, and let each of you be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ
for the forgiveness of your sins; and you shall receive the gift of the Holy
Spirit’” (Acts 2:38). Securitists, though, may correct this conditionalistic
misperception by noting that even on this isolated occasion the gift was not
conditioned on repentance. Rather, as is frequently the case,* forgiveness
was conditioned on repentance. Such forgiveness is not free and thus should
not be confused with a free gift.

*
    Mk 1:4; Lk 3:3; 17:3-4; 24:47; Acts 5:31; 8:22.

        Repentance was a precondition, not condition, for this particular gift
on this peculiar occasion. Repentance was the condition for this forgiveness,
and this forgiveness, in turn, was the condition for the gift. Mistaking
preconditions for conditions is a huge mistake. Jesus, likewise, speaks in
terms of preconditions when He says, “And if your hand causes you to
stumble, cut it off; it is better for you to enter life crippled, than having your
two hands, to go into hell, into the unquenchable fire” (Mk 9:43). By no
stretch of the imagination can an entrance into a life that costs you cutting
off your hand be considered a free gift! If Jesus is understood as speaking in
terms of preconditions to saving faith, then He need not be misunderstood as
speaking nonsense about a free gift that costs you cutting off your hand. Any
performance issue that you are allowing to keep you from trusting in Christ
needs to be repented of if that is what it takes for you to come to saving
faith. In such a psychological frame of mind, you have placed a precondition
on yourself that you must meet in order to enter into possession of the free
gift of eternal life. If you are unwilling to believe until you give up some
particular sin, then give it up so that you can believe. God’s condition,
however, for the gift of eternal life is faith (not your precondition—not your
giving up that sin).

       Logic
        Yet expecting logical consistency within sermons is not wrong. The
Bible expects us to approach its teachings rationally. Indeed, the Bible
enjoins us to be logical. Being created in the image of God means, among
other things, that we were created as rational beings because God is a
rational being. Logic is rooted in the nature of God and thus in our nature. At
our deepest level, we do not appreciate contradictions or doubletalk. We
have an innate desire for truth—for logical consistency. Sure, there are limits
to what logic can do. Scripture takes precedence over logic. Logic is a
servant to Scripture. Even so, it is a disservice to Scripture to dispense with
the service performed by logic.
        The very word theology is derived from the combination of two
biblical Greek words: theos (God) and logos (logic). Biblical theology is
logical teaching about God. Sermons that are true to the nature of God will
not violate God’s moral laws or His logical laws. The axiomatic law of non-
contradiction (A is not non-A) is that no two contradictory statements can be
true at the same time and in the same sense. Not even God can do what is
logically impossible, such as making a round square, because that would go
against His very nature. Granted, mysteries exist that go beyond human
reason, but they do not go against it. The human inability to know truth
comprehensively does not nullify the ability to know truth fundamentally.
God is truth, and truth is the means to knowing God. Jesus identified
Himself as truth and the means to the Father (Jn 14:6). Since we can know
truth, we can know God fundamentally.
        Just as A cannot be non-A, just as light is not darkness, just as sweet
is not bitter, just as it is wrong to “call evil good, and good evil” (Is 5:20), so
it is wrong to call free costly! Pastors are being irrational when they talk of
the free gift of eternal life as being costly to the believer. The gift is costly to
the Giver (God), of course, but it is free to the recipient (the believer). The
Bible tells us “to refute those who contradict” sound doctrine (Tit 1:9). How
can contradictions to sound doctrine be refuted if sound doctrine is
composed of contradictions? Obviously, sound doctrine is presumed to be
free of contradictions. Any pastoral supposition that sound doctrine has
contradictions is antithetical to sound doctrine.

       As noted in the beginning chapter, the solution to this unnecessary

                        Illustration 10. Simple Solution




        Just as there are some things that parents may give to their children
freely as presents, so there are some things that God gives to His children
freely just because they are His children. On the other hand, loving parents
may give certain things as rewards to their children for good behavior or for
performing certain tasks. God also rewards His children in like fashion.
Specifically, the crown of life is promised to God’s children for being loving
and faithful (Jam 1:12; Rev 2:10).

conditioned on Christian virtues is not given freely. Therefore, one form of
eternal life (namely the water of life) is free, but another form of eternal life
(namely the crown of life) is costly. The Bible is not speaking
contradictorily when it speaks of two different aspects of eternal life—one
being free and the other being costly. The law of non-contradiction (A is not
non-A) is not being violated because eternal life is being spoken of in two
different senses.

hand, righteousness additionally may be achieved as a reward for loving the
Lord in terms of fighting the good fight, finishing the course, and keeping
the faith (2Tim 4:7-8). Lovingly persevering is rewarded by the Lord with a
crown. Two different forms of righteousness are entailed: one costly, the
other free.

       Guarantee
       Arrabon (down payment, guarantee) in Eph 1:14 provides a
commercial usage that is easy to understand. If you make a down payment
on your house to the mortgage company to guarantee that you will finish
paying off the house but fail to finish paying off the house, then you lose the
down payment and the mortgage company gets to keep it. The giver of the
down payment loses the down payment to the recipient of the transaction if
the giver of the down payment fails to complete the transaction. In this case,
God loses the Holy Spirit to us if He does not make good on His promise to
complete our redemption on the specified day. God is the one making the
promise in this context; we are not the ones making the promise.

we are the ones making the down payment rather than God—as if God sold
us His house (i.e. heaven) and is requiring that we make the down payment
on the frontend and the monthly payments on the backend. This picture is
false because God is the one making the payment, not us. And He does not
do so as a contract for our services. Instead, He does so as an unconditional
expression of His love for us as His children.*

believers, likewise, are described as given to Christ, some interpreters

giving of believers to Christ is a gift insofar as the believers are concerned.

       Come to Me
        The passage before us casts more than one monkey wrench, however,
into the conditionalistic machinery from which the ungiven-unsaved-
unsealed perspective is produced. At the beginning of this chapter, I have
recast this passage in an expanded translation that brings some of these
obstacles more clearly into focus. To begin with, note that the highlighted
phrase describing believers as those who come to Jesus occurs three times.
This phrase indicates coming to Christ initially (as even conditionalists will
admit). Therefore, some interpreters suggest that Jesus is promising to
welcome those who come to Him. This suggestion is not so bad in and of
itself. The problem occurs when conditionalists try to limit the promise to
mean that Jesus is only promising to welcome believers and giving no
assurance to keep them. We will return to that thought in a moment.
        First, let it be admitted that comes to Me would refer to the point in
time when believers initially come to Christ. Therefore, a punctiliar aspect
(z) is indicated. Conditionalists are correct to perceive an initial welcome.
For conditionalists, however, this initial faith is not enough to remain saved
since conditionalists condition remaining sealed, saved, and given on
continuous believing as an ongoing linear action (£). Thus, many (but not
all) conditionalists insist that in Jn 6:35, believes means continues to believe.
Obviously, this conditionalistic claim clashes with the conditionalistic
explanation. Comes to Me in this verse is parallel to believes in Me.
Therefore, the following conditionalistic explanation is in error:

      Whoever ever comes to Me (z) will never ever hunger,
      Whoever continuously believes in Me (£) will never ever thirst.

       Since comes to Me is punctiliar (z) and is metaphorically equivalent
to believes in Me, this latter phrase must be punctiliar also. In other words,
the punctiliar dot (z) rules out a parallel linear arrow (£). Those
conditionalists who impose a linear action in the second phrase ignore the
punctiliar action in the first phrase. Yet conditionalists themselves readily
will concede that comes to Me in the first phrase is punctiliar. As a result, the
true situation is pictured properly as thus:

      Whoever ever comes to Me (z) will never ever hunger,
      Whoever ever believes in Me (z) will never ever thirst.

      The punctiliar dot in the first phrase demands a punctiliar dot in the
second phrase.19 Whoever ever believes in Jesus at any point (z) in time is
promised that he or she will never ever hunger or thirst for eternal life any
time thereafter. The only condition is that one believes in Christ once (z) to
remain saved thenceforth.

       Whoever and Whosoever Ever
       The translation of this Greek construction is normally translatable as
whoever ever. The syntactical probability of this rendition is increased
greatly by the triple use of the whosoever syntax. This same whosoever
construction occurs in the most famous verse in the Bible: “For God so
loved the world, that He gave His only begotten Son, that whosoever ever
believes in Him will not perish, but have eternal life” (Jn 3:16; TM). That
whosoever ever is the true sense of this construction in this present passage
is confirmed indisputably by the contextual come to and by the promise of
eternal life (and its attendant security) to the one who comes (even initially).
Syntactical probability gives way to contextual certainty.

       Should versus Will
       Conditionalists will object to such a translation by insisting that such
phrases are translated properly as should not perish, hunger, or thirst.
Supposedly, since the verses may be translated as saying should rather than
shall or will, these verses reputedly do not denote certainty. To be sure, the
subjunctive mood does not, in and of itself, prove that the potential action
will take place. Jesus is not guaranteeing that people will believe, for
example.
       On other hand, Jesus is guaranteeing what will happen if they do
believe. Hence, the mood should be translated as will to convey this
contextual certainty.20 Almost all translations concur in translating in Jn 6:35
in such a manner as to convey the certainty: Whoever ever comes/believes
(z) will never ever hunger/thirst.21
       That will is the proper translation of the subjunctive mood in this
context is confirmed by the interchangeable presence of the indicative mood
to denote factual certainty. As a statement of fact, Jesus is saying, “I will
raise him up on the last day” (Jn 6:40).22 The certainty of being raised by the
Son in v. 40 is just as certain as coming once one has been given by the
Father in v. 37. No one doubts that those who are given will come; thus, no
aspectual reason exists to doubt that those who are given will be raised by
the Son.

       Never and Never Ever
      By their very nature, litotes (i.e., understatements) tend to be rather
emphatic. The litotes in this passage are especially strong. In fact, the double
negative with the subjunctive is the strongest affirmation possible in that it
not only rules out the factual occurrence of the negated event but also rules
out even its possibility. Thus, Jesus is not merely saying, “I will never ever
cast out” (v. 37). Indeed, the Lord is affirming even more forcefully, “I
cannot cast out,” and understandably so since God “cannot lie” when He
promises eternal life (Tit 1:2).

safe. She was given to him for safe keeping.

       What Baptism Is Not
     Water baptism is not a condition for salvation from hell. Those who

conditioned on faith alone. Make no mistake; they have rejected the gospel

       What Baptism Is
     Baptism is a public profession of faith that signifies the believer’s
personal commitment to run the race. Baptism is intended for sheep, not rats.
Baptism is required for full-fledged discipleship (Mt 28:19-20) and is the

regarding the crown (1Cor 9:27).

be saved; but he who has disbelieved shall be condemned” (Mk 16:16). Note
carefully that disbelief is the sole consideration pertaining to condemnation
and that salvation and condemnation are referring to two different things.

                •    Believe ⇒ No Condemnation

salvation from eternal damnation in hell. Implicitly, this type of salvation
(from such condemnation) is by faith alone. Those who simply believe are
assured of salvation in this regard.

is referring to a salvation that culminates in a crown. When Jesus concludes

       Three Types of Salvation
        Broadly speaking, the Bible uses the verb save and the noun salvation
to describe three types of spiritual salvation. The accomplished phase of
salvation refers to the past moment in time when the individual first believed
in Christ and, thereby, received the free gift of eternal life. Consequently and
eternally, this person is saved unconditionally from hell. It is a done deal.
The saved sheep is no longer in danger of drowning in the lake.
        The current, ongoing phase of salvation refers to the sheep’s present
running for the prize. During this phase, the sheep often will cry out for help
and for salvation in terms of facing everyday challenges. The sheep do not
cry out for salvation in terms of reaching heaven. That is a done deal.
Heaven is not the prize for which the sheep run. The sheep run for a prize
within heaven—a crown—not for entrance into heaven.
        The future potential phase of salvation refers to possible co-
glorification with Christ: “On the one hand, if you are children of God, then
you are heirs; on the other hand, if you co-suffer with Christ in order that
you also may be co-glorified with Christ, then you are co-heirs with Christ”
(Rom 8:17, TM). The demarcation of co-glorification is reserved in the
second half of the verse for those believers who are willing to pay the price.
Simple heirship is conditioned only on being a child of God. Co-heirship,
however, is conditioned on co-suffering with Christ.
        Jesus ran the race set before Him for the joy set before Him. Believers
too must run the race set before them if they are to obtain the joyful reward
set before them of sitting on thrones with Him (Heb 12:1-2; cp. 10:35;
Rev 3:21). All sheep (i.e., all believers, all children of God) will enter
heaven. Only faithful sheep, though, will rule with Christ in heaven. The
experience of this future dimension of salvation in terms of kingdom

       A Father’s Perspective
      I help Jon in a lot of ways. He is my son, my sheep so to speak, part of
my household/sheepfold. But I do not help Jon remain my child. I give him
no help whatsoever in that regard. Why? Because he does not need my help
to be my child. His being my child is not contingent on anything he does or
could do. His earthly father is not some berserk parent screaming at his son
to perform well at a cross country meet as if every drop of his parental love
for his son is wrapped up in how well his son performs. Jon remaining my
child or being loved as my child is not conditioned on his performance. Far
from it! To put it mildly, I would feel insulted if Jon thought he needed my
help to remain the object of my fatherly love for him.
      Granted, it is natural and proper for parents to love it when their
children perform well and to shout words of encouragement. Still, this is a
far cry from screaming as if the child’s relationship is in jeopardy unless the
child performs well. My love for him as his father and his relationship to me
as my child are not conditioned on his performance.
        In the same way, Jon does not need his heavenly Father’s help to
remain His child because his being a child of God is in no way contingent
upon anything Jon does now that he has become a child of God. Once born,
always born. Once a child of God, always a child of God. Those who think
otherwise insult the fatherly character of God. The helpful encouragement
that his Father shouts to run the race is not intended to encourage Jon to
work hard at remaining His child but to work hard at earning the crown.
These warnings and exhortations are God’s way of saying, “The crown is at
stake, so run well my son.”

crucified with the result that His word would be fulfilled. Fulfilling His

their hardhearted unbelief, Scripture would be fulfilled (Jn 12:38). As a
result of their unbelieving hatred, Scripture would be fulfilled (Jn 15:25). As
a result of their casting dice for His garments, Scripture would be fulfilled
(Jn 19:24).
      On the other hand, some of the things Jesus did were with intention of
fulfilling God’s word. He told those who had come to arrest Him to let those
with Him to go their way in order that (hina) His word would be fulfilled
(Jn 18:9). On the cross, it was no accident that He said, “I am thirsty”
(Jn 19:28). He intentionally fulfilled Scripture with that statement. John
purposefully makes a parenthetical insert to clarify that Jesus made this
Endnotes                                                                  75
statement in order that (hina) Scripture would be fulfilled. Such passages
are examples of hina purpose-result clauses.
      A passage of Scripture like Jn 3:16 likewise expresses purpose-result.
Translating this verse as should not perish expresses purpose. Will not perish
expresses result. Both translations are legitimate. For an unbeliever, this
verse conveys intention; for the believer the verse assures the result. God’s
intention is that all may find salvation in Christ by believing in Him for
eternal life. Once they believe, they are assured of having eternal life.
      Phil 2:10-11 contains a well-known purpose-result clause: God has
exalted Jesus in order that (hina) every knee would/will bow and in order
that (hina) every tongue would/will confess that Jesus Christ is Lord. (The
initial hina is applicable to both verbs). To claim that the hina purpose-result
clause only means that everyone should bow and confess (rather than will
bow and confess) borders on blaspheme. Not even the devil will have any
choice other than to acknowledge the Lordship of Christ. Freewill
speculations will not override God’s sovereign will. Similarly, to assert that
Jn 3:16 only means that believers should not perish (rather than will not
perish) is tantamount to soteriological blasphemy.

         The expression ekballo exo (cast out) occurs in Mt 21:39; Lk 13:28;
Jn 6:37; 12:31; Acts 7:38. Expulsion rather than mere rejection is under
consideration. The NLT misses the sense of the phrase when it translates
Jn 6:37 as “never reject them.” To the contrary, in each of the other verses,
NLT properly uses out to translate the expression. In fact, the Greek
expression stresses the out by expressing it with both the verb and adverb.
Not only does exo mean out, the prefix ek in ekballo (cast out) does also.
The promise that He would not cast them out presupposes that He had
welcomed them inside.

         To preserve the neuter gender of pas hos, various translations render
it as whatever, everything, or all that. Doing so, however, is both awkward
and unnecessary. People are clearly meant, not things, in these texts
(Jn 6:37,39; 17:2). The whatever of the BBE in 6:37a rightly becomes
anyone in 6:37b because the latter phrase is masculine rather than neuter.
Everything becomes anyone in the NAB and NRS. In a host of translations,
all that in 6:37a becomes him or whoever in 6:37b. The Greek text simply is
using a neuter idiomatic expression to refer to a group of people (not to a
group of things). Therefore, the more natural counterparts in English would
be to render this neuter in personal terms, such as whoever, everyone, or all
whom. The first two translations are popular here; the last translation is
popular in Jn 17:2.
      This collective abstract neuter expression uses pan (a neuter form of
pas) with ho (a neuter form of hos). Some commentators, such as Morris
(John, NICNT, 367), overstress the collective nature of those who are given
idiomatically in 6:37a so as to suppose that it subordinates the individuality
of those who come in 6:37b to God’s sovereignty. Borchert, always on the
Page 76                                                      The Race of Grace
lookout for what he considers unresolvable tension, errs in the opposite
direction and speculates that the collective giving in 6:37 only denotes God’s
“general intention” for all people to come. Still, Borchert is correct that in
Jn 6:40, the pas ho (the latter being the Greek article on this occasion) would
refer to “each individual authentic coming to Jesus” (Borchert, John, NAC,
265).
      Given the offsetting comments of the commentators, a more neutral
(and natural) proposal would be to equate the pas hos of 6:37,39 with the
pas ho of 6:40. Although the ho used with pas in 6:37 and 6:39 is a relative
pronoun (and should not be confused with ho, the Greek article used with
pas in 6:40), I do not find any substantial reason to differentiate between
these expressions in this context. Michaels notes the collective neuter in 6:37
and thinks that it must be used corporately while the singular masculine
participle for coming to Jesus focuses on individuals. Like Morris, he
perceives a “framework of sovereignty and election” that practically rejects
the old Gospel song, “Whoever Will” (Michaels, John, NICNT, 376-377). A
better recourse would be to keep the old Gospel song and acknowledge that
whosoever will may come. A simple grammatical observation can solve the
collective-versus-individual tension easily enough. This tension is not as
unresolvable as some would make it out to be. By grammatically combining
the collective and singular forms, Jesus provides a beautiful picture of a
distributive aspect. (For illustration, see my book, John in Living Color.)
      As Robertson notes, pa/j o` plus the participle uses a common
construction in which “the idea is ‘every [pa/j],’ and o` and the participle are
in apposition.” Basically, the action conveyed by every (speaking
collectively) is distributed to each one individually. Most significantly, for
these constructions, pa/j o` “is practically equivalent to pa/j o[stij” (GGNT,
772). But o[stij is “often equivalent to o[j” (GD). In this same discussion,
Robertson also cites the pa/n o] in Jn 6:37,39. The very construction that
Robertson is describing above (pa/j o` plus the participle) occurs in Jn 6:40
and, thus, is in juxtaposition with pa/n o] plus the participle in Jn 6:37,39.
This juxtaposition is only icing on the cake. Even if pa/j o` did not occur in
v. 40, one would have grounds for considering the pa/n o] as practically
equivalent to pa/j o` by virtue of Robertson’s grammatical comments. The
statements in vv. 37 and 39 reinforce this conclusion. But when v. 40 is
added to consideration, not equating pa/n o] with pa/j o` becomes practically
inexcusable. As a result, because pa/j o` in v. 40 is best rendered
distributively as whosoever ever, I have rendered pa/n o] in vv. 37 and 39
within my translation in the same manner. After all, the Greek expressions
are contextually equivalent.
      Similarly, although Robertson suggests that pa/n o] be translated
impersonally as whatsoever in Jn 17:2 (WPNT), the neuter typically is
rendered in a personal manner as: all whom (ASV, DRA, ESV, NAS, NRS,
Endnotes                                                                   77
RSV), all those (BBE, GNV, NIV, NJB), all them (GNV), everyone (NET),
each one (NLT). Therefore, I would amend Robertson’s whatsoever by
translating it as whosoever. As even Robertson points out, the o] refers to
persons a few verses later in Jn 17:24 (GGNT, 653). Robertson also states
that the pa/n construction is explained by the plural auvtoi/j in 17:2 (WPNT).
Note, however, that pa/n o] is used in conjunction with a singular whoever
comes (to.n evrco,menon) in Jn 6:37, a singular him (auvtou/ and auvto.) in
Jn 6:39, and a plural them (auvtoi/j) in 17:2. The collective neuter is
explained by both the singular and plural. A distributive aspect would easily
accommodate both. For example, the distributive whoever ever believes
sometimes occurs in the singular (Jn 3:15-16,18,36; 6:35,47; 7:38) and
sometimes in the plural (Jn 1:12; 7:39; 8:31). In Jn 7:38-39, it occurs in both
forms interchangeably.

         Judas is one such rabbit. Some forfeitists insist that Judas was a
genuine believer given by the Father to Jesus but that Judas, nevertheless,
lost his salvation. The Judas exception clause in Jn 17:12 is thought to prove
that this is the case: “While I was with them, I was keeping them in Your
name which You have given Me; and I guarded them, and not one of them
perished except [ei me] the son of perdition, in order that the Scripture might
be fulfilled” (TM). To be fair to forfeitists, let it be noted, however, that not

the group that was given because of the preponderance of the evidence.

inclusion. Multiple instances may be cited: The priests were not part of
David’s group (Mt 12:4). Elijah being sent to none of them except to
Zarephath does not mean that they were part of the group to which he was
sent (Lk 4:26). That Naaman is exempted from the group of lepers in Israel
who were cleansed does not mean that he was part of the Israeli lepers
(Lk 4:27). That the men in Rev 9:4 form an exception to the grass and trees
does not mean that they previously were grass and trees! John does not mean
that the only practicing liars during the eternal state who are allowed into the
New Jerusalem are those whose names are in the Book of Life (Rev 21:27)!

(Jn 6:64). Securitists correctly observe that Judas is lumped in with
unbelievers initially, thereby indicating that he never believed.
Subsequently, Judas stands apart from the group that God had given Jesus
(Jn 18:9). Not merely is Judas an exception to Jesus’ protection, he is an
Endnotes                                                                  83
exception to Jesus’ possession. He is not part of the group that was given to
Jesus by the Father.
      Semantically, Jesus does not say that He lost Judas, only that Judas was
lost. To be sure, there is a play on words: None of them perished (apollumi)
but the perishing (apoleia) one. Yet apollumi can also mean to lose. Jesus
could have made a play on words in the first person rather than the third
person with something like this: “I lost none of them but the lost one.”
However, Jesus does not express His wordplay in first person but third
person. Implicitly, the lost one was not lost by Jesus. What is implicit here is
explicit shortly thereafter when Jesus indeed does use the first person for
apollumi: "Of those whom Thou hast given Me I lost not one” (Jn 18:9).
How many did Jesus lose of those who were given to Him? None, no
exceptions! Jesus should be understood as saying, “While I was with them, I
was keeping them in Your name which You have given Me; and I guarded
them, and not one of them perished except the son of perdition [who never
was given]” (Jn 17:12, TM). The same words are used for not one of
those/them in both verses and are also rendered as none by some
translations.

guarded those whom You have given Me, and none of them perished except
the son of perdition” (Jn 17:12; TM; MT). To put Jesus’ statements together
logically so that they form a complementary whole, the sense of the verses
could be expressed jointly this way: “I lost none whom You have given Me.
I guarded them, and none was lost except the one who never was given.”
Judas was excluded by explanation to avoid misunderstanding rather than by
removal to denote previous inclusion.

that Jesus is saying that believers who are following Him in discipleship are
secure because they have believed in Him, not because of their ongoing
discipleship.

Accordingly, if you believe, then you are a sheep. Conversely, if you are a
sheep, then you believe. Believers are sheep, and sheep are believers. The
condition for protection is that one be a sheep/believer, not that one be a
follower/disciple.
      Following is a natural expression of the sheep’s faith, not a necessary
condition for its protection. That is, the impartation or retention of the free
gift of eternal life is not conditioned on performance. Pertaining to being a
protected sheep, following in discipleship is natural, not necessary. (For a
related example, consider the fact that separation of the sheep from the goats
is conditioned on one being a sheep, not on one’s performance as a sheep.
See my book Woolly Wolves and Woolless Sheep.)
      Then again, even if some degree of following in discipleship is merely
descriptive of the type of behavior one would generally anticipate from a
sheep, such following is prescriptive as to what is necessary for the sheep to
experience the eternal life abundantly. Going in and out and finding pasture
is a picture of the abundant life available to Jesus’ followers (Jn 10:9-10).
Such following is not a condition for the salvation mentioned in these verses
but is a condition for experiencing eternal life in its fullness. Thus, even if
following is being used jointly as a description of discipleship and a
prescription for the fullness of eternal life, it is not a condition for the
protection afforded the sheep. Protection is not conditioned on the sheep’s
performance. Therefore, a descriptive-prescriptive interpretation is
plausible.
      More likely, perhaps, given the immediate context of Jn 10:27, the
statement that sheep follow is simply prescriptive on this occasion. Follow
is best understood metaphorically as simply referring to believe. A
comparison with the preceding verse (Jn 10:26) shows that the sheep are
those who hear and follow (i.e., believe). Follow = believe. To believe as a
(z) punctiliar response contextually suffices for salvation/protection (see
enter in Jn 10:9). Jesus’ point is that the sheep believe in Him rather than in
someone else (much less in themselves) for their protection (Jn 10:5). The
conceptual and structural parallel in Jn 5:24 provides strong collaboration
for this metaphorical interpretation. (For further argumentation along this
line, see my book Believe: An Aspectual and Metaphorical Analysis from the
Gospel of John). Thus, this prescriptive interpretation understands follow to
refer to punctiliar faith, not to linear discipleship.
      Interestingly enough, the parallel in Jn 5:24 also dovetails the
metaphorical interpretation with my defense of unconditional security
derived from Heb 10:14 in that both verses syntactically have a powerful
parallel relationship. Accordingly, even if follow is linear, it might denote a
() distributive rather than (£) durative aspect—yet another argument
Page 86                                                       The Race of Grace
for unconditional security. (For a discussion of the aspectual relationship
between Heb 10:14 and Jn 5:24, see my book Fallen from Grace but Not
from Perfection.) This prescriptive-distributive interpretation observes that
as more and more converts come to saving faith punctiliarly, the group of
believers following Christ grows numerically.
      Even if follow is descriptive, it need not be descriptive of full-fledged
discipleship in this context. Rather, it simply may be describing what saving
faith normally looks like: believing in Christ for one’s protection rather than
in one’s performance or someone else. Follower = believer. That such
following is descriptively linear does not mean that it is necessitatively
durative. Logically, the continued quantitative possession of the free
dimension of eternal life already imparted cannot be conditioned on
continuing to believe. (See Illustration 12. Duality of Eternal Life, 22.) This
combined prescriptive interpretation understands follow to refer to
descriptive belief, not to linear discipleship. In this case, the securitist could
contend for a prescriptive-descriptive interpretation. The saving faith
which is required (i.e., which is prescriptive) for salvation is described in
terms of how it would look in everyday life. One need not think it strange to
picture believers as believing.
      To reiterate the basic options available to the securitist from the
discussion as articulated in this note, this summation is offered:
•   Descriptive: follow is descriptive of discipleship as a reasonable
    expectation rather than a soteriological necessitation.
•   Descriptive-prescriptive: follow is descriptive of discipleship as a
    reasonable expectation rather than a soteriological necessitation, yet
    such following is a misthological necessitation for having eternal life
    abundantly.
•   Prescriptive: follow is a metaphor saving faith (which by its very nature
    is punctiliar in terms of meeting the soteric necessity for protection).
•   Prescriptive-distributive: follow is a metaphor for saving faith (which
    by its very nature is punctiliar in terms of meeting the soteric necessity
    for protection), yet such faith might be distributively linear for the class
    of believers.
•   Prescriptive-descriptive: follow is a metaphor for saving faith (which
    by its very nature is punctiliar in terms of meeting the soteric necessity
    for protection), yet such faith might be descriptively linear for the
    individual believer.
