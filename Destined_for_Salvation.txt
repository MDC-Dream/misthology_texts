                                                  Introduction
       For God has not destined us for wrath, but for obtaining salvation through our Lord Jesus Christ, 10 who
      died for us, that whether we are awake or asleep, we may live together with Him. (1Thess 5:9-10)

    According to pretribulationalists, 1Thess 5:9-10 is the premier text affirming a pretribulational rapture. Yet within their
ranks, at least five primary interpretations exist as to how to interpret this key text. In order to survey these options, we will
need to examine a number of key words within Paul’s statement in view of the immediate context, the broader context of the
epistle, the context of the parallel passage in the second epistle to the Thessalonians, and factor conceptual parallels from the
NT into consideration also. This introduction will begin with a bird’s eye, overview of the five options. The body of the
discussion will then evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of these models as they relate to this focal passage. This study will
be a supplemental summary of the treatment provided in my book, The Outer Darkness.

                              Some Representative Examples
       In 1979, Edgar, a graduate of DTS (Dallas Theological Seminary), wrote an article in which he argued that Paul’s
point in 1Thess 5:10 is that morally asleep believers will be raptured and thus spared the wrath of the tribulation.1* He framed
the discussion of this passage around how one interprets the word asleep, as referring either to being morally or physically
asleep. The implications are staggering if it refers to being morally asleep. In that case, not only does the passage logically
provide conclusive proof for an unconditional rapture but also for unconditional security. The majority opinion is that Paul is
only affirming the rapture of those who are physically asleep (i.e., who have died). Edgar wrote powerfully in favor of the
minority opinion, in which Paul is understood as affirming an unconditional rapture and, therefore, by logical extension,
unconditional security. Notably, 1979 was the same year that Walvoord (the president of DTS†) released a revised and
enlarged edition of his book, The Rapture Question, which contrastively takes the majority position and simultaneously
makes the rapture a reward for believers who are morally awake (i.e., watchful/faithful).

*
  In 1978, the year preceding Edgar’s article, Thomas provided a preview of this Edgar-Hodgian perspective, saying that in
1Thess 5:10 Paul “had to calm their fears by convincing them of their participation in the parousia regardless of their
degree of watchfulness” (bold mine). Thomas attempts to reconcile this consolation with the exhortations by suggesting that
Paul was warning “them against the extremes of overacting” (EBC, 286). In 1893, Govett advocated the minority view,
regarding morally asleep as the reference in 5:10, just like Constable, Edgar, Hodges, Thomas, and Wilkin (see Illustration
1). Yet Govett would disagree with them in regard to the significance (see Illustration 2). Leading proponents of the
respective views have been chosen for the chart, not necessarily the originator of the view.

        Although adopting Edgar’s position necessarily requires that one view the rapture as a gift, many of those rejecting his
position still would regard the rapture as a gift. In fact, the majority position, as he has framed the discussion, seems rather
evenly divided as to the gift-versus-reward perspective. Due to his comments in Rapture, Walvoord is taken herein as a
representative of the majority perspective in which the rapture is considered a reward for faithful believers. One might
suppose from that description that he would advocate a partial rapture. However, Walvoord is thoroughly opposed to the
partial-rapture position. For that reward position, one may consult Govett, who indeed does advocate a partial rapture. The
primary difference between these two rewards positions is that Walvoord believes that all believers will be faithful; therefore,
all believers will qualify for the rapture. Govett, on the other hand, believes that only some believers will be sufficiently
faithful to qualify for the rapture. Viewed from this perspective, then, we have two competing pretribulational views for a
conditional rapture that picture the passage as promising a reward for faithful believers.
        In 1982, Hodges wrote an article in Walvoord: A Tribute that advocated the same minority position as Edgar, making
the rapture a gift, of course.2 Ironically, in this tribute to Walvoord, Hodges posed an interpretation that cannot be attributed
to Walvoord. With his advocacy of the minority view regarding asleep, Hodges posed an unconditional rapture that is granted
freely to all believers, even unfaithful ones.
    In 1983, the DTS Bible Knowledge Commentary (BKC) on the NT was released, in which Constable concurred with the
Edgar-Hodgian minority conclusion. Therefore, in rapid-fire succession we have a concurrence of Thomas (1978), Edgar
(1979), Hodges (1982), and Constable (1983) in favor of the minority position. Some have listened. In 1995, Martin (NAC)
summarized both the physical and moral options for asleep in 5:10. And he shows a positive inclination toward the Edgar-
Hodgian position in his conclusion: “Even if they should falter, Christ will not fail.”3
    In 2000, when the revised journal articles by Edgar and Hodges were released within the same issue of CTSJ, Edgar and
Hodges acknowledged that their position was still not being well received in the scholarly community at large.4 For example,
Hodges states: “The true force of the phrase whether we wake or sleep is crucial. By-and-large expositors have overlooked its
A Supplemental Summary                                                                                                   Page 5
actual meaning, especially post-tribulational expositors. Frequently, they construe this phrase as a reference to Christians who
are either alive (wake) or dead (sleep) at Christ’s coming. But careful examination of the Greek text shows that this is not
exegetically possible” (emphasis his).5 Despite Hodges’ strong claim that the majority position is not exegetically possible,
advocates of the majority position still remain exegetically persistent in resisting Hodges’ conclusion because of a glaring
weakness within Hodges’ position. Edgar and Hodges essentially have insisted on limiting the basis for Paul’s appeal for
moral alertness to mere gratitude. Most expositors have insisted that more than gratitude must be involved. Otherwise, in
their ears, they hear Edgar and Hodges rephrasing Paul as exhorting, “Be awake because it does not matter if you stay
asleep!” Understandably, such an perception is difficult to accept.
        In 2005, Phillips’ (JPCS) adopted the Edgar-Hodgian perspective regarding being morally asleep. Importantly, though,
Phillips thinks that the Bema provides part of the incentive to heed Paul’s exhortation to be watchful. Within his discussion
of this passage, he quotes the Lord’s warning in Mk 13:35-37 as being applicable to believers, as to their need to be watchful
in view of His coming: “This is a clear call for pre-Rapture alertness. Now is no time to be careless and lethargic about
spiritual things. The truth of the Lord’s coming is intended to keep us alert and watchful.”6 This language recalls Phillips’
earlier comments regarding 1Thess 3:13, where Paul prays that the Thessalonians will increase in love so that they may be
“unblamable in holiness before our God and Father at the coming [parousia] of our Lord Jesus with all His saints.” The Bema
is clearly linked with the rapture in this earlier Thessalonian context by Paul and Phillips:

      The Lord’s presence in the cloud signals the opening of His court....At this great arraignment, the works of the
      Lord’s people will be reviewed, to be rewarded or rebuked as need be. Some believers will appear before Him
      empty-handed and will see their life deeds go up in smoke....Rewards will be given, and each one’s position [of
      service] in the coming kingdom will be determined. Remember that this is a judgment seat, not a mercy seat. It
      will be a sobering event.7

       Phillips commenced his discussion of the Lord’s parousia by reminding his readers, “We have already seen that the
word refers to the Lord’s presence, specifically in a prophetic context...and to our appearing before Him at the judgment seat
of Christ.”8 The earlier discussion to which Phillips refers is concerning the crown of exultation in connection with in the
presence (emprosthen) of our Lord Jesus at His coming (parousia) in 1Thess 2:19. There, Phillips states, “The judgment seat
of Christ is to be feared. It is not going to be a Sunday School picnic.”9 Likewise, in 1Thess 5:23, where Paul prays (not
guarantees!) that they will be “blameless at the coming [parousia] of our Lord Jesus Christ,” Phillips again reminds his
readers, “Jesus is coming! We need to keep ourselves in ‘spotless integrity’ in view of our meeting with him in the air.”10
This last statement harkens back to the context of 1Thess 4:15, the only other occasion Paul uses parousia in this
Thessalonian epistle. In Phillips’ mind, then, Paul’s teaching regarding the parousia presumes the background of the Lord’s
warning, even in the context of the consolation regarding the rapture in 1Thess 4:13-5:11. In Phillips’ assessment this Bema
background provides a crucial part of the basis for Paul’s exhortation to be watchful in 1Thess 5:6-8. This is an advance over
Edgar and Hodges, who exclude the Bema as part of the basis for Paul’s exhortation in this particular passage. Linking the
Lord’s coming with the Bema is nothing new, of course, not even in this Thessalonian passage among majority advocates.
The important point to note for present purposes, however, is that not all those holding the Edgar-Hodgian minority view
would limit the basis for the exhortation to gratitude, even after the release of the Edgar-Hodgian articles.
       In 2010, Wilkin (GNTC) concurred with the Edgar-Hodgian minority view but not with the Edgar-Hodgian limitation
regarding gratitude. Like Phillips, Wilkin clearly thinks that the Bema provides part of the incentive for the exhortation:

              Implicit in Paul’s statement in v. 4 is that the Rapture might overtake believers as a thief in the night.
      This does not mean that such believers would miss the Rapture. It means they would be unpleasantly
      surprised....
              Paul does not explain in these verses what watchful believers will get or what sleeping believers will
      fail to receive. However, we know from other texts in Paul and elsewhere that what is at stake is ruling
      with Christ....Surely the believers in Thessalonica knew this as well. Compare 5:23. (Italics his.)11

        Commendably, Wilkin does an equally excellent job in noting the Bema motif in other Thessalonian passages as well.
For example, concerning walking worthy of God in 1Thess 2:12, he surmises, “Those who serve a president or a king must
put forth a supreme effort.” Why? Because those who do so “will receive rewards.” What reward exactly? The realization of
“being called into His kingdom” is at stake, which “is something more than entrance and citizenship” since “all believers are
guaranteed to be in the Lord’s kingdom.” “Believers are called to rule with Christ.”12
        In contradistinction to Wilkin, however, Govett is of the firm belief that in 1Thess 5:1-11 Paul indeed tells us exactly
what is at stake—the rapture and entrance into millennial kingdom. Yet Govett is of the same opinion as Edgar, Hodges, and
Wilkin regarding the minority perception that Paul’s comfort applies to believers who are morally asleep. Therefore, within
the flow chart regarding asleep, Govett must be graphed as falling under the branch for the minority position (see Illustration
1); whereas, regarding the flowchart pertaining to the wrath and salvation, he must be placed under the reward branch (see
Illustration 2).

        In 2009, Stallard (TFCBC), a fellow FG pretribulationist, provided an extended quote from Hodges’ article and yet
completely ignored the conclusion to which Hodges was driving in 5:10—that whether we are morally awake or asleep we
will be with the Lord. Why?13 The weakness of the minority position, mentioned above, was probably too great for Stallard to
ignore. Not all who would treat the rapture as a gift in this passage would necessarily do so if their theology was judged
holistically. Nevertheless, Stallard may be used to represent that group of expositors who interpret this passage as a gift and
yet side with the majority perspective.
        As for my position as charted in Illustration 1, it might be described as a Govett-Hodgian-Wilkin combination, though
I will progress further than just providing a mediation of their positions. Hence one might combine the hermeneutical values
for their respective branches under the minority trunk to derive the relative hermeneutical strength of my argument as nine
(2+3+4). More comprehensively, though, as seen in Illustration 2, my position has a cumulative score of ten on a scale of ten
(1+2+3+4=10) when the branches under both main trunks are combined.
        In a word, I take the Bema position further than my fellow FG counterparts. Phillips and Wilkin have taken positive
steps to address the weakness of the minority position by grounding Paul’s exhortations in the Bema and, thereby, not
limiting the basis for the exhortations to gratitude for unconditional benefits such as the rapture. However, Phillips and
Wilkin have not gone nearly as far as the text will allow, indeed even encourage, in addressing this misperceived weakness of
the minority position. When done properly and fully, there is no weakness to the minority position. My mediation will take
the strengths (not the weaknesses) of both—the majority and the minority positions—and combine them into a strong
position devoid of the weakness of either position. This completes the survey as described by the two charts. We can now
explore these positions in more detail.

                                                    Mediation
    In 1992, through personal correspondence, I briefly posed an interpretation to Hodges that would have eliminated the
handicap in his position altogether. Nevertheless, he declined from embracing this solution because of complications that he
perceived in its closest cross reference (2Thess 2:13). With their revised articles in 2000, Edgar and Hodges have had
opportunity to try to overcome their handicap. If the response by Stallard, among others, is a barometer in gauging the
success by Edgar and Hodges in doing so, they have failed to provide a fully persuasive argument. Reasonably, then, it is
time that the FG community consider the alternative that I posed and stop playing with a handicap. Hodges fought very well
for a man with one arm tied behind his back in defending the minority position. Imagine how much better he would have
been able to fight the good fight if he had had both arms available. I pose that we fight this fight at full strength, with an
interpretation that powerfully explains both—the exhortations and consolation—not just one or the other. Last year, in 2012,
I finally released my book, The Outer Darkness, which fully addresses Hodges’ concern for both this primary passage and its
sister passage. I never thought it would take twenty years to do so, but of course I undertook a far more ambitious range of
inquiry than simply dealing with these two passages. In the present discussion I will summarize some of my extensive
discussion therein pertaining to these two passages and augment it with further discussion of other related passages.
        The crucial component of Hodges’s argument is, naturally, to interpret being asleep in 1Thess 4 as referring to being
physically asleep (i.e., dead) but in 1Thess 5 as being morally asleep (i.e., unfaithful). Stallard has posed a Walvoordian-
Hodgian hybrid in which being asleep in 1Thess 5:10 is still regarded being physically asleep (so Walvoord, etc.) but the
passage is understood, by those in FG at least, as still affirming the rapture of morally asleep believers (so Hodges). My
position will accord fully with Hodges on that matter (Illustration 1). At the same time, I will pose a mediation between the
gift and reward positions.
        This mediation is not like the confusion that exists in the Lordship camp, in which a gift is considered a reward or a
reward a gift. Axiomatically, a gift must not be confused with a reward so as to pose some such nonsense as a free reward
that all believers receive. Considering the fact that Walvoord’s proposes that all the genuine believers are being exhorted and
consequently will be faithful and thus rewarded in this passage, I am reminded of a statement by Fuller, in another
connection, that would be equally applicable to the present situation: “A command that everyone keeps is superfluous, and a
reward that everyone receives for a virtue that everyone has is nonsense.”14 Additionally, as to the present passage, any
model that confuses a gift with a reward is worthless as an overall model. Therefore, I find Walvoord’s model to be
superfluous nonsense and have assigned it zero hermeneutical value. Notwithstanding, certain exegetical observations made
by those who hold his model are very valuable and must not be set aside. Observing the uselessness of the Walvoordian
branch as a model does not justify dismissing all the insights by him or others who hold such a model. Those holding this
model make some valid points that must be factored into consideration when developing an alternative model.
        Nor is my mediation like Stallard’s hybrid, which attempts to combine the weak aspects of the Walvoordian and
Hodgian positions. Combining their weaknesses results in a compromise, not a successful mediation. Contrastively, the
mediation proposed herein will combine the strengths of the Walvoordian and Hodgian positions into a strong mediation. On

Exhortations
       Four let us exhortations are found within the first epistle to the Thessalonians, all within the immediate context leading
up to Paul’s explanation in verses 9 and 10 as to why his exhortations need to be heeded.15 The explanatory hoti that
introduces vv. 9-10 is typically translated as for or because. Within these two verses Paul will provide his explanation as to
why his four exhortations in the three preceding verses must be heeded:
      6
        So then let us not sleep as others do, but let us be alert and [let us] be sober. 7 For those who sleep do their
      sleeping at night, and those who get drunk get drunk at night. 8 But since we are of the day, let us be sober,
      having put on the breastplate of faith and love, and as a helmet, the hope of salvation. (1Thess 5:6-8)

         Obviously, one’s interpretation of vv. 9-10 must be able to give a convincing explanation as to how Paul’s statement
in these two verses provides motivation to heed his exhortations. The strength of the reward model is that it has strong
explanatory power: Be alert because your salvation from the eschatological wrath is at stake! The weakness for the gift model
is that its advocates tend to limit the basis for compliance to gratitude or some equivalent. But as applied to the tribulational
wrath, it yields a very strange sense: Be alert because even if you are not alert you will be delivered from the wrath of the
tribulation! While an appeal to gratitude for unconditional deliverance is theoretically possible, it hardly seems probable.
From the perspective of exhortations, the advantage clearly favors the reward branch of the hermeneutical tree.

Comfort/Encourage
       Paul ends his first paragraph regarding the rapture with this word of solace: “Therefore comfort one another with these
words” (1Thess 4:17). If Paul’s purpose is to warn them that their salvation from the tribulation is at stake, then words of
warning rather than of comfort would be the natural expectation. His conclusion for the second paragraph also seems better
suited for encouragement rather than alarm: “Therefore encourage one another, and build up one another, just as you also are
doing” (1Thess 5:11). Stallard believes that this encouragement is a recap of the comfort. This recap assumption would be in
harmony with both the reward view and the gift view. Basically, both paragraphs are presumed to be teaching the same thing:
The pretribulational timing of the rapture is either a comfort to the faithful believers only (so Govett and Walvoord), or it is a
comfort to unfaithful believers also (so Stallard, Hodges, and Wilkin). The advantage regarding comfort is in favor of the gift
view, but it is weak compared to the favor enjoyed by the reward view regarding the nature of the exhortation. With this
broad perspective providing the big picture, we can now investigate some of the particulars of each position further.

Reward
       Both of the reward models in Illustration 2 (i.e., Govett and Walvoord) teach a conditional rapture. The difference
between the two positions lies in their assessment as to how many believers will be raptured. The FG branch (represented by
Govett) affirms that only faithful believers are raptured but all believers are unconditionally secure in terms of their entrance
into the eternal kingdom. The Reformed branch (represented by Walvoord) also affirms that only faithful believers are
raptured but then argues that all believers are faithful. Fruit shows root. If your fruit does not prove that you are saved, then
you will not be raptured. Although some of those holding this latter perspective insist that they are not conditioning salvation
upon faithfulness, the logical extension of their argument and the exegetical basis for their argument nullify any such claims.
Proveitists are conditional securitists. They teach a final salvation that is conditioned on performance.

                                                                 Walvoord
     As the spokesperson for the conditional-full-rapture position, I have chosen Walvoord. Indeed, when I did a quick Google
search for partial rapture theory to see what the current perceptions are, the first link that came up was a link for an article by
Walvoord refuting Govett’s partial rapture theory. This article presents the heart of Walvoord’s position from his book, The
Rapture Question, which I already have interacted with in my book, The Outer Darkness.
     In certain respects, Walvoord’s article is an example of the pot calling the kettle black. Largely, due to the power of
popular opinion among premillennialists, Walvoord starts with the premise that the benefits of salvation by grace apart from
works extends to the rapture. In doing so, though, he assumes the point to be proven as part of his opening premise. He begs
the question in order to characterize Govett as not believing in the gospel of grace. However, if one dispenses with
Walvoord’s arguing in a circle, one can readily deduce that Walvoord is the one who does not believe the gospel of grace.
According to Walvoord’s article, “the evidence of salvation, good works, is pointed to as necessary to salvation”
(Jam 2:21-26). But such a statement is equivalent to asserting that good works are necessary for salvation!23
     In other words, good works are necessary to go to Walvoordian heaven: “Those without works are not true believers.”
Works are necessary evidence of your faith: faith ⇒ works ⇒ heaven. In this Walvoordian backloading of the gospel, as is
normally the case in such Reformed theology, final justification is conditioned on your good works. In response to Govett’s
claim that the rapture is a reward for good words, Walvoord would ask Govett, “How much works?” But why not turn right
around and ask Walvoord, “How much works are required to prove that you are a genuine believer and thus permitted into
heaven within your Reformed view?” When it comes to salvation from eternal damnation, Walvoord, not Govett, is the one
teaching “a works principle in opposition to Scriptural teaching on grace.” For that matter, even Walvoord considers the
rapture a reward; he just convolutes his acknowledging this to be the case by saying that it “is a reward only in the sense that
it is a fruit of faith in Christ.”24 Therefore, the branch leading to Walvoord’s position in the chart has been labeled LS.*
     While Walvoord acts as though Govett would not allow any difference between merely professing believers and genuine
believers, the truth of the matter is that Walvoord is actually critical of Govett’s allowing unfaithful believers into heaven
because Walvoord’s premise rejects the FG premise that unfaithful believers can be genuine believers. But how faithful do
you have to be in order to qualify as a genuine believer in Walvoordian theology. Faithful enough to qualify for the rapture!
     Walvoord’s comments in this article are in accord with his chapter on this subject in his book, Rapture.25 As discussed
more fully in my book, however, although like Walvoord I reject the partial rapture position, I find Walvoord’s soteriological
criticism of Govett (and Lang) to be unfair and inaccurate and some of Walvoord’s eschatological criticisms of their position
to be objectionable. For example, Walvoord says, concerning Mt 24:40-51, that faithfulness determines who is a true
believer.26 I object strongly. If that were the case, then faithfulness is required to enter heaven, and salvation from hell is
determined ultimately by faith and works, which is the conclusion to which Walvoord’s soteriology logically leads.
     As to the more immediate point at hand, Walvoord’s analysis is not only defective soteriologically but dysfunctional
exegetically. Concerning 1Thess 5:6, he inexplicably thinks that genuine believers inevitably will watch for the Lord’s return
and live lives in keeping with that watching and thus demonstrate their faith. On the contrary, the very fact that Paul is
writing to believers who already are sons of light, and on that basis urging them to live like sons of light, indicates that Paul
does not believe that believers invariably will live like sons of light.
     When Paul says, “Since we are of the day, let us be sober,” he basically is saying, “Since you are saved, live like it.”
Since you are spiritually alive, stay spiritually awake! Put your faith and love into practice. This is what he means with his
quadruple repetition of let us (Gr. text, 1Thess 5:6,8). As an exhortation, it is something we should do, not something we
inevitably will do. Likewise, in Eph 5:8 Paul exhorts, “You were formerly darkness, but now you are light in the Lord; walk
as children of light.”

sleep (katheudo) is an exhortation to be morally awake (and it is), then the consolation in v. 10 that whether we are awake
(gregoreo) or sleep (katheudo) we will be with the Lord is an unconditional assurance that even if believers are morally
asleep they will be with the Lord! Govett understood this! Walvoord does not! These verses deliver a staggering punch to
Govett’s partial rapture but a death blow to Walvoord’s Reformed premise. Not only is the possibility of carnality affirmed,
the impossibility of that carnality separating morally sleeping believers from the Lord is confirmed just as clearly.

works package. Thus he criticizes Govett for making the rapture and entrance rewards conditioned on works. Once one digs
below the rhetoric, however, one finds that Walvoord himself has made the whole package conditioned on works. “The
translation and resurrection of the church [i.e., the rapture] is a part of its [the church’s] salvation provided by grace and is a
reward only in the sense that it is a fruit of faith in Christ.”28 Thus the rapture is a reward for church-age believers according
to Walvoord. Likewise, “the reward given to believers at the judgment of the Gentiles [at the conclusion of the tribulation] is
entrance into the millennial kingdom....Their reward is entrance into the millennial kingdom.”29 According to Walvoord,
entrance into the kingdom is a reward for tribulational believers. Walvoord makes entrance into the kingdom a reward! Yet
this is the position of Govett whom Walvoord criticizes!
    Walvoord is correct in saying that Govett originated the partial rapture theory because Govett thought that “participation
in the kingdom is conditional and depends on worthy conduct.”30 Govett believes that entrance is a reward, but Walvoord
believes the same thing! The only real difference between Govett and Walvoord on this matter is that Govett believes that
believers should have conduct worthy of this reward, and Walvoord believes that they will have conduct worthy of this
reward.* In all reality, both men believe the rapture is a reward.
    Stated more fully, Walvoord’s position is equivalent to saying that saving faith is the only requirement for entrance into
the millennial kingdom, but works are required for faith to be saving; thus, faith and works are both required for entrance into
the millennial kingdom. Consequently, Walvoord does not really believe that faith is the only requirement for entrance into
the kingdom. The only substantial difference between these two men is that Govett thinks that only some believers have the
works necessary to merit this entrance while Walvoord believes all believers have the works necessary for this entrance.
Pentecost does no better in his rebuttal of the partial rapture position than Walvoord. After erroneously charging the partial
rapture position with a defective soteriological understanding, Pentecost then proceeds to blunder by claiming that the partial
rapture position “would make rewards a legal obligation on the part of God, rather than a gracious gift.”31 Pentecost thinks
that rewards are gifts and that the rapture is a reward that is given as a gift to all believers. This is confused nonsense.
                                                              Summary
    As seen above, we have two competing models for a conditional rapture: the partial rapture position (as represented by
Govett) and the majority opinion (as represented by Walvoord). Of these two arguments, Govett’s position is certainly
superior. As a theological system, it is soteriologically consistent in distinguishing a gift from a reward. In its exegesis of this
key text for the rapture, it provides a strong rationale for the exhortations. Further, it supplies a plausible basis for the
consolation. In contrast, Walvoord’s position is marked by inconsistency in confusing gift with reward, conditions entrance
into heaven on performance, fails to distinguish consistently between the forms of sleep being delineated by Paul within the
two passages, does not allow for the reality of carnality, and assumes the typically weak Reformed basis for the exhortation.
Overall, the majority position as represented by those like Walvoord is worthless as a model and hence given zero value in
the hermeneutical tree.

Gift-Reward
       The rapture is introduced in 1Thess 1:10, where Paul informs the Thessalonians that Jesus delivers [rhuomai] us from
the coming tribulational wrath. Unfortunately, like so many others, Hodges assumes that this deliverance is to be equated
with the salvation in 1Thess 5:8, so he translates the latter in terms of the former, asserting that Paul “exhorts the
Thessalonians to put on as a helmet the hope of salvation. This phrase might well be rendered, as a helmet the expectation of
deliverance [or, ‘rescue’]” (bracketed insert his; bold mine).36 According to this common mindset, the salvation of 5:8 equals
the deliverance of 1:10. Not so! If Paul meant to communicate the same deliverance in 5:8 as he did in 1:10, he easily could
have done so by using the verb save (sozo) in 1:10. Instead, he uses a different word (i.e., deliver; rhuomai) in 1:10 and then
uses salvation in 5:8-9, where he calls that salvation a hope.
       Hodges and company do very well in pursuing the significance of Paul’s using two different words for sleep. They
should do equally as well in acknowledging the import of his using two different words for deliver/salvation. Hodges is
mistaken in equating the deliverance of 1:10 with the salvation of 5:8-9. In a word, Hodges’ error is in making this equation:
deliverance = salvation.
       Govett, on the other hand, is correct in recognizing that the hope of salvation is a reward. His error lies in assuming
that the rapture was part of that reward: rapture = reward. He then compounds this error by making the hope of salvation in
5:8 a reward but salvation in 5:9 a gift. The passage enjoins gift with reward, but not in the manner stated by Govett.

passage. In the next verse, Jesus warns that if you act in this unworthy manner, you will pay the consequences.

being morally awake, Hodges responds to his critics: “Some suppose that an unconditional guarantee of deliverance from the
tribulation undermines the exhortation to watchfulness.”38 I respond, “Of course we do!” Like a number of others, I think that
Hodges’ attempt to reduce all of Paul’s statements in this context to unconditional guarantees undermines the exhortations.
Doing so makes Paul sound like he is exhorting, “Stay awake because it does not matter if you stay asleep!”
        Nevertheless, Hodges continues in his own defense: “However, such thinking flatly contradicts the apostle’s pattern of
exhortation elsewhere. For example, Paul did not sense that a believer’s absolute assurance that he is justified and eternally
saved by faith apart from works should lead to an indifference about good works (Ephesians 2:8–10).”39 Fair enough, should
is the way I would render Eph 2:10 as well: we should walk in good works—because of the unconditional benefits given to
us by the Lord. Gratitude is a powerful and proper motivation. Agreed. However, does this mean that believers necessarily
will walk the way they should? Of course not! The free gift of eternal life is absolutely free, but many believers will refuse to
walk in appropriate gratitude. Hodges justifiably acknowledges this reality elsewhere in his writings. So is Hodges only
saying that believers should be awake—nothing is at stake for them in this Thessalonian passage? Apparently this is Hodges’
intent in this Thessalonian context. But is such a conclusion truly representative of the Pauline pattern when his epistles are
interpreted holistically? Even within the epistle to the Ephesians, to which Hodges deferred us, Paul will go on to warn
believers to be awake morally because inheriting the kingdom is at stake: So be careful how you walk (Eph 5:3-15). They not
only should walk in good works, they must walk in good works if they are to inherit the kingdom. Hodges would concur with
this assessment. Gratitude for God’s unconditional gift is certainly a large part of the expected attitude within the Ephesian
epistle, but it is not the sole basis for the moral exhortations therein.

elsewhere, that Paul’s exhortations are normally based on more than an appeal to simple gratitude. Rewards are frequently at
stake in other epistles. Why not completely obliterate the primary objection raised by the majority objection by showing that
rewards are clearly at stake within this very passage, especially when exegetical factors strongly point to this conclusion?
Why weaken the exhortations to mere gratitude when the text offers so much more?
    Edgar attempts to defend the minority view by appealing to 1Jn 3:3 as proof that hope (as a confident expectation of the
Lord’s return) can indeed be motivational for moral purity.45 Even so, this minority response to the criticism made by the
majority view is woefully inadequate. To begin with, not even Hodges thinks this is what is meant by 1Jn 3:3. Hodges
weakens this defense of the minority view from this verse when he says: “No doubt His anticipated return can have strongly
positive spiritual effects. But this is not what John is likely to mean in this context” (emphasis his).46 Although there is a
measure of truth to Edgar’s defense, his appeal to 1Jn 3:3 fails to take into account that this verse does not necessarily teach
that the anticipation of the Lord’s return automatically has a temporally morally purifying effect on all believers in the
interim. Quite the contrary, Matthew and Luke show that some believers develop the opposite attitude. Since the Lord’s
coming is delayed, from their carnal perspective, some believers think that they can get away with irresponsible behavior
(Mt 24:48; 25:25-26; Lk 12:45; cp. Mt 25:5). Therefore, Edgar’s defense of the minority position by means of 1Jn 3:3 is not
sustainable.

passages. The salvation in 1Thess 5:8-9 must be won by faithfulness on the part of believers. I pose that this passage is,
therefore, describing misthological salvation—an aspect of salvation won as a reward. The rapture, in contrast, is a gift.

       In the first construct, hope and appearing are both united under one article (the) and joined by the conjunction (and)
and refer to the same thing (glory). In the second construct, God and Savior are both united under one article (the) and joined
by the conjunction (and) and refer to the same person (Jesus). Jesus is our God and Savior. According to many TNAN
advocates, we might also pose something similar to this for the first construct: Glory is our hope and His appearing. His
appearing is our hope. Because the phrase, “the blessed hope and glorious appearing,” contains two nouns in the same case,
introduced by a singular article, and joined by and (kai), the frequent presumption is that they refer to the same thing (EBC)
so that kai is epexegetical (Lenski and NAC). Jesus’ glorious appearing is our blessed hope.
       Contrary to many of my fellow pretribulationalists, I am skeptical that Paul intends to be understood as referring to the
blessed hope of His glorious appearing. Instead, given the broader context of his epistle to Titus and the versatility of the
Greek grammar, Paul is looking for the blessed hope of eternal life and His glorious appearing. Wuest and company have
oversimplified Granville Sharp’s rule so as to make this rule apply just as equally to the first TNAN construct as the latter
TNAN construct. This oversimplification is common and results in frequent errors. In an article dedicated to Sharp’s rule,
Wallace responds to Wuest’s article and explains the limitations that Wuest (and others) fail to mention:

             In other words, in the construction article-noun-καί-noun Sharp delineated four requirements that he felt
      needed to be met if the two nouns were necessarily to be seen as having the same referent:* Both nouns must be
      (1) personal (they must refer to a person, not a thing), (2) common epithets (not proper names), (3) in the
      same case,† and (4) singular in number.‡ The significance of these requirements can hardly be overestimated, for
      those who have misunderstood Sharp’s rule have done so almost without exception because they were unaware
      of the restrictions that Sharp set forth.76

   The second construct refers to a person—Jesus. So Sharp’s rule truly applies. But the first construct refers to a thing—
glory. So contrary to Wuest and a host of other interpreters who share the same premise, Sharp’s rule does not necessarily
apply, at least not equally, to the TNAN-1because it is impersonal. At the most basic level, a TNAN construction always
denotes some type of basic unity. At a midrange level, it may indicate equality. At the highest level it may connote identify.

faithful believers are golden trophies—royal trophies.* God’s trophy case has plenty of room for all His trophies, but some
trophies are more glorious than others.

if anything, about the NT use of the word hope for promised reward. A confident expectation need not necessarily be an

                                                    Conclusion
       Being destined for salvation in 1Thess 5:9 has nothing to do with being predestined in eternity past for salvation from
eternal damnation. Rather, God has determined that believers should be morally awake and thereby conditionally come into
possession of the full realization of the salvation He envisions for them in the form of millennial rulership. Unconditional
deliverance from tribulational wrath is enjoined with conditional salvation in the form of millennial rulership. Such
juxtapositions between free and costly aspects of a related event certainly have precedence in the Pauline writings. This
conclusion is derived by Paul’s insertion of a word before salvation that strongly suggests active participation on the part of
believers for the realization for such salvation, an insertion that is absent from before wrath, thus conveying the impression
that deliverance from tribulational wrath is unconditional for the believer but that the realization of misthological salvation is
very much conditional. Confirmation for this interpretation comes in the next verse, where believers are informed that as a
result of what God has proposed in 5:9, they will live with the Lord by virtue of His death even if they are morally asleep. In
a nutshell, as the ripples of 1Thess 5:8-10 are examined, one finds this same truth encapsulated in a variety of other passages.
       Salvation through sanctification in 2Thess 2:13 is thought by some interpreters to refer to unconditional deliverance
from the wrath of the tribulation as a result of positional or preconversional sanctification. Support for this understanding is
sought from the parallel passage in 1Thess 5:9-10, where believers are assured unconditionally that whether they are morally
awake or asleep, they will be delivered from the tribulation. Opponents of this view are quick to point out that Paul’s
exhortations to be awake morally would make little sense if Paul simply is assuring the Thessalonians that they will be
delivered from the tribulation irrespectively, even if they are morally asleep. In that case Paul would be making a nonsensical
exhortation: “Be morally awake because God will deliver you even if you are morally asleep.” Such objections can be
overcome easily, however, by a differentiation between unconditional tribulational deliverance and conditional millennial
salvation. Such salvation encompasses more than mere tribulational deliverance. As a result, 2Thess 2:13 is best understood
as dealing with progressive sanctification as a means to misthological co-glorification. The gift of unconditional tribulational
deliverance is followed contingently by the reward of conditional millennial rulership: Be morally awake because
misthological salvation is at stake! This word of warning to the sleepy is a word of encouragement to the watchful. For this
reason, Paul ends his exhortation on a positive note. Let us be encouraged in our watchfulness as a result of this study.

        Thinking spherically, in terms of Jn 3:16-18 and 5:24, for example, we may state correctly that those who never have
heard the gospel are in a state of unbelief and thus spiritual death. They are unbelievers. Once they hear the gospel and
disbelieve it, they pass into a state of disbelief. These unbelievers become disbelievers. Stated in these terms, disbelievers
cannot go back into a state of being classified as simple unbelievers. They are now unbelievers who have disbelieved. They
have passed into a smaller and more accountable sphere of unbelief that may be classified as disbelief. There is no going
back. Even if some disbelievers forget having heard the gospel and thus have no conscious recollection of having disbelieved
it, they will still be held accountable for having rejected the gospel in the Day of Judgment. Judgment will be more severe for
them for having rejected it, even if during the latter course of their lives they do not recall having done so.
        Similarly, if disbelievers reach a point in time where they believe the gospel, they move into the sphere of having
eternal life as a free gift. In terms of this result, they cannot go back into a state of soteriological disbelief or death. These
disbelievers are now irrevocably reclassified as believers. Even if later they were to apostatize, they will not come into
judgment as unregenerate disbelievers. Soteriologically, they still are believers. The one-way nature of these transition points
is depicted in the illustration by an increase in the size of the two exit-entry points and by the increased size of the goat and
sheep respectively. The second goat is too large to pass back through the exit-entry point to its left. Having exited the state of
being an unbeliever and entered the state of being a classified as a disbeliever, this second goat cannot go back to the state of
being classified as an unbeliever. The sheep, in turn, is too large to pass back through the exit-entry point to its left. Having
exited the state of disbelief and entered into the state of being classified as a believer, the sheep cannot go back to the state of
being classified as a disbeliever.
        The spherical passage into life is static in that one cannot lose this life or pass out of this sphere back into the sphere of
death. Ontologically and categorically, believers cannot pass out of the sphere of life back into the sphere of death.
Accordingly, one might say that being saved by His life is soteriological and static in this sense. He has saved us by placing
us in His sphere of life when He gave us eternal life. Our position in this sphere of life is static. Having placed us in the
sphere by giving us eternal life, the Lord no longer needs to do anything to keep us in this sphere. Statically, we are protected
by the power and life of God soteriologically in our position.
