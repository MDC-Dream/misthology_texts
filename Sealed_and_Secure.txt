                              Purpose Statement
       Both Mere Christianity and Moral Christianity and Fallen from
Grace but Not from Perfection are rather advanced and comprehensive
demonstrations that perseverance in faith cannot be made a condition for
reaching heaven without teaching salvation by works.
       Sealed and Secure is an intermediate-level demonstration that being
sealed by the Sprit is an unconditional promise to anyone who has ever
believed savingly in Christ for eternal life. Specifically, remaining sealed is
not conditioned on the believer persevering in the faith. In addition to
responding to specific counterarguments from conditionalists regarding the
unconditional permanent nature of the seal, a defense of the premise of
unconditional promises in general is supplied.

       Arminianism is a branch of conditional security which teaches that
God contingently predetermines that those who persevere will be chosen
(elected) for heaven.* Human perseverance is the condition for divine
election. The free will of the believer is emphasized. In Arminianism,
reaching heaven is conditioned on the believer’s perseverance. (Arminians
may be defined broadly at the popular level as those who believe that
regeneration actually might be lost.) The Arminian order of conditional
results is: perseverance, then predestination, then heaven. Arminianism is
summarized by the acronym PEARS:
• Prevenient Grace—means God’s preparatory grace by which He takes
    the prerequisite step of providing mankind with the pragmatic ability to
    search for Him and find Him in saving faith.
• Election on Condition—means that God conditionally has elected all
    mankind to salvation. However, God has only predestined that those
    who of their own free will respond in faith to this offer of salvation and
    persevere in this faith will be saved. Predestination is thus conditioned
    on persevering faith.
• Atonement for All—means that the atonement is available sufficiently
    for all mankind, and its efficaciousness is limited only by their response.
• Resistible Grace—means that God’s prevenient grace may be resisted
    so that it does not result in salvation.
• Salvation through Perseverance—means that God’s grace is not only
    resistible; it is also reversible. Believers may fall irremediably from the
    state of saving grace if they fail to persevere. Those who persevere are
    elected to salvation from eternal damnation as a result of persevering.
    Believers must persevere in order to be saved, in the end. This doctrine
    also is popularly known as NOSAS, which stands for not once saved

       Calminianism = Calvinism + Arminianism. The term Calminianism
was popularized by Norman Geisler in his endeavor to adopt a mediating
position between Calvinism (TULIP)* and Arminianism (PEARS).† His
Calminian approach basically takes the point of agreement between the two
systems regarding the soteriological‡ necessity of perseverance and replaces
the Arminian S of PEARS with a synthesis of the Calvinistic P from
TULIP.§ The end result is an abbreviated PS Calminian acronym.1
       Regardless of the preferred full acronym for Calminianism, final
salvation from hell comes through perseverance in all forms of
Calminianism (as well as in Calvinism and Arminianism). By definition,
then, Calminianism is a mediation between Calvinism and Arminianism
that, like its two predecessors, makes perseverance necessary for
salvation from eternal damnation. Since the present book does not make
perseverance necessary for salvation from eternal damnation, it should be
categorized separately from Calminianism. Contrary to Calminian claims
otherwise, in making perseverance descriptively necessary, Calminianism
also necessarily makes perseverance prescriptively necessary: both
exegetically and logically. The present book rejects any and all Calminian
attempts at harmonization in favor of a Marrowistic mediation which teaches
that perseverance is necessary for rewards in heaven rather than for entrance
into heaven itself.

       Conditional security is the conviction that once a person believes in
Christ for eternal life, that person is conditionally secure in Christ and
conditionally assured of reaching heaven. According to conditional
securitists, a believer must persevere in order to reach heaven. (Both
proveitism and forfeitism advocate conditional security.)

       Conditionalist refers to an advocate of conditional security—the
conviction that once a person believes in Christ for eternal life, that person is
conditionally secure in Christ and must persevere in order to reach heaven.

       Forfeitist refers to an advocate of conditional security who believes
that those who do not persevere forfeit (i.e., lose) the gift of eternal life.

       Free Grace Salvationists are those who insist that the biblical
requirement for salvation from eternal damnation is that one simply believes
in the Lord Jesus for eternal life. This understanding is exemplified by the
Grace Evangelical Society (GES) motto: Faith Alone in Christ Alone. From
their perspective, unconditional security is guaranteed upon the recognition
that the free gift of eternal life is offered freely to one who simply believes
in Jesus Christ for eternal life.

       Insecuritist refers to an advocate of conditional security.
Perseverance (in faith and/or faithfulness) is the postconversional condition
for reaching heaven according to insecuritists.

       Linear faith (£ ) is ongoing faith that takes place over a period of
time. Although linear aspect is represented frequently by a line (—) in Greek
grammars, herein it is represented as an arrow so that a line can be used to
represent a state in contradistinction to an ongoing action.

       Regeneration is the theological term used to describe the impartation
of eternal life as a free gift (to one’s spirit) which occurs when a person
believes in Christ for eternal life. Such a person is said to be born again (i.e.,
given spiritual life in the form of eternal life as a free gift).

       Saving faith is a theological term used to refer to the faith which
results in salvation from hell. For an unconditional securitist, such faith is
regarded as punctiliar (z). For the conditional securitist, such faith is
regarded as linear (£). In other words, securitists believe that initial faith is
sufficient for salvation from eternal damnation. Insecuritists, in contrast,
teach that initial faith is insufficient since it must be followed by
perseverance in faith in order to qualify for heaven.

      Securitist refers to an advocate of unconditional security—the
conviction that once a person believes in Christ for eternal life, that person is
unconditionally secure in Christ and is assured of unconditionally reaching
heaven.

       Soteriology is based on two biblical Greek words, soteria (salvation)
and logos (teaching) and correspondingly refers to biblical teaching about
salvation. Although the biblical concept of salvation includes many
dimensions other than deliverance from hell, it is this aspect of salvation to
which writers refer frequently when they use the word soteriology. Thus,
salvation from hell (or more specifically, from eternal damnation in the Lake
of Fire) will be referred to as soteriological salvation or eternal salvation. A
soteriological interpretation of a passage thus would treat the passage as
dealing with eternal salvation from the Lake of Fire (Rev 20:14-15).

       Unconditional security is the conviction that once a person believes
in Christ for eternal life, that person is unconditionally secure in Christ and
assured of unconditionally reaching heaven. Technically, this articulation is
superior to saying that a believer is unconditionally assured of reaching
heaven. A Calvinist might affirm the latter but could not affirm the former.

        Unconditional securitist is someone who believes that perseverance
is not necessary to enter heaven.

       Introduction
       The biblical affirmations surrounding the sealing of the believer in
Christ is a particularly strong affirmation of unconditional security.2 This
book will explore the arguments both for and against this understanding of
this biblical theme. Just how secure is the believer in Christ? The present
chapter will focus on texts related most directly to the believer’s sealing. The
next chapter will dig beneath the surface to consider whether implicit
conditionality might be found beneath the surface of the explicit security
found in these texts.

       Tense of Believe
       According to conditionalists,* one must continue to believe in order

unconditional protection to the one who has believed regardless of whether

immediate response herein will be on the tense of the verb (pisteuo) itself in
Eph 1:13, translated above as having believed. This tense is antithetical to
Ashby’s exegetical critique. The verb in question is an aorist participle, not a

that we are sealed as long as we believe (as in classical Arminianism), he

•   As often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the Lord’s

       As it is, the Arminian argument in Eph 1:13 puts words in Paul’s
mouth that are not there. Paul is not saying people are sealed as long as they
believe or as often as they believe. Instead, he clearly is affirming that the
person is sealed who has believed. Paul simply uses an aorist participle to
show that the condition of faith which he has in mind is a punctiliar response
which is contingently antecedent to being sealed. Such aoristic participles
normally are translated as some form of having, after, or when. For example,
akouo (the first verb in the verse) is also an aorist participle and is typically
translated as after listening (NAS) or after you heard (KJV, NKJ, DRA,
ERV, NJB, RWB, TNT, WEB). But it is also just as easily translated as
having heard (ASV, DBY, RSV, YLT) or when you heard (CSB, NET, NIB,

NIV, NRS). Likewise, although the second verb (pisteuo) most frequently is
translated as having believed, it be just as well could translated as after you
believed (KJV, RWB, WEB) or when you believed (CSB, NET, NLT).
       So let us not think that the translation of having believed is a perfect
tense denoting a faith in the past that continues to sustain a state of faith into
the present so that sealing is contingent on this lingering affect. Paul does
not use the perfect tense on this occasion. He is not referring to a state of
faith which necessarily continues into the present (z▬ ). Likewise, Lenski’s
comment that hearing and believing are ingressive aorists ({       ¨ ) makes no

sense. Paul is not referring to an act of faith which necessarily continues into
the present. Even Lenski has to admit: “The moment we come to faith the
sealing occurs.”4 Our sealing is not conditioned on our continuous believing
anymore than it is contingent upon our continuous hearing. Paul is not
saying that as long as or as often as we hear the gospel then we believe and
are sealed. Continuous hearing is not his point at all. He is simply using a
series of three aorist participles to give a sequential chain of punctiliar
events:
                    you heard → you believed → you were sealed

       Lenski acknowledges, “The aorist ‘were sealed’ fits one act of
sealing.”5 Paul, as even Lenski has to admit, is not saying that protracted
hearing or believing results in sealing. Since the sealing occurs
instantaneously the very moment we first believe, the sealing must be
conditioned on punctiliar faith (z), not on linear believing (£). In other
words, Paul does not say, “He who hears continuously and believes
continuously is sealed continuously.”
       Paul uses this aoristic chain to show his ordo salutis: hearing
precedes believing, and believing precedes sealing.* Paul, therefore, is
assuring: “When you heard and subsequently believed, you were
consequently sealed.” The sealing is the consequence of a (z) punctiliar
response of faith. Although these events are temporally simultaneous, they
are logically sequential.

       Linear Classification
        Objections are easy to anticipate: How can our aoristic sealing have a
lingering effect if our aoristic faith does not have a lingering effect? How
can I stress the resulting state of the benefit when I have rejected the
resulting state of the contingency? How can securitists claim that the aoristic
sealing results in a ongoing state when they deny that the aoristic faith upon
which it was conditioned results in a lingering state?
        Such objections misunderstand the comments above about the aoristic
nature of sealing faith. The faith which results in one being sealed does not
linger from the standpoint of contingency, but such faith necessarily must be
linear in terms of the resultant seal. That punctiliar (z) act of faith lingers on
in the resultant state (▬) of being sealed. In terms of lingering result, one
remains a believer even if one ceases to believe.* One may still be labeled a
believer in terms of the result that came about from that completed action.
Therefore, the Arminian objection that there is no such thing as sealed
unbelievers begs the question as to whether the term believer is being used
in terms of result or requirement.† The aoristic sealing used to describe the
believer as sealed refers to the result rather than the requirement.
        With that clarification in mind, securitists themselves may well
respond to conditionalists by remarking, “Of course there is no such thing as
a sealed unbeliever.” God does not seal unbelievers. The Lord does not seal
a person until that person becomes a believer. At that point in time, the Lord
then marks that person as a believer with His seal. From that point forward,
that person is a marked man. From the perspective of that mark, the person

       Guarantee
        Whether the believing is impermanent, the sealing is permanent. How
do we know this? Well, in addition to the sense of the tense and the absence
of such words such as chronos and hosakis (in Eph 1:13) to imply linear

to keep it. The giver of the down payment loses the down payment to the
recipient of the transaction if the giver of the down payment fails to
complete the transaction. In this case, God loses the Holy Spirit to us if He
does not make good on His promise to complete our redemption on the
specified day. God is the one making the promise in this context; we are not
the ones making the promise.

we make co-payments. Such a transaction does not picture a gift, but a

       Non-returnable
        Still, the Arminian immediately will respond that the believer might
return the down payment. According to the Bible, however, God’s gifts are
not so easily returned. In fact, they are non-returnable. God will not repent
of the gifts He has given us (Rom 11:29). He will neither take them away
from us nor allow us to return them. We cannot return our justification to
God because God would have to unjustify us. God is our justifier
(Rom 3:26; 11:33), not our unjustifier; hence, we cannot become unjustified.
Nor can we unsave ourselves because to do so we would have to get God to
unsave us and thereby cause Him to go back on His promise to give us these
gifts freely and irrevocably. To return the down payment to the owner, the
owner would have to receive it. To receive it, He must take it. To take it, He
must revoke on His promise that the reception and retention of the down
payment were conditioned only on our initial acceptance. The down
payment is ours to keep, not ours to return.

       Dualistic Protection
        Peter apparently is affirming unconditional security by means of

misconstrued as conditional promises to guard that aspect of our lives/souls
which He has immutably guaranteed as His possession. We remain His child
as a result of trusting Him for eternal life even if we fail to entrust Him with
our daily lives.

       Power versus Ability
       At the ontological level, God has given us eternal life by imparting it
to our spirits. Not even God has the power or ability to snuff out eternal life.
Eternal life is God’s kind of life. As such it cannot be snuffed out because
God cannot die.23 God cannot take His own life, so how can He take our life
when He has given us the same kind of life that He has? God cannot kill us
or Himself. God’s theoretical ability to kill Himself or us spiritually is not a
function of power. As Murphree explains, “Even infinite power cannot do
what cannot be done….infinite power does not entail infinite ability.”24
There are logical, moral, and self-imposed limitations on God’s ability. God
does not have the ability to kill Himself. Suicide is not an option for God.
Pertaining to the gift of eternal life that God has given us, homicide is not a
divine option either. Even if God had the power to kill us by taking eternal
life away from us, He does not have the ability to do so because it would run
counter to His own immutability. Our experience of that life may fluctuate
temporally and misthologically. Even God’s experience of eternal life is not
impassable; nevertheless, His possession of life is immutable. In the same
Page 26                                                     Sealed and Secure
way, our soteriological possession of eternal life is grounded unflinchingly
in the Lord’s unconditional possession of us. What gives an Arminian the
right to teach that a believer who irrevocably has been given God’s kind of
life can die in the sense of losing the gift of eternal life that has been
imparted to him or her by a God who cannot lie or die? Eternal life is not
only life with God, it is the life of God. Having the latter guarantees the
former.

       Transaction versus Realization
        As deduced elsewhere, the nominal and verbal forms of peripoiesis
mean to gain and keep as a possession by paying the necessary purchase
price, thus to achieve and win by much effort. Sometimes it is the Lord who
puts forth the effort and who pays the price, even to the extent of laying
down His life and shedding His blood for the sheep. In such cases, He is the
active agent who has purchased us for Himself as His own possession. He is
the purchaser, we are the possession. Not only are we the passive possession
in such cases, it must be noted that the purchase has been completed; we are
the purchased possession. The transaction has been consummated. No
further price waits to be paid. We have been marked as paid in full. Paul
uses the aorist in Acts 20:28 in harmony with the completed nature of the
transaction. The action is punctiliar: He purchased (z) us with His blood.
        Those translations that bring out the completed nature of the
transaction are to be commended. The full price of our redemption already
has been paid for by the blood of Christ. That payment was made for us, not
to us. The Holy Spirit, in contrast, is given to us. To be sure, an overlapping
of the word payment occurs in both cases. But the blood of Christ by which
we were redeemed was paid to God’s justice. The Spirit, by whom we are
sealed, was given to us (as a down payment), not to God’s justice.
        Although we still wait for the unconditional realization of the
transition, the transaction has been completed. The manner in which
peripoiesis is used in such cases (Acts 20:28; Eph 1:14; 1Pet 2:9) logically
conveys unconditional security. It is a done deal.
        At other times, however, we are the active agent. We are the ones
making the purchase. By paying the necessary purchase price, in order to
achieve and win the benefit by much effort, we are currently in the process
of purchasing the misthological salvation of our souls. These misthological
transactions (Lk 17:33 CT; 1Thess 5:9; 2Thess 2:14; 1Tim 3:13; Heb 10:39)
are not complete. And in them we are not passive. As a result of them, we
will be the owners rather than what is owned. In failing to give due
consideration to the completed versus uncompleted nature of the purchase,
to the divine versus human agents making the purchase, to the agent versus
the object of the purchase, and to the means of the purchase (as to whether it
is by Christ’s blood versus our service), it is not surprising that
conditionalists have failed to discern the unconditional versus conditional

nature of the purchase. One can only derive conditional security from such
texts by ignoring a list of details within the texts. The security is in the
details. Too bad Corner did not take time to examine the details in his

       Sealed or Saved
        Corner asks, “Does the Bible say Christians are sealed unto the day of
redemption or saved unto the day of redemption (Eph 4:30)?” (Emphasis
his.)25 Fair enough. Securitists have a couple of questions of their own.
(1) Does the Bible say Christians are sealed unto the day of redemption or
probated unto the day of redemption (Eph 4:30)? (2) Does the Bible say
Christians are sealed unto the day of redemption or sealed until the day of
redemption (Eph 4:30)?
        With his question, Corner evidently hopes to interject a dichotomy
between being sealed and being saved. But does he provide any examples
where the unsaved are sealed or the sealed are unsaved? No. Does not the
fact that they are sealed mean that they are saved? Yes, soterically. Does that
mean that securitists are speaking equatively? Not necessarily, but it does
mean that in failing to deduce the semantic overlap between the sealed and
saved symmetry, Corner has implied a false dichotomy. To speak
substantively of being sealed (as the Bible does in Rev 7:4), the sealed are
saved, and the saved are sealed. At the very least, this is the case in the
Pauline passages, which are the passages in dispute. So what does Corner
hope to accomplish with his question? In terms of security, there is no
substantial difference between having been sealed with the Spirit in Eph
1:13 and having been saved by grace in the immediate context, fourteen
verses later (Eph 2:5,8).
        If anything, in examining the broader biblical spectrum, being sealed
is a stronger affirmation of security than is being saved. Misthologists are
well aware of cases where being saved by God is linearly contingent upon
the performance of the person who is saved. Both sanctification and co-
glorification salvation are contingent upon the performance of the person
who is already soteriologically saved. However, we know of no cases where
being sealed by God is linearly contingent upon the action of the one who is
sealed. One may take the mark of God or the mark of the beast, but one
cannot take both. Nor can one take one and then the other. One may not take
the mark of God and the mark of the beast simultaneously or
interchangeably. Both God and the beast use a permanent marker.
        Cases where seals are broken do exist. God’s seals on the scroll in
Revelation were broken at God’s appropriate time. Nonetheless, the seals
were not broken because of some defect in the scroll’s performance. The
breaking of the seals was not due to an unfilled linear contingency on the
part of the scroll. Nor was the scroll able to break its own seals. Far to the
Page 28                                                       Sealed and Secure
contrary, the cry goes out: “Who is worthy to open the book and to break its
seals?” (Rev 5:2)
        Yet what if the Arminian, in hopes of finding some example of an
object becoming unsealed, wishes to ignore such details and still insists that
seals may be broken? Then let it be noted that even the scrolls were not
unsealed in the sense of having the seal removed. These seals were broken
rather than removed. The wax signet still would have remained on the scroll.
The broken wax seal was not scrapped off the scroll. Does Corner think the
Holy Spirit can be broken? Let us hope not. However, even if he does want
to appeal to this broken imagery, it should be noted that the broken seal
remains on the object that receives the seal. Likewise, the Holy Spirit would
still remain with the believer. This truth is not only conveyed by the imagery
but also by the fact that Paul affirms that this seal is given as a down
payment to the object that is sealed. To be sure, the sealed object belongs to
God as God’s possession. However, the seal itself belongs to the object. The

       God’s Glory
       Paul concludes Eph 1:14 by saying that this guaranteed redemption is
to the praise of His glory (doxa). Our redemption is not to the praise of the
glory of our perseverance but to the praise of the glory of His preservation.
Contrary to Calminianism, our perseverance cannot be a condition for the
retention of our redemption; otherwise, we would get to share the glory for
our redemption. The exclusive nature of this doxology rules out our active
cooperation in retaining this redemption. Sure, we may work in coming to
saving faith, but the punctiliar response of faith itself has no meritorious
value and is not a work. Moreover, our salvation is not conditioned
theologically on preconversional works.

faith does have meritorious value and can result in our praise (1Pet 1:7).

Sealing is based on our initial faith, not our persevering in faith. Otherwise,
our sealing would be conditioned on our merit and result in our praise. Our
possession of life was, and remains, conditioned on our singular punctiliar
passive response of saving faith so that God’s grace solely may be glorified
in that our redemption was, and is, not conditioned on anything meritorious

       Jonah
        Jonah was given a message by God: “Yet forty days and Nineveh will

wanted to see Nineveh destroyed.

      How then are we to follow the example of Abraham and Jonah in
reconciling the fact that God can change His mind concerning promises He
has made and yet not be considered a liar? How can we harmonize the fact
that God does indeed change His mind when elsewhere we are told, “God is
not a man, that he should lie, nor a son of man, that he should change his
mind. Does he speak and then not act? Does he promise and not fulfill?”
(Num 23:19; NIV) Corner believes God’s promises are conditional. This text

seems to say otherwise. Yet, I cannot stop here and claim victory over
Corner’s argument because there is more to this story than meets the eye.
The affirmation that God does not lie or change His mind is well known
(1Sam 15:29). At the same time, multiple affirmations show that God, does
in fact, change His mind (Ex 32:14; Jonah 3:10).
        Men not only change their mind amorally but also immorally. God
never changes His mind immorally. Thus, one might respond analogously
that even among men it is recognized that there are matters about which a
man may change his mind without being considered a liar. The same is true
of God. He does not change His mind regarding matters that would make
Him a liar (Heb 6:18). Still, analogies can only be pressed so far. The
analogy of comparing God to men breaks down since the text explicitly
says: “God is not a man…that He should change his mind.” I take this to
mean that God is not a man that He could change His mind in such a manner
that would make Him a liar. He never changes His mind in a manner that
would be inconsistent with the justice and grace of His own nature. Since
man can lie, man is incapable of making an immutable promise. Conversely,
it must be acknowledged that God is capable of making immutable
promises. As a matter of fact, that is the only kind of promise He can make
(1Kings 8:56; 2Cor 1:20). Eternal life, in fact, is a promise (2Tim 1:1). Paul
clearly regards this promise of eternal life as immutable since it was
promised by “God who cannot lie” (Tit 1:2). It is at this juncture that I spot
weaknesses, major ones, in Corner’s argument.

His mind. Inexcusably, however, he fails to consider the texts on the other
side of the discussion which indicate that God cannot change His mind.
Second, this failure in Corner’s treatment leads to a failure in his logic in
that he misses the logical corollary of a balanced deliberation that considers
text on both sides of the equation. Logically, the fact that God can change
His mind about some promises (because they may be dependent on
unspoken contingencies) does not necessarily mean that God can change His
mind regarding every promise. Not every promise is dependent upon such
subsequent contingencies. Just ask Abraham.

the object lesson that God provided Abraham by which Abraham could rest

       The backbone of Corner’s defense for viewing all of God’s promises
as implicitly conditional is found in his citing classic texts which reveal God
changing His mind. The text that Corner uses to introduce his section
entitled God’s Promises are Conditional is Jer 18:7-10. In Haley’s book,
Alleged Discrepancies of the Bible, after supplying a two-column grouping
of texts which appear to be at odds with one another in asserting that God is
changeable versus unchangeable, Haley likewise uses Jer 18:7-10 to
commence the premise of his solution:

              Here is brought clearly to view the underlying condition,
      which, if not expressed, is implied, in God’s promises and
      threats. Whenever God, in consequence of a change in
      character in certain persons, does not execute the threats or
      fulfill the promises he made to them, the explanation is
      obvious. In every such case, the change is in man, rather than in
      God” (italics his; bold mine).49

       Very well, Corner, Haley, and I are all in agreement that a passage
such as this brings out the implicit underlying contingency that may exist in
God’s promises. Whenever we see God change His mind regarding a
positive or negative condition, we can be certain that there was an
underlying condition (at least for the individual realization). The problem is
that Corner has sought to press this limited concurrence to mean that there is
underlying contingency in all of God’s promises.
       Once again, I will not dismiss Corner’s argument just because of this
mistake in his logic. In Corner’s defense, I will acknowledge that by my best
estimation it seems that Haley is of the same opinion as Corner in perceiving
universal conditionality underlying all of God’s promises. But unlike
Corner, Haley supplies two columns pertaining to the discussion of such
texts: one side dealing with God’s immutability, the other with His
mutability. Haley explains that precisely because God is immutable
(unchangeable) in His character, He must be mutable (changeable) in His
dealings with men and His emotions toward them. Splendid, I fully agree.
Further, Haley makes a most important observation that is not mentioned by
Corner: “Some of his [God’s] declarations are absolute and unconditional;
the greater part, however, including promises and threatenings, turn upon
conditions either expressed or implied.”50
       Apparently, Haley makes a distinction between God’s declarations
and promises and thinks that God’s promises are always conditional, at least
by implication. If I could but affirm from Haley’s discussion that he believes
Chapter 2. Conditional and Unconditional Promises                     Page 51
that God’s immutable character causes some of His promises to be
unconditional and some to be conditional, then I would be in full accord
with Haley’s discussion of immutability. Indeed, internal consistency within
Haley’s discussion would seem to require that he agree that this is indeed the
case. And we have seen passages above which link God’s promises with His
immutability.
       Unfortunately, I must hesitate from citing Haley as being in full
agreement with my position in that some of Haley’s subsequent comments
lead me to suspect that he believes God is mutable in all His dealings with
men. Even his internal discussion within the section of immutability gives
the impression that Haley believes that a change in man requires a
corresponding change in how God deals with man. Correspondingly, in
Haley’s estimation, if a believer actually ceased to be a believer, he would
lose eternal life.51 So as it is, I must cite Haley as being in agreement with
Corner’s thesis, even though to Haley’s credit he digs deeper than Corner in
that Haley acknowledges that the conditionality is rooted in God’s
mutability and even deeper still in recognizing that God’s mutability is
founded upon God’s immutability. Surprisingly, Haley evidently fails to
deduce that since some of God’s declarations are unconditional, some of
God’s promises (such as the promised gift of life) are unconditional as well.
From his own analysis, Haley should have deduced that some of God’s
promises are unconditional while most are conditional. His failure to do so
causes me to regard him as a Calvinistic advocate of conditional security.
       I, therefore, call Haley to the witness stand as a hostile witness in my
defense of unconditional security. In my cross-examination of Haley’s
Calvinistic concurrence with Corner’s conditional security, I find that Haley
has gathered evidence regarding God’s immutability that may serve as a
foundation from which to offer my objection to the Corner-Haley opinion
that 1Sam 2:30 proves that any time there is a subsequent change in the man
who has received the promise then there may no longer be an applicability of
the promise.52 They seek to turn a limited truth into a universal truth and in
doing so have overstepped the boundaries of the text.
       It comes as no surprise when Calvinists join their fellow conditional
securitists in the Arminian camp in affirming conditional security from a text
such as 1Sam 2:30: “Therefore the Lord God of Israel declares, ‘I did indeed
say that your house and the house of your father should walk before Me
forever’; but now the Lord declares, ‘Far be it from Me—for those who
honor Me I will honor, and those who despise Me will be lightly esteemed.’”
In commenting on this verse, Smith asks, “Can then a promise of God be
withdrawn?” His answer is an unqualified, “Yes, assuredly.” Honoring God
is “one of these conditions essential on man’s part to secure the fulfillment
of God’s promises.”53 In short, if you want security, you will have to secure
it by honoring God. One would deduce from such unqualified statements
that to secure the fulfillment of God’s promises regarding eternal life one
Page 52                                                      Sealed and Secure
must honor God throughout one’s life. This conditional security is the joint
premise of both Arminianism and Calvinism.

       OSAS Position
       The OSAS thesis, in contrast, is that some of God’s immutable
declarations are tied irreversibly to His character so as to rule out the
possibility of linear contingency. To be sure, God, who is faithful and just
(Dt 15:4), may nevertheless spurn Israel whom He has begotten as a son
(Dt 32:18-19). Also, God may divorce Israel whom He has married as a wife
(Jer 3:8). Further OSAS may concede that these texts typologically reveal
how God may deal with present-day believers. Granted, from the joint
conditional perspective of both Arminianism and Calminianism, a text like
2Tim 2:13, which is addressed to NT believers, is taken to mean that “if we
are faithless, then God remains faithful” to His promises to punish us with
soteriological severity (cf. Hendriksen, NTC). In complete contrast, OSAS
believes that 2Tim 2:13 affirms unconditional soteriological security: Even
if we are not faithful in keeping His word, God is faithful to keep His word
by keeping us.
       Even if OSAS were to agree with soteriological conditionalists and
conclude that 2Tim 2:13 means that God is faithful to judge us, OSAS
would respond that this judgment is only misthological in scope. God will
judge unfaithful believers at the Judgment Seat of Christ (JSC). God’s
despising us as His sons, divorcing us as His wife, or being faithful to His
promises to punish us when we deny Him are all, at the most, pictures of
conditional security applied exclusively at the misthological level. Not all
sons will inherit the kingdom. Not all those betrothed to the Lord will be
found worthy to become His Bride.
       At the individual and misthological level, those who bed with the
devil will be given a certificate of divorce so that they do not become the
Bride of Christ. (A divorce was necessary to break a Jewish betrothal.) Paul
is not afraid of invisible monsters under the bed. These misthological
monsters are real, and they are visible for all who have eyes to see:
      2
        For I am jealous for you with a godly jealousy; for I betrothed you to
      one husband, that to Christ I might present you as a pure virgin. 3 But
      I am afraid, lest as the serpent deceived Eve by his craftiness, your
      minds should be led astray from the simplicity and purity of devotion
      to Christ. (2Cor 11:2-3)

       Paul is afraid, and rightly so, that he might not be able to present
these Corinthian believers as pure virgins to the Lord at the JSC. Likewise,
in dealing with the believers at Galatia, he expresses similar fears: “I fear for
Chapter 2. Conditional and Unconditional Promises                       Page 53
you, that perhaps I have labored over you in vain” (Gal 4:11). He does not
question their regeneration,* but he does question whether they will qualify
for the reward of the bridal inheritance. To inherit the kingdom as the Bride
of Christ (i.e., as a co-ruler with Christ), one must be a faithful believer in
Christ. Being loved as the Bride of Christ necessarily requires faithfulness
to Christ.
       At the soteriological level, however, the Lord still loves us and we
remain His own even if we are unfaithful. One reason OSAS affirms that we
remain His own is because of the Lord’s promise and prayer to
unconditionally keep us (Jn 10:28-29; 17:11). But such matters aside, for the
purpose of the present discussion, the focus will be on the classic problem
posed by Haley between God’s immutability and mutability and the impact
this has on Corner’s premise of universal changeability regarding promises.

       Errors by Unconditionalists
       In defending eternal security, Geisler devotes a paragraph to
defending the premise that “salvation is an unconditional promise” and a
joint paragraph to affirming that “salvation is an irrevocable gift.” To his
credit, unlike the conditional securitists in the Calvinistic and Arminian
camps, Geisler cites 2Tim 2:13 in defense: “God can never take back the gift
of salvation. He is bound by his own unconditional promise to be faithful,
even if we are faithless, for he cannot deny himself (2Tim. 2:13).”55 I also
note appreciatively that in an expanded discussion Geisler affirms that God’s
promise of the gift of eternal life is an irrevocable, unconditional promise
and clearly grounds this affirmation in God’s immutability: “When he makes
an unconditional promise he never fails to keep it (cf. Gen 12:1-3; Heb 6:16-
18)….The only thing that makes God morally bound to keep his word is his
unchangeable nature.”56 Truly, God’s immutability makes His gifts
unconditionally irrevocable.
       Unfortunately, however, just as Corner overstates the case for
unconditional security by assuming that all God’s promises are conditional
since some of His promises are conditional, Geisler mistakenly seems to

overstate the case for eternal security in assuming that just because God
makes an immutable promise, the promise must be unconditional.
        For example, Geisler wrongly cites Heb 6:17-18 as an example of an
unconditional promise of eternal security: “In the same way God, desiring
even more to show to the heirs of the promise the unchangeableness of His
purpose, interposed with an oath, in order that by two unchangeable things,
in which it is impossible for God to lie, we may have strong encouragement,
we who have fled for refuge in laying hold of the hope set before us.” But
this text is only an irrevocable promise to believers who meet the condition
of ongoing faith and faithfulness.
        Rather than being a soteriological promise, as Geisler mistakenly
assumes, this text is a misthological promise. Lest it be thought that I am
being too hard on my fellow securitists, I will point out that even some of
my fellow misthologists, such as Dillow, have fallen into the trap of taking
the same short cut in defending eternal security by soteriologically appealing
to this text from Hebrews.57 However, this misthological promise does not
apply unconditionally to believers until they have completed the course of
their lives and have been found worthy of the reward. This text, like
Heb 10:36, is saying that believers must endure in order to receive what was
promised: “For you have need of endurance, so that when you have done the
will of God, you may receive what was promised.” The question is, “What
was promised—a gift or a reward?” A study of the word promise in the book
of Hebrews readily confirms that the promise of a reward is in view. This is
not to say, as will be seen below, that a text like Heb 6:17-18 has no value in
affirming unconditional security, only one cannot take a short cut in exegesis
and make a jump in logic to use this verse directly to support this position.
Misthology has no need of missing links in its argument.
        Similarly, Peter’s affirmation that a believer’s inheritance is
“imperishable and undefiled and will not fade away, reserved in heaven for
you” (1Pet 1:4) is yet another text to which some of my well-meaning fellow

unconditional security: “It is God who has reserved it for us. Did He make a
mistake in reserving it for us?”58 Of course not. God has not made a mistake

just because a potential reward is eternal that it must be unconditional in its
final realization. The reward is eternal; the reservation is not.59 Both this text

       Rewards are Conditional
        The problem, then, is that certain securitists and misthologists have
failed to perceive that some of the texts that they are citing in defense of
unconditional security are actually dealing with the conditional nature of
eternal rewards. By pointing out the conditional nature of rewards in such
texts, conditional securitists are easily able to offset these mistaken appeals
by unconditional securitists. Nevertheless, in failing to recognize that such
passages are talking about the conditional nature of rewards, unconditional
securitists are making an even more grievous error in that they condition
one’s salvation from hell on one’s works.
        Corner has failed to note that the texts he poses in defense of
underlying conditionality only confirm the conditional nature of rewards.
God’s intention was that the realization of the perpetual priesthood should be
an eternal reward for their conduct (Num 25:13; cp. Ex 29:9). God clarifies
in 1Sam 2:30, however, that the realization of this reward is contingent on
ongoing faithfulness in that He would honor those who honor Him and
despise those who despise Him. Undoubtedly, the language of rewards
rather than that of a gift is being implemented. Being honored by God is
conditioned on serving God and successfully enduring trials (Jn 12:26;
1Pet 1:7). Obviously, a reward motif based on service is in view since
rewards are conditioned on works. In conditioning the gift of eternal life on
the same thing as a reward, Corner is attempting to condition the gift on
works and thereby turn the gift into a reward.

       Missing Links
       The fact that a reward may be contingent on linear contingency is not
exactly ground-breaking news. It does not take a rocket scientist to figure
out that rewards are earned. Lay people can figure that out, even if some
scholars cannot. On the other hand, it would take a long chain of evolution
in one’s theological thought process to successfully turn (A) the
imperishable gift of eternal life (which a believer already possesses) into
(B) a reward (which the believer must struggle to keep and endure to
obtain). Too many missing links and categorical gaps exist in Corner’s
soteriological phylogeny to think that he has successfully turned a gift into a
reward.
       We do not earn a gift. Just because a gift and a reward share the
similar characteristic of being a promise does not mean that they can be dug
out of the text and arranged in a theological, evolutionary tree so as to link
one inseparably to the other with a line of linear contingency. A gift is a
different kind of theological animal than is a reward. They cannot be mated
Chapter 2. Conditional and Unconditional Promises                       Page 57
to produce the hybrid that Corner has imaged: a linear contingent gift.
Securitists, therefore, may liken Corner’s futile exegetical attempts to turn a
gift into a reward (with a biased collection of certain texts) to the failed
efforts of evolutionists to make a monkey out of man by appealing to the
fossil record.
        Just as The Fossils Still Say NO!, to quote the subtitle of Gish’s
classic rebuttal of evolution, a systematic examination of the biblical texts
still responds unyieldingly to Corner’s endeavor to classify all of God’s
promises as being conditioned on ongoing performance. By picturing God as
giving man the promise of eternal life as a gift and then taking it back
because of man’s performance, Corner is making God out to be a liar.
Placing the conditional label upon such promises turns out to be libel.

       Surely Live
       What about the fact that God promises the righteous that they will
surely live and the unrighteous that they will surely die and then clarifies that
this promise is to be understood conditionally (Eze 18:13-14)? Do such texts
(which Corner digs up from only one side of the theological column to put
on display as examples of seemingly unconditional promises which are
really conditional) provide the missing links Corner needs to prove that all
of God’s promises are in reality conditional? Not at all.
       These seemingly-unconditional-but-really-conditional promises are
not unconditional promises of a gift that were subsequently turned into a
conditional promise of reward through a process of theological
macroevolution over a long period of prophetic time. These texts, like
1Sam 2:30, were promises of reward all along, which when traced through
their development in Scripture and examined under the lens of progressive
revelation are seen to be mere examples of misthological microevolution in
which their underlying contingency becomes more pronounced in some
passages than others.
       Evolutionists are not the only ones to confuse microevolution with
macroevolution. Conditional securitists do so also. Corner has seen the
microevolution in which the misthological nature of some linear conditional
promises is more clearly brought out subsequently, and he, consequently,
has concluded that one may assume macroevolution in which all promises
are regarded as evolving from the same misthological nature. As to passages
such as Eze 33:13, the first thing to note is that God’s promise that the
righteous will surely live is not to be misconstrued to mean that they will
unconditionally live, as God Himself clarifies in the passage itself.
       Second, the passage is not talking about either imputational
righteousness or soteriological life. Rather, practical righteousness and the
reward of temporal righteousness are in view. The passage begins with
repeated warnings of the sword taking their lives if they are wicked. Yet
those who warn the wicked of their impending deaths will save their own
Page 58                                                       Sealed and Secure
lives (literally, save their souls, Eze 33:9). But this terminology (i.e., the
saving of a soul) is always used temporally or misthologically in the Bible,
never soteriologically.
        Those who do not warn the wicked will, like the wicked, die. The
people complain that they are rotting away (obviously rotting on earth not in
hell) and are fearful that they will not live (Eze 33:10). Again, they are
concerned about being killed, about staying alive on planet Earth, in a dog-
eat-dog world where it often seems that only the fittest survive. Theological
speculation about ontological extinction is the last thing on their minds.
They are not trying to stay alive on the branch of some imaginary
evolutional soteric tree which is trying to turn their gift of life into a reward.
They simply are trying to survive, not only individually but corporately, as
the house of Israel (Eze 33:11). The survival of the house of Israel is also a
concern in the background passage (Eze 18:31), where the issue of life and
death is clarified as the wicked person being put to death as a form of capital
punishment rather than soteriological demise.
        Third, God has several reasons to be keenly interested in the survival
of Israel as a nation. The Lord has too many unfulfilled, unconditional
promises invested in the nation of Israel to allow it to slip away quietly into
the night never to be heard from again within the pages of history. The OT is
a record of Jewish history. At the time in which Ezekiel was writing, the
promised Messiah was yet to come from them and for them. And the
restoration of the Davidic kingdom still awaits a future time. The miraculous
restoration of the nation of Israel in our time is an event unparalleled in
history. This modern miracle stands as a testimony to the fact that God
knows how to keep a promise, even when that promise is made to a people
who are faithless. The unconditional nature of God’s promise to the nation
of Israel is further seen in that the nation of Israel was no longer intact in
order to meet any linear contingency before being restored as a nation. As to
OT typology, unconditional security is pictured in how God treats Israel.
Amidst the misthological insecurity, pictures of unconditional security are
linked to God’s immutability (Mal 3:6).

of life was made to the righteous who stays righteous that the precedence is
established, at least in principle, that the believer must remain a believer in
order to continue to have life as a believer. After all, Israel is an OT picture
of a born-again believer. In other words, Arminians will agree that God
promises eternal life to a believer, but they will then stipulate that in order to
keep eternal life one must remain a believer. Supposedly, the promises no
longer apply if one ceases to be a believer. The above passages from Ezekiel
might be sought for typological support:

31:23). Having already examined and refuted this type of argument from
multiple vantage points within the course of this present discussion and
elsewhere, it will suffice here to mention briefly a few objections to augment
the present defense of unconditional security, which regards it as
inconsistent to think that one must remain a believer in order to retain the
free gift of eternal life.

       Believer ≠ Ongoing Believer
        First, linear contingency necessarily implies, if not outright demands,
that the benefit bestowed be regarded as a reward rather than a gift. Thus, to
base the possession of the gift of eternal life on linear contingency is to turn
this gift into a reward.* To apply a misthological syllogism indiscriminately
to a gift can be like trying to put your right foot into your left shoe. It is not a
natural selection. Second, the context in Ezekiel undoubtedly is talking
about the reward of life. The NT is clear that the gift of life is not
conditioned on works and thus not a reward. The typology is in disparity.
Third, just because some believers no longer believe does not mean that they
are no longer considered believers. They may remain classified as believers

your hamburger does not mean that God must treat you differently. An
amoral human action would not necessarily invoke a divine reaction. For
that matter, one need not jump to the conclusion that each immoral action by
man results in a negative reaction by God. For one thing, when man sinned,
God atoned. This was a very positive reaction by God to a very negative
action by man. Rather than merely responding as man’s condemner, God
responded as man’s redeemer. Secondly, in God’s positive reaction, God has
taken counteraction to make it possible for Him not to respond negatively to
man as a sinner just because man sins. By a declaration of justification, the
Lord has found a permanent solution to sin in which He has, as expressed in
the song Glorious Day, “justified freely forever” those who ever believe (cf.
Heb 10:10,14). Need we be reminded that this justification is a gift (Rom
3:24)? This type of permanency rules out linear contingency since the
benefit already has been bestowed freely and eternally. God has “perfected
for all time” those who have at any time been justified freely forever.*
        In order to convey the permanency of regeneration, Peter stresses that
it is sourced in the immutability (imperishability) of God’s word (1Pet 1:23).
In doing so, Peter ontologically links the immutability of God with the new
nature of man in such a way as to require that one acknowledge that man’s
new nature is ontologically immutable so that regeneration is irrevocable. It
is not necessary that God withdraw eternal life from us each time we sin.

          Impossible
       God cannot change His dealings with believers as believers because
they cannot become unregenerate or unjustified. Believers may cease to
believe functionally, but they cannot cease legally and ontologically to be
considered believers in terms of the benefits of their justification and
regeneration which were conveyed irrevocably upon them when they first
believed. Stated more concisely, believers may no longer be believers
functionally, but they certainly are believers legally and ontologically. Just
as one who has committed murder and who has been sentenced to death does
not have to continue to commit murder in order to be considered a murderer
or to remain under the sentence of death, so believers do not functionally
have to continue to believe in order to be considered believers or to remain
in possession of eternal life. Just as it is impossible for God to lie, it is
impossible for believers to die (Jn 11:26). Filling in the blank with the word
impossible is certainly possible.* No philosophical reason prevents the blank
from being filled in differently, depending on various biblical conditions.
The question expressed by the blank, in fact, poses a false trichotomy
between the possibilities.
       Sometimes God may or may not choose to respond differently to man
when man sins. It is not necessary that the Lord discipline His children for
everything they do wrong. (For example, it is not necessary that a mother
discipline her little girl each time her daughter does something wrong. Love
makes it possible, at times, to overlook the offense of another.) On the other
hand, sometimes God necessarily may have to respond differently to man
when man sins. There are some things that God in His righteousness cannot
overlook. (Likewise, there are things that even a loving mother simply
cannot overlook.) Then again, sometimes God cannot respond differently to
man when man sins. God will still regard His child as His child. (A loving
mother is still going to regard her sinful daughter as her daughter.)

undisturbed by the change in surface conditions. At those depths, God could
not love us more, and He will not love us less.

to positive specimens. There are also examples of God reaching a point
where He says, in effect, “Enough is enough.” Moses flatly stated as much:
“The Lord was angry with me on your account, and would not listen to me;
and the Lord said to me, 'Enough! Speak to Me no more of this matter’”

not enter His rest (Heb 3:11; 4:3), the Lord was not speaking hypothetically.

unconditional. There was nothing the children of Israel could do to reverse
it. They repented. But it was too late to enter that land and take possession of
it or experience a restful inheritance in it (Num 14:39-40; Dt 1:41-46).

changed God’s mind, it was impossible for these blood-bought Jews to enter
the rest even though they repented. Even so, it may be impossible for God’s
children today to obtain that promised reward even if they repent (Heb 6:6).
God’s immutable nature enables Him to make immutable promises and
threats which cannot be overthrown by subsequent obedience or
disobedience.
       God irrevocably has promised to remain unchanging in certain
aspects toward changing man even if man makes certain changes. By the
sheer nature of the case, in such cases man cannot make subsequent changes
that will undo the results of his earlier changes if God already has responded
irrevocably to man’s earlier changes.

may make after having taken that mark or seal, that person’s fate is sealed

in that mark or seal.

to remind us that our immutable God can make unconditional promises. The
fact that the Lord can be steadfast in carrying out certain threats, regardless
of our subsequent change in performance, gives us reason to believe that He
can be just as steadfast in carrying out certain positive promises, regardless
of our subsequent change in performance. In such cases, we cannot change
the outcome regardless of what subsequent changes we make. The outcome
already has been determined unconditionally from the moment God
responded irrevocably.

misthologists must carefully note the context in which they discover facts
pertaining to conditionality.

mistakes made by soteriologists while digging on the mountain of

rewards and those found in lower altitudes is that there is no longer any

versus apostasy. For Calvinists, Haley’s comments regarding perseverance

will never perish in Jn 10:28 (to cite one of Haley’s examples) does not
mean that it is impossible for believers to perish, only that they will not
perish. In other words, hypothetically speaking, it is possible for believers to
apostatize and perish. A believer’s keeping the regeneration which God
promised him or her is contingent upon the believer remaining a believer
and not committing apostasy. In TULIP, regeneration does not prevent
apostasy. Rather, God’s irresistible electing grace prevents apostasy, which,
in turn, prevents one from losing one’s regeneration: election →
perseverance → retention of regeneration. Some Calvinists, particularly at
the popular level, think that the order of causation in Calvinism is: election
→ retention of regeneration → perseverance. But this is a mistaken
understanding of the prevailing view within Calvinism. Although exceptions
have been noted elsewhere in my writings, the predominate Calvinistic
consensus tends to regard the perseverance of the saints (POTS) as
necessary for the preservation of the saints (POTS). For them, POTS refers
jointly to the perseverance of the saints and the preservation of the saints:
the former being the means of the latter. If (hypothetically and
Calvinistically speaking) believers were not to persevere, they would lose
their regeneration. As Vance has documented in his chapter Perseverance of
the Saints, Calvinists, by large, do not believe in eternal security. See
Laurence M. Vance, The Other Side of Calvinism, revised edition
