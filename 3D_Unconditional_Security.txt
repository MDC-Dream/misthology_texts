                                                    Key Terms
      Unconditional security is the conviction that once a person believes in Christ for eternal life, that person is
unconditionally secure in Christ and unconditionally assured of reaching heaven unconditionally. Technically, this
articulation is superior to saying that believers are unconditionally assured of reaching heaven. In Calvinism, God
unconditionally elects certain individuals to reach heaven through means of their perseverance. The elect not only will
persevere, they must persevere in order to reach heaven. Within Calvinism, reaching heaven is conditioned on the
perseverance of the elect. In contradistinction, unconditional security does not condition reaching heaven upon the believer’s
perseverance. Accordingly, unconditional election is not to be confused with unconditional security.
      Conditional security is the conviction that once a person believes in Christ for eternal life, that person is conditionally
secure in Christ and conditionally assured of reaching heaven. According to conditional securitists, it is incumbent upon
believers to persevere in order to reach heaven. Correspondingly, Calvinism is a form of conditional security.
      Conditionalist refers to an advocate of conditional security—the conviction that once a person believes in Christ for
eternal life, that person is conditionally secure in Christ and must persevere in order to reach heaven. Someone who believes
in unconditional election may still be a conditionalist.
      Hardcore Conditionalist refers to an advocate of conditional security who believes that perseverance in both faith and
faithfulness are necessary to reach heaven.
      Softcore Conditionalist refers to an advocate of conditional security who believes that perseverance in faith (as opposed
to perseverance in faithfulness) is all that is necessary to reach heaven.
      Soft-shelled Conditionalist refers to an advocate of conditional security who believes that only repentance or
commitment (rather than both repentance and commitment) are required soteriologically on the frontend.
      Securitist refers to an advocate of unconditional security—the conviction that once a person believes in Christ for eternal
life, that person is unconditionally secure in Christ and is assured of unconditionally reaching heaven. Most significantly,
reaching heaven is not conditioned on the believer’s perseverance.
      Hardcore Securitist refers to a strong advocate of unconditional security who believes that apostasy is possible and thus
does not believe that continuation in the faith is necessary to reach heaven.
      Soft-shelled Securitist refers to a soft advocate of unconditional security who believes that apostasy is impossible and
may regard continuation in the faith (as opposed to perseverance in the faith) as necessary to reach heaven.
      Strong Securitist refers to a strong advocate of unconditional security who affirms the soteriological necessity of
believing implicitly in unconditional security at the point of saving faith.
      Soft Securitist refers to a soft advocate of unconditional security who does not affirm the soteriological necessity of
believing implicitly in unconditional security at the point of saving faith.

                                                  Introduction
     Discussions of unconditional security normally focus on perseverance. Securitists object to adding perseverance onto the
backend of the gospel as a necessity to reach heaven. From the securitist perception, adding perseverance as a soteric1
necessity would backload the gospel with salvation by works, which is biblically forbidden and logically inconsistent. This
conceptual overview will focus on backend matters, for that is all that typically matters in many discussions of unconditional
security. Nevertheless, issues pertaining to frontloading the gospel will be mentioned briefly in connection with the necessity
of security. For securitists also object to frontloading the gospel since doing so would condition the believer’s security upon
the believer’s performance. This 3D overview of unconditional security will briefly survey the available options pertaining to
unconditional security along three axes: veracity, necessity, and intensity.

Veracity
     The principle horizontal axis, pictured on the cover of this book, is concerned with the veracity of unconditional security
and deals with the question: “Is unconditional security true?” This axis is the most important dimension of the discussion.
Both ends of this veracity-axis are assigned negative values since unconditional security is denied by those outside the sphere
on either side of the axis. The plus sign at the center of the axis (and thus at the heart of the sphere) shows that unconditional
security is affirmed by those within the spherical center of this debate. Both strong and soft forms of conditional security will
lie outside the sphere of unconditional security, of course. In terms of conditional security, Arminianism and Calvinism are
the mirror reflections of one another. For the Arminian, perseverance plus election results in heaven. For the Calvinist,
election plus perseverance results in heaven. For both, reaching heaven is conditioned on perseverance.

                                              Strong and Soft Conditionalism
     “What constitutes perseverance?” is the question that differentiates the strong and soft forms of conditional security.
Strong conditionalists assert that the perseverance necessary to reach heaven consists of both faith and faithfulness. Soft
conditionalists insist that reaching heaven can only be contingent upon perseverance in faith. In their correct estimation, to
condition entrance into heaven on both faith and faithfulness would teach salvation by works. Thus, soft conditionalists
rightly perceive that works-righteousness is blatantly present in the strong forms of condition security. Soft conditionalists
appropriately reject the strong forms of conditional security. Nevertheless, the soft forms frequently fall into a gray zone by
failing to recognize the works-righteousness latent in the soft position in that perseverance in faith is rewardable and thus
misthological.2 Perseverance is a work whether that perseverance pertains to faith and works or just to faith.
                                                  Conditionalistic Assurance
     In conditionalism, God exhorts us to persevere because He has conditioned our reaching heaven on our perseverance.
We must actively avail ourselves of God’s enabling grace so that we will not fail and thus go to hell. Since our final salvation
is conditioned on our cooperating role in this process, we cannot be absolutely sure that we will attain that goal. Hence, the
exhortation to take heed lest we fall applies to us all (1Cor 10:12). After all, unconditional assurance is impossible when one
is working from a premise of conditional security.

                                                   Soft Unconditional Security
     Because soft conditionalism conditions entrance into heaven only on one’s perseverance in faith, the premature
conclusion might be made that no unconditional securitist, in counter distinction, would insist that all believers necessarily
will continue in the faith. Alas, such is not the case, however, in that allowances must be made within the securitistic sphere
for securitists on both sides of the plus sign. Semi-securitists will be pictured within the gray zone outside the sphere.
                                                          Discipline
     On the left-hand side of the securitist sphere, some securitists (and semi-securitists), favoring an Arminian perspective,
believe that God disciplines us for the purpose of keeping us saved. For their perspective, if we were not to continue in the
faith, we would lose our regeneration. So God disciplines us so as to prevent us from committing apostasy and thereby losing
our regeneration. Losing our faith is hypothetically possible, but God prevents it from being realistically possible. Other than
the obvious fact that soft Arminians believe that believers might actually lose their faith and thus their regeneration, one
might well ask, “What else distinguishes these soft securitists (SS) from soft conditionalists (SC)?” After all, soft Calvinists
also frequently speak of apostasy as only being hypothetically possible. What distinguishes a soft discipline-oriented
securitist (SDOS) from a soft Calvinist?
                                  Illustration 2. Soft Acronyms for Unconditional Security




                                                          Regeneration
     Help in answering that question can be derived from interaction with soft securitists on the Calvinistic side of the
sphere.3 Ironically, some soft regeneration-oriented securitists (SROS) on the right-hand side of the sphere are more
appropriately inclined to insist that we could not even hypothetically lose our regeneration. Indeed, some of them would
assert that our regeneration preserves our faith. Surprisingly, some credit for this perspective may be traced back through the
Calvinistic confessions to Calvin himself in his comments on 1Jn 3:9. Even if we were to deny the faith with our lips, and
perhaps moreover try to convince ourselves that we no longer believe, nevertheless, in our heart of hearts, we still would
retain a smoldering ember of faith that even we cannot completely extinguish because of our new nature. While some
Calvinists may allow the truth of this statement, they offset it by simultaneously making perseverance necessary to retain
regeneration. Although such a combination is logically contradictory, it is necessitated within Calvinistic theology.
Securitists, in contrast, do not enjoin the security embedded in regeneration with the contingency explicit in perseverance.
                              Differentiation between Soft Conditionalists and Soft Securitists
     Despite the fact that soft conditionalists and soft Calvinists sometimes partially share a mutual perspective, substantial
differences can be discerned between consistent soft securitists and consistent soft conditionalists. The soft securitist would
allow that some believers might take a very passive role in maintaining their faith. Rather than discipline themselves, some
believers might leave their discipline entirely up to God. Just as some children leave the discipline of themselves entirely up
to their parents, so some believers leave the discipline of themselves wholly up to God. Self-discipline is a foreign concept to

such believers. Like children, they resist rather than yield to their Father’s discipline. Nevertheless, they can rest assured that
they are God’s children and that He will do all that is necessary to keep them saved—even if it kills them (1Cor 5:5;
1Cor 11:32). Premature death, if necessary, will guard them from eternal death. Even if such children develop a rebellious,
party-hardy attitude and break their Father’s heart, they will not break their Father’s will. He will keep them safe, even from
themselves, when it comes to their remaining His children. God has assumed exclusive responsibility for keeping them safe.
      Even on the left side of the sphere, then, three crucial areas separate soft-shelled securitists (SS) from soft conditionalists
(SC). Soft conditionalists are outside the sphere of unconditionally security, while soft securitists are pictured on the outer
shell of unconditional security. Soft discipline-oriented security (SDOS) is not to be confused with soft conditionalism (SC).
Conditionalists are performatists. They condition the believer reaching heaven on the believer’s performance/perseverance.
Securitists do not. Practical points of demarcation between SDOS and SC may be discerned also.
      First, just as the father of a little child assumes exclusive responsibility to discipline his child in regard to his child’s
safety, so God has assumed exclusive responsibility, at the fundamental level of unconditional security, to keep His child safe
from hell. A father who allows his little girl to play with matches with the result that she is burned to death would be found
criminally negligent. God does not let His children play with the matches of hell. His children might fall down and get carpet
burns, but they will not burn in hell. God has placed this hellish type of burning beyond the scope of possibility for His
children. In contrast to soft conditionalists, who assert that you must assume some measure of responsibility for your own
safety in this capacity, soft securitists insist that God has assumed sole responsibility for your safety from this level of danger.
      Second, even if God’s children actively rebel against Him, they cannot rebel to the point of going to hell. Rather than
trying to keep themselves saved, they might get angry with their Father and do everything in their power to hurt Him by
breaking His heart by indulging in worldly pursuits. Like Israel of old (depicting God’s redeemed children), they might run
after other gods only to find that they cannot run from the fact that they are God’s redeemed children—permanently. Unlike
soft conditionalists, who exhort you to respond positively to God’s discipline so that you may reach heaven, soft securitists
assure you that even if you respond negatively to God’s discipline you remain God’s child.
      Third, as a result of unconditional security, God’s children can have unconditional assurance. They can know that
whatever the future holds, their Father holds them in His hand and that He will never let go—never ever. Even if they were to
fear that someday they might try to commit spiritual suicide, they can rest assured, even today, that they could not do so to
the point of snuffing out the regenerate ember of eternal life burning deep within them or the glow of faith that emerges from
it.4 Even soft securitists can look out bravely toward the future with full assurance that if Satan were successful in defeating
them, he will not be successful in defeating their Father. Their safety is their Father’s responsibility, not theirs. In
counterpoint to soft conditionalists, who pragmatically must admit they might not reach heaven (because they might lose
their salvation or prove that they were never saved in the beginning), soft securitists can be absolutely sure that they are saved
in the present and will reach heaven in the end apart from any performance on their part.
                                                      Strong Securitists
    As attractive as soft security may seem to some securitists, soft security is weak in comparison to the full strength,
unconditional security taught by the Bible. Strong security is preferable and located in the center of the securitist sphere.
Strong securitists can rest assured that even if they commit moral and theological apostasy, they are absolutely and
unconditionally assured of reaching heaven. In contrast to those soft securitists (SDOS) who think that God’s discipline is
necessary to prevent them from losing regeneration, strong securitists can acknowledge the realistic possibility of apostasy
without having to commit intellectual suicide in thinking that the eternal life within them could die theoretically. Because
securitists have eternal life and believingly know that they have it, they can know that they will keep this regenerate life
regardless of whether they continue to believe. Yes, God has assumed exclusive responsibility for their safety. Yet He has not
conditioned that security in any shape, form, or degree upon their preservation in the faith—not even upon a God-protected
preservation in the faith in which they are completely passive.
                                                  Wolves in Sheep’s Clothing
    Undoubtedly, wolves in sheep’s clothing will try to deceive themselves (and the sheep) into thinking that they are sheep
by claiming that their soft conditionalism is really soft security. At times it will be difficult to discern the sheep from such
wolves because of the exegetical inconsistencies that naturally tend to arise in all soft forms of conditional and unconditional
security. Nevertheless, several guiding principles should be helpful in ferreting out what form of softness lies below the
various interpretations of the standard proof texts so frequently used by those in the gray zone.

                                                               1Pet 1:5
     For example, 1Pet 1:5 is a verse that unfortunately has caused some securitists to soften their view of security. They
condition unconditional security on what they perceive to be linear faith within this verse. But how can a gift be conditioned
on a faith that results in a reward? Linear faith (at least in the form of persevering faith) results in a reward and therefore is a
work. In any case, even putting aside that objection, this soft view smacks of self-apparent inconsistency in conditioning
unconditional security on linear faith. If security is unconditional, how can it be conditioned? How can you condition
unconditional security? Such proposals are counterintuitive. Nevertheless, in view of the above observations made about
distinguishing characteristics, soft security can be distinguished from soft conditionalism, at least theoretically, with a
reasonable degree of success even in the midst of needless inconsistencies (or at least needless complexities) found within
soft security. Three diagnostic questions can be asked of those holding a soft view.
                                                      Diagnostic Questions
    1.   Do believers share any responsibility for their protection within 1Pet 1:5, or does God bear exclusive
         responsibility? An Arminian wolf in sheep’s clothing will talk of God’s enabling grace and thus be more easily
         detected than the Calvinistic counterpart who attributes the believer’s faith exclusively to God’s sovereign grace.
         Notwithstanding, in conjunction with the other questions posed below, even a Calvinistic wolf might be sniffed out
         if this conditional security is grounded merely (or even in part) within the unconditional election of Calvinism.
         Posing that God unconditionally elects you to persevere so that you conditionally will reach heaven is nothing more
         than conditional security since in this proposition it is at least partially incumbent upon you to persevere in order to
         reach heaven. Calvinistic theology negates unconditional security with the addition of perseverance as a soteric
         necessity and therefore may be unmasked by further inquiry.
    2.   Will believers be saved even if they steadfastly and actively resist God’s will to protect them through faith?
         The Arminian will tend to indicate that believers must cooperate with the working of God’s power to keep
         themselves saved. Theoretically, the Calvinist might insist that God sovereignly causes believers to keep believing
         without any active cooperation on the believers’ part. Exegetically, though, how Calvinists deal with exhortations to
         keep believing would provide an interesting cross-examination. Unmasking the underlying notion that God
         unconditionally elected them to reach heaven through means of perseverance begs the question: “Do they have any
         active role to play in this perseverance?” For Calvinists, perseverance is not optional. The P in the Calvinistic
         TULIP stands for perseverance, not for preservation. Calvinists do not believe in unconditional preservation but in a
         preservation predicated upon perseverance.
    3.   Are believers being logically consistent in being unconditionally certain they will reach heaven apart from
         anything they do? The Arminian will object to the thought that believers can be absolutely certain that they will
         reach heaven. The Calvinist will tend to speak more so in terms of unconditional election rather than unconditional
         assurance or security. Ask Calvinists, “What will happen if you fail to persevere?” Those Calvinists who are true to
         the Calvinistic system and honest as to the outcome of that system would have to acknowledge that failure to
         persevere would prove that they are not elect. The follow-up question would be, “Is it possible that you might not
         persevere?” Calvinistically, this possibility would have to be acknowledged (1Cor 10:12). Yet this mere possibility
         proves that their proveitist perception is capable of falsification by subsequent proof and therefore unreliable for
         present proof.

     At this point, some will argue that they are absolutely certain that they are saved and that they will persevere. If so, they
are either Calvinists who are not being true to the implications of their own theology, or, if they have passed the other two
questions posed above, they might be soft securitists with a mistaken notion of what is entailed in perseverance. If they insist
that they are absolutely certain they will reach heaven apart from anything they may do because God bears the exclusive
responsibility to keep them saved even if they rebel against His will, then the presumption should be granted that they are soft
securitists who have so softened the doctrine of perseverance that it no longer retains its biblical meaning. They do not assert
biblical perseverance when they affirm their personal perseverance. Clarification should be sought as to what they mean by
perseverance because a perseverance that requires no effort on their part is neither biblical nor logical. Obviously, an
effortless perseverance would be a misnomer and fall outside the Calvinistic spectrum. An innocent-until-proven-guilty
policy would be permissible regarding those who insist that they will reach heaven effortlessly apart from any performance
on their part. A careful cross-examination with passages dealing with exhortations and warnings would be advisable to clarify
the meaning and role of perseverance in their perspective.
     In Calvinism, God has not unconditionally guaranteed the perseverance of the elect. Rather, He implements the use of
means (such as the exhortations and warnings) to cause the elect to persevere. If the elect were to fail to respond
appropriately to these means, they would go to hell. Therefore, their use of these means is their means of reaching heaven,
which means, of course, that they are trying to get to heaven by means of their works. Attempting to label these Calvinistic

works as God’s works will not work since the elect are the one’s actually performing such works in trying to keep their faith
alive so that they can reach heaven. Biblically, God works within the elect by energizing them to work, not by doing the work
for them or in place of them. God-enabled human work is still human work. Trusting in this work as a means of reaching
heaven places one outside the sphere of unconditional security as one who is trusting in his or her performance as a necessary

                                          Practical Differences between SDOS and SCC
     The soft discipline-oriented securitist (SDOS) might reason: “I am going to heaven even if I decide to live like the devil
because God will do whatever is necessary (through regeneration, external and internal influences, and even death) to keep
me from sinking so low that my faith would die completely. I’d better not live like the devil because, if necessary, God will
kill me to save me.”
     The soft Calvinistic conditionalist (SCC), who is trying to stand in the gray zone with one foot in soft conditionalism and
the other foot in soft security, would reason similarly: “I am going to heaven because I cannot live like the devil. God will do
whatever is necessary to cause me to do whatever is necessary to keep from sinking so low that my faith would die
completely. I’d better not try to live like the devil because, if necessary, God will kill me to save me.”
     The distinction is subtle and runs the insurmountable risk of being deadly. Trying to avoid hell in this SCC manner
actually embraces a false gospel that leads to hell. In this soft form of conditionalism, not only is God doing whatever is
necessary to keep believers saved, believers themselves are doing whatever is necessary to cause themselves to do whatever
is necessary to keep themselves saved. Such believers are trusting in their doing whatever is necessary to reach heaven as a
means of their reaching heaven. Therefore, such believers are not believers in the NT sense. Such people have failed to
believe in God for a security that is not conditioned on their performance. They have misconstrued the means of reaching
heaven.5
                                                        Mark of the Beast
     Take the mark of the beast for example. A soft discipline-oriented securitist (SDOS) living during the tribulation might
think: “I’d better not give in to the desire to take the mark of the beast because God will kill me if I were to do so. God will
not let me go to hell, so He will kill me before allowing me to do something that would send me to hell. If I give in to the
desire to take the hell-resulting mark of the beast, I will die.” In allowing the possibility that such a person might still
experience saving faith, I am seeking to be as open as possible to how much logical inconsistency might be permissible
within the soft securitistic ranks. In this scenario, believers do not trust in anything they may actively do in order to keep
themselves saved from hell. Indeed, if they decide to take the mark, they can rest assured that God will kill them and thereby
keep them safe from hell.
     The soft Calvinistic conditionalist (SCC), trying to live in the gray zone of semi-security, might reason in like fashion:
“I cannot give in to the desire to take the mark of the beast because God will do whatever is necessary to keep me from
giving in to the desire to take that hell-resulting mark. I will not give in.”6 This conditionalist is trusting in his or her God-
given resolve not to take the mark of the beast as a means of reaching heaven and thus is trusting in his or her God-enabled
performance for what was supposed to have been a God-given free gift.
     Giving in to the desire to live like the devil or to take the mark of the devil is possible within soft securitism (SS). It is
not possible in soft Calvinistic conditionalism (SC), though, for the elect to give in to the desire to live according to the flesh
or to take the mark of the beast. Soft conditionalistic Calvinists (SCC) believe that God will do whatever is necessary to
cause them to do whatever is necessary to avoid taking that mark or dying soteriologically. Such soft conditionalists assume
that God irresistibly will use their fear of this hell-resulting mark to cause them to resist giving in to the desire to take this
mark. They endure the trials and hardships of not taking the mark for the sake of reaching heaven and thus escaping hell.

zone is likely to be found to exist around the securitist sphere separating (not so successfully at times) the soft conditionalist

zone so as to make it bulge out in favor of their interpretation.

                                                      A Practical Example
     To move this discussion from the abstract to the concrete, an example based on a recent movie inspired by true life
events will be helpful. In the movie The Vow, a young woman suffers a head injury in a car wreck. As a result, the doctors
keep her in a coma for an extended period of time in order to allow her brain to recover from the trauma and swelling. When
she awakens, she does not recognize her husband nor remember having been married nor recall her marriage vows. She has
lost her memory of the past five years and never regains it.
     For sake of illustration, suppose a similar event happened to a young lady, whom we will call Judy, who was an atheist.
Judy came to faith in Christ during her freshman year in college. Shortly after college graduation, however, she is involved in
a car wreck and suffers brain trauma. When she awakens weeks later, she has forgotten everything since her senior year in
high school. Needless to say, her college diploma is practically worthless. Everything she learned in college is gone. More
importantly, her faith in Christ is gone—through no fault of her own. Given the Arminian premise, she lost her regeneration
either during her coma or upon awaking from it. If she lost her regeneration upon awaking, then her surviving the tragedy
was the true tragedy. It would have been better for Judy to have died during the coma and to have gone on to heaven rather
than to have lived through the coma and thus to have become a Judas upon awakening from the coma. Quite against her free
will, Judy became a Judas. Suppose, like Judas, she died shortly thereafter. Arminians insist that believers must be able to
lose regeneration because otherwise their free will would be violated. In this example, though, the Arminian insistence that
retention of regeneration is conditioned on persevering in faith would require them to conclude that she lost her regeneration
against her free will. If you are an Arminian, fear getting into a car. It may take you to hell, and there may nothing you can do
about it. Your Arminian vows will do nothing to save you from an Arminian hell in such a case.
     Suppose you shrink back from such a conclusion with the remark, “Well, God would never allow such a thing to happen
to a genuine believer.” Such is the mindset of many Calvinists. Mark this statement well in that case, Calvinist, if this ever
happens to you, then your Calvinistic friends and family will have to conclude, when you awaken from your coma, that you
never were a genuine believer. So what will you do then? Hopefully, come to the place where you repeat those vows? But
what if you do not live long enough to come to such a place? Or what if you get angry at God for having allowed you to have
undergone such a tragedy and never come to that place again? For that matter, if those vows did not save you the first time,
why think that they will save you the second time—even if you could return to the place of making those vows again?
     Securitists have real answers for such potential, real-life situations. For starters, you never have to worry that anything,
not even the loss of your faith, whether intentional or unintentional, could cause you to lose your regeneration. Even if
something like this did happen to you, rewards are at stake, not your salvation from hell. Second, even awaking from a coma
in an apostate state would not misthologically make you an apostate. Misthology is premised upon moral ability. Since you
did not lose your faith because of something you did wrong, you cannot be faulted for having lost your faith. Third, you have
some splendid promises that apply to you, even though you no longer believe them, because God never stopped counting you
as a believer, not for a moment. God will give you the opportunity to escape that apostate state and again become conformed
to Christlikeness (Rom 8:28; 1Cor 10:13). For in His eyes you are still a believer. In fact, unbeknownst even to you, at the
metaphysical core of your being, you still believe. Your physical impairment did not impair your metaphysical core.
     Many out-of-body experiences have been documented at near-death states. People who have undergone such experiences
have seen events going on in the operating room where they physically were lying unconscious. Metaphysically, at the core
of their being, in their spirit, they are aware of such events going on in the operating room and elsewhere. We may deduce
that Judy’s spirit is aware that she is a believer (Rom 8:16). Even while she is in the operating room, during the coma, and
upon awaking from the coma, her spirit is aware of what is happening and what has happened. Although her soul no longer
has any recollection of her faith in Christ and is completely atheistic because of the physical impairment, nevertheless, her
spirit lives on and continues to believe in Christ. The physical limitation of her body may impair her soul, but not her spirit.
Documented experiences and deductive logic lead to this conclusion as being only reasonable.

metaphysical aspect of our being. Saving faith also would be a function of this dimension of our being. Our spirit interacts

     To briefly recap in terms of the three primary axes, the principle question pertaining to the veracity-axis addresses
whether unconditional security is true. If it is true, under what conditions does it apply? Is the quantity of eternal life
contingent in any way upon perseverance. Is the unending quantitative dimension of this life only potentially available to
believers? Must they persevere in order to maintain this quantity of life?

     The necessity-axis, therefore, is not addressing the question as to whether perseverance
is necessary to reach heaven. Consistent securitists are agreed that it is not. Rather, the

Soft securitists, however, would open the narrow gate to allow soft conditionalists into

allow strong conditionalistic wolves into the sheepfold of believers.
     Calling conditionalists wolves in sheep’s clothing will be thought to be uncharitably harsh by those who prefer a more
irenic interaction. Yet not only for the sake of the sheep and for the sake of logical consistency and biblical fidelity, but also
for the sake of the wolves, those securitists who hold a narrow view of the narrow gate cannot afford to be less than explicit
in their deducing the intent of the logical and biblical nature of the warnings pertaining to such wolves. How are we to warn
the flock of the wolves if we do not point out the wolves to the flock? The wolves in Mt 7:15 are to be identified either by
their: (1) teaching, (2) performance, or (3) teaching and performance. To interpret the false teachers in this passage as being
hell-bound wolves and then identify them by means of either of the latter two options would lead to a salvation from hell that
is conditioned on performance. Unconditional security would be denied implicitly. This is not an option for the consistent
securitist. These wolves are to be identified as such by their teaching. They teach a false gospel of performance-based
security. Necessarily, they must be identified as conditional securitists. The only loving thing that a biblically-aware securitist
can do is point out the dangers of unconditionalism, not only to the sheep but also to the wolves. Let both be warned of the
dangers of unconditional security.
     The third axis focuses on the quality of eternal life. Jesus not only came that we might have life quantitatively but
abundantly and therefore qualitatively (Jn 10:10). Although the quantitative aspect is foundational, the qualitative
dimension is pivotal. At this juncture, the axis of eternal life pivots to a vertical orientation. Perspectives differ far
more widely among securitists along this axis. Differences of opinion in this arena are not nearly as crucial as they
are in the other two axes. Indeed, a number of good books defending unconditional security have denied that the outer
darkness is a rewards concept. While such books tend to be rather weak in the misthological department, they
nevertheless can be commended in the area of soteriology. To a considerable extent, their soteriological strengths
help to offset their misthological weaknesses. Notwithstanding such anomalies, this axis is vital for providing proper
incentives for Christian performance and purification for the believer’s motives.
     While this overview of unconditional security is only suggestive of the dimensions encountered in the study of this
essential doctrine, nonetheless it allows one to get a bird’s eye picture of some of the major concepts being explored within
such studies. This quantitative-quantitative-qualitative life is first and foremost quantitative and thus eternal. This much
should be self-apparent to anyone, even a child. Sometimes a childlike answer to the question, “Will I go to heaven when I
die?” that responds with an unqualified, “Yes, because I have believed in Jesus for eternal life!” in many ways surpasses the
answers provided by those who have studied the question intently. The child can sense that going to heaven qualitatively
qualifies as being called life. That this life extends into heaven is understood to mean that it lasts forever. Many children have
come to saving faith by simply concluding that they will live forever with Jesus in heaven because they believed in the Lord
Jesus as their Savior. Such a childlike understanding of eternal life, though so immature in understanding the nature of
heaven, is completely sufficient to reach heaven.

                                                       Typical Answer
        Typically, the answer given to the question, “Does God need us?” is, “No, God does not need us; we need Him.” The
premiere proof text used to support this assessment is Acts 17:24-25: “The God who made the world and all things in it, since
He is Lord of heaven and earth, does not dwell in temples made with hands; neither is He served by human hands, as though
He needed anything, since He Himself gives to all life and breath and all things.” Hence, the standard deduction is that God
does not need anyone or anything. Theologically, the stress is that God is self-existent, self-sustaining, and self-fulfilling. As
a Trinity, He is perfectly happy within Himself. He does not need us to complete His happiness. He did not create us because
of any unmet need, for example, and He did not become dependent upon us after creating us.
                                                        Balanced Answer
        Of course, God does not need us in order to exist (Ps 50:9-12) or find fulfillment within Himself. But is self-
fulfillment the only filling that God desires? The Bible tells us that God wants us. In fact, He even jealously desires us and
wants to fill us. He loves us and wants to be loved by us. A moment’s worth of reflection would concede, then, that He needs
us if He wants to complete such desires. His desire to express His love for us and be loved by us would be unfilled without
His loving us and our loving Him in response. To fulfill His desire to love us and be loved by us, He would need to love and
be loved in return.
        God does not need us in order to love or be loved—only to love us and to be loved by us. Within the members of the
Trinity, the need to love and be loved is already fully met—within the limitations of that self-contained sphere. Yet in order
to fulfill His desire to love others outside of Himself and be loved outside of Himself, God would need to create others
outside of Himself, naturally. The maximization of His love in this external, reciprocal expression required our creation.
Some object by arguing that if God created us because He wanted to be loved by us, this would make His love selfish. To the
contrary, God’s desire to be loved is not selfish. Rather, the nature of love is to make oneself vulnerable to the desire to be
loved in return. God is vulnerable to His desire to be love because He is love. Love does not limit itself to those who
reciprocate with love; nevertheless, it desires such reciprocity. To desire loving reciprocity is not selfish.

plans into motion that require our involvement in order to complete those plans. God needs our involvement in order to bring

of Scripture concerning the woman at the well in Jn 4 expresses it this way:

       Contrary to the objection of some, to acknowledge that Jesus decided to place Himself in a position where He needed

rulership will be realized for the maximization of that loving union with the result that ultimately it will be realized.
                                            Macrocosmic versus Microcosmic Levels
        At the individual, microcosmic level we cannot play emotional blackmail with God and threaten not to love Him (and
thereby hurt Him) if He fails to respond in the manner we desire for Him to respond to us and then think that we will thwart
the fullness of His joy as a result. To be sure, at the microscopic level we have the power to hurt God, but at the macrocosmic
level His joy will be made full with or without us by those who decide to respond to Him in love at the microcosmic level.
We can choose to be a part of that microcosmic fulfillment or not, but we cannot thwart the macrocosmic fulfillment that God
will experience by those who do respond positivity to Him at the microcosmic level. God is sovereign. At the macrocosmic
level, He will accomplish His objective with or without our aid in our own particular microcosmic sphere. In love, He has
made Himself vulnerable to being hurt by us. Yet if we chose to spend our life hurting God, we will find that it will hurt us
far more.
                                                       Infinite Versus Finite
        The ability of the finite to hurt the infinite is dwarfed by the ability of the infinite to hurt the finite. A microscopic
splinter that is quickly removed and thrown into the fire has little to rejoice about in the millisecond of pain that it inflicts
upon the one who casts it into the fire for its permanent destruction. The finite cannot hurt the infinite infinitely or eternally.
But the infinite can hurt the finite eternally. God can be hurt but only to a finite degree and for a finite duration. We, on the
other hand, can be hurt infinitely in the sense of eternally.

        The Bible is very clear and graphic in depicting God rendering such hurt on those who refuse to act in accordance
with His desires. To perceive God as needy does not picture Him as wimpy. Even without us, God would be completely

has determined that His cosmic love will achieve maximum realization, even without the help of those who choose not to

                                                       Bride at the Well
        Jesus needed the woman at the well in order to make His joy complete of having a successful harvest in the manner in
which He had chosen to circumscribe His ministry that day. Here was a woman having a terrible time finding a man who
wanted to have and keep her for a wife. Yet she was being offered to become the Bride of Christ. The Lord wanted her, and
He needed her cooperation if she was to become His Bride.
        The friend of the bridegroom stands outside the door to listen for the voice of the bridegroom, confirming that the
bridegroom has found the bride to be a virgin so that the bridegroom will consummate the marriage. Otherwise, under the
law, the groom would have grounds for having his betrothed put to death (Dt 22:13-21). By coming to Christ in saving faith,
this woman was now betrothed to Christ as a “pure virgin” (1Cor 11:2). If subsequently she devoted herself to Christ, she
would qualify for the consummation of ruling with Christ as His bridal queen. Otherwise, the right to consummate the union
of co-regency with Christ would be forfeited (2Cor 11:3; 2Tim 2:12).
        The OT episode of Rebecca at the well is widely recognized as a typological picture of the Bride of Christ. As a sign,
Abraham’s servant asked God that the girl from whom he would ask water would not only provide water for himself but for
his camels as well. As it turned out, the girl from whom he asked for the water was a beautiful virgin, who did indeed water
the camels (Gen 24:1-61). The parallel between her and the woman at the well has not escaped the notice of the
commentators, although the full significance of the logical relationship has not been explored. As it turns out in Jn 4, when
Jesus asks this woman for a drink, she winds up watering the whole village with her testimony of living water! Logically, we
may infer that she became betrothed to Christ just as Rebecca was typologically.

faithful believers as His bridal companions. In order to fulfill this desire, He needs faithful believers who are willing to work
in His fields that are “white for harvest” (Jn 4:35). As these new believers came to faith in Christ for eternal life, they could
then proceed to clothe themselves morally in white garments and thus prepare themselves to become bridal companions: “Let

                                                         Not Selfish
       Certainly, the desire to have one’s own joy made full by the love of another is not unbiblical or selfish (cp. Phil 2:2).

rewarded with our love is not ungodly. To have His joy made full in this area necessitates our love.
                                                       Body of Christ
        As believers, we are members of Christ’s body. Paul affirms this truth in 1Cor 6:15, right after warning believers that
those believers who live in an unrighteous manner will not “inherit the kingdom” (1Cor 6:9-10). Yes, positionally, they have
been made pure virgins, so to speak (1Cor 6:11), but their consummation as the Bride, who would co-rule, would require that
they be morally pure as well. Being a member of Christ’s body of believers only requires that one be a believer in Christ.
However, to be a member of His bridal body, who co-rules with Him, requires that one be a morally pure believer.
                                                          I Need You
        Contrary to many theologians, as members of Christ body, we are confident that Christ will not tell us, “I have no
need of you” (1Cor 12:21). He does need us. Christ is the head of the body, the church. The head will not tell the rest of
body, “I do not need you.” Nor will Christ tell the church, “I do not need you.” By logical inference, we likewise may deduce
that Christ would not tell His Bride, “I do not need you.” She is part of His body. When a man and woman join in holy
matrimony, they are considered one body (one flesh). Part of God’s plan is to picture the Lord joining Himself to us (wherein
we become one spirit with the Lord). Moreover, when God created Eve, He did so saying, “It is not good for the man to be
alone; I will make him a helper suitable for him.” (Gen 2:18). Typologically, this pictures the making of the Bride of Christ.
By this picture is God not suggesting that the highest good for the Son cannot be achieved apart from the Son having bridal
companions?
        God did not create us because He was lonely in terms of self-sufficiency. However, in terms of interdependence, God
could not realize a reciprocal love with those outside of Himself that mirrors the love that He finds within Himself until He
created those outside of Himself that could mirror that love. Doing so would even allow Him to increase the reciprocal love
He experiences within Himself (Jn 10:17). God did not have to create us. He could have chosen to be content with the self-
sufficiency of His own internal love. Yet He chose to create us in order to expand His love to include other dimensions. And
He created us in the manner in which He did in order to maximize that love. God could have chosen to stand alone apart from
us, and He could have been happy within the loving sphere of His self-sufficiency. He had a choice, and in terms of the
maximization of His love, He decided it was not good that He should be alone in the paradise of that self-sufficiency. Could
it be that in the creation of Adam and Eve, to some extent at least, God is telling us His story in relation to creation? After all,
Adam was created in God’s image and is a type of Christ. God’s desire to provide a wife for Adam, who was to be loved by
Adam and Himself, was to picture the love of Christ and His Father for the Bride of Christ (made ready by the Spirit of
Christ). But to maximize her love for Him, He allowed her to fall so that He might stoop to lift her.

individuals at the microscopic level. Whatever pain God may allow Himself to be subjected to by us, in placing Himself in

    To further clarify the classifications being made herein as to the various forms of securitism and conditionalism, the 3D

    In this revised model, strong securitism has been pictured as a hardcore center within the outer shell of soft securitism. In
the strong view of hardcore securitism, the possibility of actual apostasy is allowed. Regenerate believers do sometimes fall
away from the faith, but they do not forfeit their regeneration in doing so. Such matters as soteric justification, the Spirit’s

unconditionally secure those who at any time have believed in Christ for eternal life.
    The soft outer shell of the soft securitism might be conceived of as a force field held in place by the nucleus. This force
field might be pictured as a shield of faith similar to that pictured in Eph 6:16 with a critical difference. The shield of faith
raised by the believer as part of the Christian armor is exterior to the shield of faith being projected and protected by God at
this atomic level. The believer has no control over the inner shield of faith being pictured above. Therefore, this nuclear
shield surrounding the securitistic atomic core must not be confused with the shield of faith for which believers are
responsible in Eph 6:16 in their battle to extinguish the flaming arrows of doubt. Soft securitists theorize that this nuclear
shield of faith pictured above is either so unassailably protected by God’s discipline or so inexhaustibly generated by

from losing their regeneration. Such discipline does not cause believers to believe, but it does prevent believers from ceasing
to believe. For example, the cover over my grill does not cause the coals within my grill to keep burning, yet the cover does
prevent the rain from putting the coals out when I am grilling in the rain. Likewise, God’s discipline protects the believer’s
faith from going out altogether. The flames of faith may die down, but the embers still burn.
    In the SROS version of soft securitism, regeneration prevents believers from falling away from the faith. Proponents of
SROS are not necessarily agreed that the preservation of the believer’s faith is necessary to prevent believers from losing
regeneration. Some SROS securitists think that believers must not fall away from the faith because they would lose their
regeneration in that case, and God will not allow this to happen. In this case, SROS might picture regeneration as producing
its own force field of faith and thereby guaranteeing its own survival. In that sense, regeneration is self-perpetuating. It
generates an impenetrable force field by which it protects itself. Others within SROS simply conclude that believers will not
fall away from faith because their regeneration prevents apostasy. Loss of regeneration is not at stake. Nevertheless,
regeneration preserves the believer’s faith, making it impossible for believers to apostatize.

Because a range of values along three axes would be more descriptive of FG theology than trying to limit oneself to a 1D

                                                             Veracity
    Veracity is the primary axes. “Is unconditional security true?” is the focus of this discussion. “What constitutes
unconditional security?” is a necessary consideration as well. Contrary to the opinion of some in FG, yet depending upon
one’s definitions, both strong and soft Calvinists should be excluded from inclusion within the FG sphere. Unconditional
election cannot be equated with unconditional security. A security conditioned upon perseverance cannot be rightly called
unconditional.
    Accordingly, some within the FG arena would seek to exclude those writers in the FG camp who insist that preservation
in the faith is necessary to reach heaven. In counterpoint, notwithstanding my disagreement with the preservationist point of
view, I would allow that those FG writers who merely affirm the preservation of the believer’s faith as a soteriological
necessity should not be excluded from the FG fellowship. Preservation should not be equated with perseverance.
Preservationists, at least of the consistent FG variety, do not condition security upon a believer’s performance (not even upon
the believer’s perseverance in the faith) and hence should be allowed under the FG umbrella.
    On the other hand, some writers (who try to pass themselves off as proponents of FG theology) frontload the gospel with
either repentance or commitment as necessary components of saving faith. By posing one versus the other as soteriologically
necessary, they seek to present themselves as advocates of FG theology. In reality, they are wolves in FG clothing and must
be excluded from the protective sphere of FG theology. The maximum range along the veracity-axis should not be expanded
to accommodate their perspective. This maximum range must exclude frontloading the gospel with performance (by means
of commitment or repentance) and prohibit backloading the gospel (by means of perseverance in faithfulness or perseverance
in faith). Closing the door to one false form of the gospel while opening it to another will not suffice. The FG door must be
shut to all forms of the gospel that would seek to incorporate good works as a necessary means of entering heaven.
    In a similar fashion, the necessity of minimum FG values in the comprehensive approach is attested by the necessary
exclusion of some who would affirm unconditional security and yet be excluded from the FG community. The universalist,
for example, would allow that everyone who believes in Christ is unconditionally secure. Moreover, the evangelical
inclusivist, who believes in Christ for eternal life, might be said to qualify for eternal life (even in FG terms).
Notwithstanding, neither a universalist nor an evangelical inclusivist would be regarded as belonging to the FG camp.

exclusivism is the more appropriate representation of what is embraced in the FG community. At its heart, FG theology is an

                                                             Necessity
    Necessity is the second most import axis. “Does one necessarily have to believe, at least implicitly, in unconditional
security at the point of conversion in order to qualify for saving faith?” Some in the FG community, while they affirm the
veracity of unconditional security, do not think that belief in unconditional security is necessary for faith to be saving.
Strangely, according to ultra-soft FG securitists, the same accursed gospel that separates believers from Christ can bring
unbelievers to Christ (Gal 1:6-9; 5:4).26 Texts such as Jn 20:31 and 1Jn 5:1 are thought to prove that all that is soteriologically
necessary is that one believes that Jesus is the Christ (the Son of God). Those who believe that proposition are thought to be
unconditionally secure even if they reject unconditional security.
    Evidently, from the minimalistic ultra-soft perspective, those who believe that Jesus is the Christ in any sense still qualify
for eternal life. The Christological content of what it means to believe in Jesus as the Christ is so minimal that one could
virtually fill in the blank as to what it means to be Christ with any content. Theoretically, one might even define Jesus as the
Christ in the sense of one who gives a bag of marbles to those who believe in Him. After all, from a minimalistic perspective
that allows one to fill in the blank with anything, one could just as well fill it in with a bag of marbles. One could believe in
Christ for a bag of marbles and yet get eternal life as a result. Those of us in the FG community who reject this proposal
wonder if our FG brethren who hold this ultra-soft perspective have lost their marbles. Nevertheless, charity should caution
us against disavowing these ultra-soft securitists as FG brethren. Perhaps ultra-soft securitists have gone soft in the head, in
thinking that faith alone in Christ alone qualifies for unconditional security even if that faith is not alone nor in Christ alone.
Even so, although these ultra-soft securitists think that a soteriologically accursed gospel qualifies as a saving gospel, these
FG brethren do not believe an accursed gospel themselves and thus are not soteriologically accursed. As a result, the
minimum range should include these ultra-soft securitists.27
    At this juncture, the discussion tends to become very heated. Some ultra-soft securitists, apparently sensing a need for
some type of minimal value that would rule out nonsensical things like a bag of marbles, seek to raise the bar. Since ultra-soft
securitists refuse to take belief in unconditional security as their minimum value, these ultra-soft conditionalists focus on
Christology as their means of finding a minimum value for their soteriology. After all, their premise regarding Jn 20:31 and
1Jn 5:1 seemingly points them in this direction.
    Various propositions about Christ, especially regarding His death and deity, are set forth as what they hold to be the
minimum values that one must believe in order to qualify for saving faith. Unfortunately, some of those adopting this
Christological approach to finding a minimum value have ostracized their FG brethren who, as strong securitists, hold an
equally high view of Christology and yet find their minimum values elsewhere, namely in affirming the necessity of faith in
Christ as the bestower of unconditional security. Regardless, both sets of securitists should be included in the FG sphere.
    Securitists focusing on an all-embracing Christology sometimes make such a long list of Christological requirements as to
what one must believe about Christ that they risk exceeding the maximum values that can be assigned to Christ without

    Some strong securitists will minimize the amount of information theologically necessary to be believed so that Christ
means guarantor of eternal life. If you simply believe in Jesus as the one who guarantees you unconditional security as a

Christ. Saving faith consists of believing in Jesus for unconditional security. By simply believing in Jesus for unconditional
security, you get unconditional security. The beauty of this simplicity is self-apparent. You get that which you believe. If you
believe in Christ for unconditional security, that is what you get. Otherwise, you do not.
    Notwithstanding, other strong securitists seek to maximize the amount of data you must believe. They will include truths
related to Christ’s death and deity as soteriologically necessary. To believe that Jesus is the Christ requires that you do more
than simply believe in Him for eternal life. Additional information regarding His identity is added to His offer of
unconditional security as soteriologically and subjectively necessary. As above, these battle lines are also hotly contested.
    My composite of minimum and maximum values for a joint veracity-necessity plane will indicate my preference.
Minimally, those in the FG community are agreed as to the necessity of security in terms of its veracity. Maximumally,
strong FG securitists contend that faith in Christ as the bestower of unconditional security is necessary for saving faith to
occur.28 Although this faith must be explicitly in Christ, the faith in unconditional security may be implicit. A full, robust
understanding of unconditional security is not necessary for saving faith to have occurred. Moreover, believing in
unconditional security as necessary is itself not necessary for inclusion within the FG community. Soft securitists who affirm
unconditional security and yet do not regard faith in unconditional security to be soteriological necessary are genuinely found
within the FG sphere.
                                                              Intensity
    Intensity is the last and least axis to be considered in the present study. As a misthologist, I am tempted to regard those in
FG who hold an exclusively soteriological view of the outer darkness as teaching an accursed gospel because their approach
has a pronounced propensity, particularly pertaining to the third passage in question (Mt 25:14-30), to lend itself to teaching
that one must serve well or burn in hell. To teach unconditional security, in such a manner, would be grounds for dismissal
from the FG arena. Nonetheless, considering the fact that some very good defenders of unconditional security hold to an
exclusively soteriological view of the outer darkness, rejecting their interpretation of the outer darkness as being exegetically
inconsistent is to be preferred over rejecting them as being members of the FG community. The minimum value along this
reward axis, then, must be sought elsewhere.
    Even those FG writers who hold a mistaken view of the outer darkness have sufficiently affirmed the biblical doctrine of
rewards in other ways so as to fall under the FG umbrella. Therefore, the minimum value along this misthological axis would
be found in an affirmation of rewards that clearly distinguishes entrance into heaven from rewards within heaven, even if
these rewards are mistakenly thought to last only for a millennium. Although, comparatively speaking, the writings of those
FG brethren who mistake the outer darkness as dealing exclusively with soteriology will tend to be rather weak in their
misthology, they still retain enough FG misthology so as not to fall into an accursed performatistic soteriology.
    At the opposite end of the spectrum, however, some in the FG camp teach too strong a doctrine of rewards in the form of
kingdom exclusion or metaphysical Gehenna inclusion. Still, they properly regard entrance into the eternal kingdom as a free
gift, affirm unconditional security, and distinguish the free gift of eternal life from misthological dimensions of that life.
Therefore, they should not to be excluded from the FG fellowship since they do not make entrance into the eternal kingdom a
reward. The maximum range for this axis would be found elsewhere, namely in rightly excluding those writers who make
entrance into heaven a reward. Such writers properly are excluded from the FG sphere since they teach conditional security.
