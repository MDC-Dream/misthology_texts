                                                     Parenthetical Asides
   E. V. Rieu, in his, “The Four Gospels: A new translation from the Greek,” actually took a few of John’s editorial asides
out of the text and put them at the bottom of the text in the form of footnotes. Rieu only did this with a few of the most
obvious asides. Merrill Tenney, in his article, “The Footnotes of John’s Gospel” (FJG), lists about 60 such Johannine
footnotes. John O’Rourke, in his article, “Asides in the Gospel of John” (AGJ), lists over a 100 such Johannine footnotes.
The present book notes most of these asides and a few additional ones.
                                                      Books by the Author

1.  The Race of Grace—introductory book in which performance-related themes (such as perseverance) are considered
    essential for rewards in heaven. Entrance into heaven, in contrast, is regarded as free. A strong defense of unconditional
    security derived from believers being given to Jesus is a particular strength of this book.
2. Is Heaven a Gift or a Reward?—while some assert that heaven is a reward in some sense, this book maintains that
    regarding heaven itself as a gift is best.
3. Breaking the Rocking Horse—introductory book regarding the distinction between having eternal life as a gift versus
    obtaining it as a reward. An overview of the outer darkness and an exegetical treatment of the parable of the soils are this
    book’s primary strengths.
4. Mere Christianity and Moral Christianity—a more comprehensive discussion regarding perseverance (and other
    performance-related themes) as being needed for rewards in heaven rather than for entrance into heaven. This book
    provides both introductory material and advanced treatments.
5. Woolly Wolves and Woolless Sheep—a rejection of the notion that profession of salvation is proven genuine by the way a
    person lives. This book focuses on the parable of the sheep and the goats and shows the inadequacy of conditional
    security in interpreting this parable.
6. Sheep and Goats—an advanced discussion of the parable of the sheep and goats that evaluates four interpretations that
    are compatible with unconditional security.
7. Sealed and Secure—a thorough defense of a popular argument for unconditional security that also defends the premise
    of unconditional promises.
8. Fallen from Grace but Not from Perfection—an advanced defense of unconditional security which affirms that believers
    can fall from grace but not fall from perfection.
9. Salvation—a study of how the verb save (sozo) and the noun salvation (soteria) are used in the NT. A comprehensive
    chart of NT usage is provided.
10. Believe—an aspectual defense of unconditional security that examines how the verb believe is used in the Gospel of
    John. A comprehensive chart of metaphors for believe in the Gospel of John is provided also.
11. Carnal Corinth—an affirmation of the categorical existence of carnal Christians within the Corinthian epistles.
12. John in Living Color—a color-colored translation and reference manual of the Gospel of John that focuses on the
    grammatical and aspectual indications of unconditional security.
13. Dad, can I be sure I am saved?—an intermediate discussion of assurance.
14. Trichotomy—an advanced holistic dichotomous discussion of trichotomy with an integration of nikology.
15. Ordo Salutis—a Free Grace order of salvation.
16. Soteric and Mistholic Faith—an advanced discussion of the nature of faith.
17. Karpology—a discussion of the soteriological implications of singular versus plural fruit.
18. Predestination and the Open View—a discussion of predestination that focus on free will and the book of life from an
    open view.
19. Logic Tables and Diagrams—illustrations of logic with scriptural implications pertaining primarily to the soteriological
    and misthological arenas.
20. Drawing and Appointment—an examination of Calvinistic claims regarding drawing and appointment as found
    principally in Jn 6:44 and Acts 13:48.
21. The Outer Darkness—a magnum opus that ties together the author’s arguments for unconditional security and
    conditional rewards.

 Whoever ever: distributive articular present participle.1
 Whosoever ever: pas (all/every) plus a distributive articular present participle.2




 1
   Gnomic articular present participles normally indicate a whoever-ever aspect. As a general rule, whoever ever  will
 be used of singular articular participles where contextually justified, and whoever ever  will be used for plural articular
 participles; whosoever ever  will be used where the syntax is even more suggestive of a distributive aspect. The
 distributive aspect denoted herein will be reserved for those cases that pertain to punctiliar actions regarding individual
 members within the specified class.
 2
   Pas (all/every) plus a gnomic articular present participle is an even more probable indicator that whoever ever is intended.
 Both pas and the definite article have equivalent counterparts that are susceptible to this or some similar rendering.

                                                                 Time
    The indicative mood normally indicates absolute time: past, present, future.3 In contrast, participles are dependent upon
the main verb for their relative sense of time in relation to the main verb. To be sure, exceptions do occur when such matters
as contextual or lexical intrusions are taken into consideration.

                                                              Definitions
Aspect (kind of action) = internal or external or perfective (stative).
Aktionsart = aspectual + lexical + contextual + grammatical considerations.4
Ontological meaning = aspect + indicative time ≠ contextual meaning.

Internal action is generally thought by aspectologists to be portrayed by the present and imperfect tenses and is perceived
mostly as being progressive (i.e., linear, durative) with respect to the corresponding time frame in these two tenses.

External action is generally thought by aspectologists to be portrayed by the aoristic and future tenses in the indicative mood.

Perfective (stative) action-state is portrayed by the perfect and pluperfect tenses and combine the external (summary) action
with a resultant, internal, unchanging, continuous state.

                                                              Symbols
    The traditional differentiation between external and internal aspect has been adopted with modifications herein.
Modifications have been deemed necessary because even traditional grammars provide examples that do not fit into such neat
traditional categories. For example, aoristic presents and stative presents transgress internally into the aspectual fields of the
aorist and perfect when Aktionsart is allowed to factor into consideration of the internal action. Nevertheless, the traditional
viewpoint has not been discarded completely within the present text in that the durative nature of the present and imperfect
have been marked intrinsically, albeit externally rather internally.

                                                        Present and Imperfect
    Because even traditional grammars dynamically modify the internal aspect of the present and imperfect, marking the
internal aspect of these tenses statically is ill-advised. Typical linear expectations for these tense are better associated with a
linear, external aspect in reference to time. Thus, the inner part of the linear outer shell of this aspect will be left open:

                                                           Whosoever Ever
    Many translations render the Greek phrase pas ho plus a participle as whoever. However, ho plus a participle could also
be rendered as whoever. To differentiate between these two constructions, whosoever is used in the present text for the former
construction. The translation herein also implements an additional ever as a gloss to help clarify when these constructions are
being used contextually with a distributive classification so as to denote an inclusion in such a classification by a singular
occurrence of the action entailed. Although whosoever ever might be regarded by some as an over translation, it only makes
explicit what the syntax, in combination with context, already has made implicit. For example, when Jesus says, “Whosoever
ever divorces his wife except for immorality causes her to commit adultery” (Mt 5:32; CT), He is not saying, “Whosoever
continually divorces his wife for immorality causes her to commit adultery.” To suppose that the articular present participle
represents continual action in such a verse would be a serious mistake. Likewise, to assume that a text like Jn 3:16 presumes
that continuous believing is necessary for retaining possession of eternal life would be presumptuous. Because eternal life is
bestowed the moment a person believes, whosoever ever believes has this life even if such a person later were to cease to
believe. In terms of the result, such a person continues to be classified as a believer even if he or she ceases to believe.

perfects in the latter part of 3:18. Although some conditionalists think that these perfects support their case, in actuality these
perfects render the linear interpretation impossible. But let us consider the verse from the conditionalistic perspective first:

means by Lord and by Savior. Christ must be Lord in the objective sense (i.e., be God) in order to be able to grant salvation.
He must also be Lord in the subjective sense in that one must submit to His demand to believe in Him alone for eternal life in
order to be saved soteriologically. Nonetheless, submitting to Him as Lord in terms of one’s performance is not a required for
the free gift of eternal life. Still, He is a believer’s Lord even if the believer does not live in accordance with the reality of that
Lordship, in that the believer has been entrusted with abilities and responsibilities for which he or she must give account to
Lord Jesus. Being saved misthologically will require that a believer live in subjective submission to the Lordship of Christ.

had taught a lesson in which I asked one brain-teaser after another, each requiring a dual answer. Are we to think that Jesus

                                                               Logic
    Logically, my supposition concerning the construct found in verses like Jn 5:29 would be that whenever two mutually
exclusive whoever-ever responses with two mutually whoever-ever exclusive results are intended, they could be described
properly with never. Take this interpretative paraphrase of Mt 7:17-19 for example: “Every good tree produces 
saving faith; but the bad tree produces  non-saving faith. A good tree cannot produce  unsaving faith, nor can a
bad tree produce  saving faith. Every tree that does not produce  saving faith is cut down and thrown into the
fire.” Restated in terms of ever and never, one would have: “Whoever ever produces  saving faith never ever 
produces non-saving faith.” A good tree cannot ever produce bad fruit. Speaking equivalently, a good tree can never produce
bad fruit. Similarly, a bad tree cannot ever produce good fruit. A bad tree can never produce good fruit. As evil is the
negation of good so never is the negation of ever. In the following illustration, let ; = never.



                                                        A = produce good             C = produce bad = ; A

                                                        B = resurrection of life     D = fire/resurrection of judgment = ; B



    Statively speaking, a believer (i.e., a good tree) is whoever ever believes (i.e., A). If you ever believe, you are regarded a
believer. Consequently, you will experience a resurrection of life (A ⇒ B). In contrast, an unbeliever is whoever never
believed (i.e., C). If you never believe, you are regarded an unbeliever. Consequently, you will experience a resurrection of
judgment (C ⇒ D). Again, speaking strictly statively, a believer cannot ever disbelieve (i.e., produce bad fruit). Conversely,
an unbeliever cannot ever believe because whoever ever believes is a believer; whoever never believes is an unbeliever. What
about those who believe but then cease to believe? Logically, they pose no difficulty: Whoever ever believes  is a
believer; whoever never believes  is an unbeliever. The punctiliar response of faith on the part of any given individual
would label that person permanently as a believer. Never responding with a punctiliar response of faith would label a person
permanently as an unbeliever. A person who has ever believed is a believer, not an unbeliever, even if that person ceases to
believe.
    As just shown, a soteriological interpretation of Jn 5:29 is easy to explain. What about a misthological interpretation?
Whoever ever believes and performs the relative good works required for a misthological resurrection will attain that
resurrection. Whoever never believes, and thus cannot perform the good works required for a misthological resurrection, will
experience soteriological condemnation. The possibility of a misthological resurrection that is conditioned jointly on (linear)
faith and on (relatively) good works presupposes the certainty of a soteriological resurrection that is conditioned on
(punctiliar) faith alone (Jn 5:24).

    Similar statements regarding different vantage points for Jn 5:29 might be made about Jn 10:27. As explained in The Race
of Grace, this verse may be interpreted from four harmonious aspectual points of view, each of which is compatible with
unconditional security. Within the confines of GJ, however, the distributive is preferable and marked accordingly herein,
being understood as punctiliar for each individual. The context is most emphatic regarding the security of each sheep. Yet the
hint is made that more than just security is available to the sheep, and thus a secondary misthological possibility cannot be
ruled out in the fuller context of the NT. Multiple vantage points are possible, but the distributive is most probable.

    A wooden aspectual marking of this verse would yield: “While you have the light, believe £ in the light, in order that

                                                               Jn 12:37
    Some aspectologists would render the negative imperfect in this verse as: “But even though He had performed so many
signs before them, they kept on not believing  in Him.” The linear aspect is maintained in the negation. They
persisted in disbelieving Jesus. The verse does not present positive linear belief as a soteriological necessity. To the contrary,
their problem is that they never believed. Some translations suggest this linear disbelief (resulting in a failure to believe even
once) by interjecting yet or still into the verse. An alpha privative (i.e., a negative prefix for disbelieve or unbelief) would be
even clearer: “But even though He had performed so many signs before them, yet they kept on disbelieving  in Him.”
They remained unbelieving . However, rather than negatively implement such glosses to denote linear disbelief, the
translation implemented within the present text positively notes their failure to believe  even once. Although either
approach is accurate, preference has been given to the latter rendering. Linear disbelief is a failure to believe even once. John
is denying that they believed even once.
    For example, John informs us that some believers were not confessing  Jesus. Alternatively, it might be said that
they were not confessing  Jesus. Their linear refusal to confess Jesus was not punctured by even a singular instance of
public profession (Jn 12:42). Likewise, Jesus did not entrust  Himself to such believers (Jn 2:24). Or it might be
restated: Jesus was not entrusting  Himself to them. The imperfect is also used to show the persistent disbelief on the
part of Jesus’ brothers: “Even His brothers were not believing  in Him” (Jn 7:5). Or stated positively, “Not even His
brothers believed  in Him.” Even a singular positive response of saving faith would have broken their linear response
of disbelief.
