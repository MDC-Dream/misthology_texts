                               Prologue
        “There is no such thing as carnal Christians!” was the bold claim
made by the guest speaker at an FCA breakfast which I attended with my
wife and two sons. The guest speaker challenged anyone who thought
otherwise to email him. I certainly had every intention of doing so, but my
wife and children did not want me to make waves. This guest speaker was
the youth leader at the nearby church which we had just started attending
at the time, and my family did not want me to cause problems that would
make us unwelcome in the church. After all, we had started attending that
church because of the strong FCA presence there, and without a doubt
FCA was the best thing going at our local high school. Also, my
experience has been that when I ask those in the ministry to follow the
example of the noble-minded Bereans and examine such claims with me
from the Bible, they refuse. So for the sake of my children being able to
participate in the FCA programs with their friends at the local church, I bit
my tongue, held my peace, and kept my opinions to myself, only
expressing them to my family.
        We continued to attend that church, and my children continued to
attend the FCA. During that time, it became increasingly apparent that this
church was conditioning entrance into Heaven on a believer’s
performance. One of the earliest indications of this conditionalistic
mindset came in a Sunday School class that I and my wife were
subsequently attending at the church mentioned above. I suggested an
interpretation of the narrow gate in Matthew’s Sermon on the Mount that
would have been harmonious with an affirmation of unconditional
security. The teacher of the class responded that she did not believe in
eternal security and appealed to another lady in the class who concurred
with her in her denial of eternal security. This member emphatically
denied the existence of carnal Christians.
        This was the circumstance that led me to write the rough draft of the
present book years ago. Originally, it was to be part of my magnum opus,
The Outer Darkness. However, to reduce that major work to two volumes,
I had to remove the material pertaining to Carnal Corinth out of the
manuscript and place it in a standalone release. Unfortunately, various
writing projects prevented me from releasing this material until now. My
children are now grown. They are doing well in their walk with the Lord.
For I have been able to demonstrate to them the fallacy of the above
mentality so that they do not fall into legality. Legalistic Christianity is not
a healthy place to live. May this book help others who are in legalistic
churches. May the truth set you free.
       Both proveitists (such as the youth minister) and forfeitists (such as
those in the Sunday School class) are legalists. They launch vehement
attacks against what they call the carnal Christian theory because their
conditioning entrance into Heaven on a believer’s performance would be
very difficult if they acknowledged that those believers who live in a
carnal manner are allowed into Heaven. Therefore, performatists (of both
the proveitistic and forfeitistic persuasion) are loath to admit that carnal
Christians are allowed into Heaven.*




*
 Forfeitist: an advocate of conditional security who believes that those
who do not persevere forfeit (i.e., lose) the gift of eternal life.
     Legalist: someone who teaches that keeping the law is necessary for
salvation from Hell. Whether it is the OT or NT commands being insisted
upon makes no difference. Advocates may not even acknowledge that they
are teaching that keeping the law is necessary. Still, their conditioning
entrance into Heaven on performance unmasks their essential error.
Hyper-legalism adds insult to injury by additionally teaching that keeping
the OT law is necessary for NT sanctification.
     Performatist: someone who believes that God-enabled, good
performance on the believer’s part is necessary for entrance into Heaven.
     Proveitist: an advocate of conditional security who believes that fruit
is necessary proof of salvation: If you do not have this fruit, then you were
never saved to start with. Perseverance is necessary to prove that you are
saved and to enter Heaven. Thus, entrance into Heaven is conditioned on
the fruit of perseverance.

      Introduction
        Certainly, it comes as no surprise when those who deny eternal
security deny the existence of carnal Christians. Carnal security, as it is
slanderously called, is anathematized by denying the very existence of
carnal Christians. For carnal Christians have committed moral apostasy
even if they have not yet committed theological apostasy. Supposedly,
because their carnal faith has died, it will no longer save them from Hell
(Jam 2:14-16). You can certainly tell whether they are still saved by the
fruit they bear (Mt 7:13-23). Such is the popular mentality of those who
deny the existence of carnal Christians from the Arminian camp.
Surprisingly to some perhaps, these arguments are not that much different
from those who claim to believe in eternal security but deny the existence
of carnal Christians.
        The youth minister, in fact, who spoke at the FCA breakfast from a
Reformed perspective, used these two passages in a similar fashion to deny
the existence of carnal Christians. With a slight modification of the
Arminian verbiage above, those from the Reformed camp come to the
same determination: Because carnal faith of carnal Christians is dead, it
will not save them from Hell (Jam 2:14-16). You can certainly tell whether
they are saved by the fruit they bear (Mt 7:13-23). This Reformed
mentality, just like the Arminian perspective, denies unconditional
security by making salvation from the flames of Hell conditioned on how
carnal the professing believer is. Whereas an Arminian would say you are
not saved, those in the Reformed camp would say you are not really saved.
Regardless, from the perspective of either camp, what really determines
whether a believer will make it to Heaven is how well that believer lives.
        Advocates of unconditional security take a different approach,
which does not condition your salvation from Hell on how good you are.
As for Jam 2:14-16, the passage teaches carnal Christians will not be saved
from a merciless judgment at the Judgment Seat of Christ (Jam 2:13). The
context has nothing to do with salvation from Hell. Conversely, Mt 7:13-
23 is teaching that those who think that how good they are has something
to do with their going to Heaven will be sent to Hell. For the most part,
those consigning carnal believers to Hell are sentencing themselves to Hell
instead by demonstrating a works-righteous attitude. Contrastively, carnal
believers who cling to the cross as their only hope of making it to Heaven
will indeed make it, while many self-righteous pseudo Lordship

  Lordship Salvationists are those who maintain that some form or degree
of surrender to Christ as Lord of one’s life is biblically necessary for
salvation from eternal damnation. Actually, from an exclusively logical
and biblical standpoint, those (such as myself) who are opposed to today’s
so-called Lordship Salvation affirm that the only way to be saved from
Hell is to submit to the Lordship of Christ in terms of believing in Christ
for eternal life. The Lord has commanded that we believe in Him for
eternal life. Therefore, to believe in the Lord Jesus for eternal life is to
submit to His lordship in terms of what is necessary for having eternal life.
Genuine Lordship Salvationists correctly insist that one must submit to the
Lord’s will as it pertains to having eternal life by believing in Jesus (rather
than in one’s performance) for eternal life.
         As a theological term, however, the designation Lordship
Salvation has come to be associated with those who condition salvation
from eternal damnation on one’s performance (or at least on one’s intent
to change one’s performance). They frequently will be referred to as
pseudo Lordship Salvationists in this book because they refuse to submit
to what the Lord actually demands for this type of salvation—simple faith.
Because of the popular association of the term Lordship Salvationists with
these false advocates of Lordship Salvation, however, unless noted
otherwise, the popular association will be adopted by default so that the
unqualified term Lordship Salvation will be understood herein in the
popular (rather than biblical sense) as denoting performance-based
salvation.

       Corner’s Credit
       To Corner’s credit, in his popular Arminian treatment of this
passage, he tries to discredit the existence of carnal Christians by
appealing to the immediate and extended context before proceeding to
passages outside of Corinthians.1 In turn, the treatment in this present work
likewise focuses on the Corinthian epistles. The other primary proof texts
and arguments presented by Corner are dealt with much more thoroughly
in The Outer Darkness (and my other related books). A cursory response
at this juncture to one such prooftext will be allowed, though, since it
pertains to a Corinthian passage. Corner thinks that Paul’s statement in
1Cor 6:9 makes carnality practically impossible. To summarize his
argument syllogistically,
               Illustration 1. Warning to Carnal Christians
       1. The unrighteous will not inherit the kingdom.
       2. Carnal Christians are unrighteous.
       3. Therefore, carnal Christians will not inherit the kingdom.

        Despite contenders to the contrary, this aspect of Corner’s argument
is sound. Those believers who are morally unrighteous will not inherit the
kingdom. The problem for Corner’s position, however, is that his
categorical rejection of carnal Christians is based on a jump in logic.
According to Paul, inheriting the kingdom is an issue dealing with rewards
in Heaven, not entrance into Heaven (Col 3:23-24). Seen from Paul’s
perspective, this passage is very detrimental to Corner’s perspective. Paul
is warning these carnal Christians that they will fail to inherit the kingdom
if they do not clean up their act. Their actions will determine whether these
Corinthian believers inherit the kingdom. Since entrance into the kingdom
is a gift rather than a reward, it stands to reason that their entrance into the
kingdom is not in jeopardy. Paul certainly stresses in 1Cor 3:11-15 that
believers can lose all potential rewards. Since inheriting the kingdom is
one such potential reward, it must be concluded that Paul is warning these
Page 16                                                      Carnal Corinth
believers that they are in danger of forfeiting their potential inheritance of
the kingdom. Nevertheless, as Paul concludes, those believers would still
be saved even if they were to lose all potential rewards (1Cor 3:15).
Therefore, an appeal to 1Cor 6:9 boomerangs on those trying to use it in
order to deny the existence of carnal Christians.

      Corner’s Failure
       With that observation in mind, Corner’s feeble attempt to limit
carnality in 1Cor 3:1-3 to jealousy and strife falls apart in light of the
broader context. Paul is just getting started when he points out that these
believers are characterized by jealousy and strife. Still, let us pause here
for a moment and note that even if this were the full extent of Paul’s
accusation against these believers, it would be a sufficient basis to
conclude that they would not inherit the kingdom. Paul clearly warns
believers that those characterized by jealousy and strife will not inherit the
kingdom (Gal 5:19-21). If a carnal Christian is simply defined as a
Christian whose moral state is insufficient to result in his or her receiving
the reward of inheriting the kingdom, then my point is proven. Apparently,
Corner’s argument would suggest that you can get by with being
characterized by jealousy and strife and not be concerned by such trivial
degrees of carnality, in his opinion, because only more blatant forms of
carnality truly qualify a believer as being carnal. But one cannot limit
carnality to gross immorality and be true to Paul’s thought or his warnings.

      Paul’s Divisions
       Paul divides humanity in 1Cor 3:1-3 into three categories: natural,
carnal, and spiritual. Those classified as natural are most readily
identified as unbelievers. Those designated as natural are outside the
kingdom, and Christ is outside their lives. When a person trusts in Christ
for eternal life, Christ comes into the believer’s life and gives him or her
spiritual life. The person is transformed instantaneously and completely
into a new creature—a new creation. If this new believer is taught how to
experience the victorious Christian life, he or she may experience the
abundant life here and now and in due time become spiritual.
Unfortunately, a popular gospel tract, which presents similar circle
diagrams as those shown below, gives the lost person the potentially false
impression that it is possible to go from natural to spiritual in an instant.
But this is impossible. Granted, some who are converted get an immediate
taste of the abundant life. However, even they take time to mature.
Regrettably, that popular gospel tract only presents two options to the lost
reader: to be natural or immediately become spiritual. To its credit,

        Because Paul’s discussion and his expected transition to spirituality
is highly dependent upon the Holy Spirit, an enhanced drawing may be
preferred. The basic drawing has been changed to include the Holy Spirit,
pictured as a dove in the second illustration. If the lost trust in Christ for
eternal life before they die, then, at that very moment, Christ will come
into them and permanently give them eternal life, even though they have
not yet surrendered the control of their lives to Christ. They are thus carnal
at the initial point of conversion. They are sealed with the Holy Spirit, to
be sure, (2Cor 1:22; Eph 1:13), but He is not dominant in their lives.
Christ, and thus the Holy Spirit, will never leave them (Heb 13:5), even if
they grieve the Holy Spirit by failing to submit to the Lordship of Christ
(Eph 4:30). Still, if they die in this carnal state, they will be cast into the
outer darkness (which is inside of the kingdom). They will be in the
kingdom but miss the rewards reserved for those believers who progress
to spiritual maturity.
        Alternatively, if after trusting in Christ for eternal life they (by the
power of the Holy Spirit) move on to surrender the control of their lives to
the Lordship of Christ and learn to live the Spirit-filled life, then they will
have abundant life now in the present (Jn 10:10). Given time, they will
become what Paul wishes the Corinthians were—spiritual. Spirit-filled
believers manifest the fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:22-23). They will have an
abundant entrance into the kingdom in the future (2Pet 1:11). Not only will
they be in the kingdom, in other words, but they will rule the kingdom.
        They cannot bypass the carnal stage and go directly to being
spiritual any more than they can bypass spiritual infancy and go directly
to spiritual maturity. Experiential knowledge is necessary to move from
carnal to spiritual. Putting what you know intellectually into experiential
practice over a period of time is required to gain true spirituality. Not even
Paul could enable the Corinthian believers to bypass the carnal stage. If
they were not able to bypass carnality under Paul’s tutorage, assuming that
those whom we evangelize can bypass infancy because we draw a few
circles is arrogant. Certainly, such illustrations can be very helpful, but
they must be conveyed with a strong sense of realism and accurate
explanation. If presented in the logical and biblical order, as in the above
illustration, in which the carnal stage is a necessary precursor to becoming
spiritual, then these types of diagrams become truly useful.

       Inevitability of Carnality
       When my oldest son, Jeremy, learned how to ride his bicycle by
using training wheels, I was very reluctant to take those training wheels
off, remembering the bloody knees of my own childhood when I first
learned how to ride. Jeremy’s training wheels were left on until they were
Chapter 1. Carnal Christians?                                         Page 19
literally worn off the bike. The training had paid off. When the training
wheels finally came off his bike, he had no bloody knees. Jeremy rode
flawlessly and was given a new bike as a result. His old bike was then
given to his younger brother, Jonathan, who had seen his older brother ride
for months using those training wheels. Jonathan had a thorough
understanding of how to ride from watching his older brother. And now
that the training wheels had been taken off, the old bike was now his. Did
I tell Jonathan that he could hop on the bike and ride it without training
wheels? Not hardly. Newer and even sturdier training wheels were put on

       Reality of Carnality
       Theoretically, all believers will press on to maturity and advance
from carnality to maturity regardless of the reason they came to Christ.
Realistically and biblically speaking, on the other hand, it is only a
hypothetical possibility. Likewise, speaking theoretically, perhaps in next
year’s basketball season no one playing in the NBA will miss a single jump
shot and will make all their free throws. After all, they are professionals!
Such expectations would be foolish, however. Likewise, to think that all
Christians will mature because they are genuine believers is delusional.
Paul goes on to say that some believers are so carnal that all their works
are represented as going up in smoke at the Judgment Seat of Christ. They
will fail miserably in terms of receiving Heavenly rewards. Yet, in that
same passage, Paul affirms that such Christians are nevertheless true
believers and are saved (1Cor 3:11-15).

      Introduction
       As a child, I would attend R.A. (Royal Ambassador) meetings and
sing, “We’re royal ambassadors, ambassadors of Christ.” The common
assumption was that all believers are ambassadors. After all, in 2Cor 5:20
Paul plainly says in black and white that we are ambassadors for Christ.
Maybe some believers are better ambassadors than others, but we are all
ambassadors and should try to be the very best royal ambassadors we can
be—such was my reasoning as a child.
       However, upon growing up, it became necessary to abandon this
childish interpretation. While attending an adult Sunday School class at a
church that promotes Lordship Salvation, it was very apparent that not all
adults are willing to abandon childish views, however. They interpreted
2Cor 5:17 as saying that all believers live like new creatures: “Therefore
if any man is in Christ, he is a new creature; the old things passed away;
behold, new things have come.” Their underlying assumption was an
inversion: If you do not live like a new creature, then you are not a new
creature. Or as one LS evangelist expressed it, “If Christ is on the inside,
there will be a change on the outside.” The implied inversion was: If there
is no change on the outside, then there was no change on the inside. The
way you act on the outside proves whether Christ is on the inside.
       From this perspective, the way you live determines whether you are
really saved. Fruit proves root. Consequently, your life will determine
whether you have eternal life. Ultimately, for proveitistic theology, the
way you live determines whether you go to Heaven or Hell.* Those
holding the popular Lordship point of view would assume that 2Cor 5:20
proves that all believers are ambassadors for Christ to the lost: “Therefore,
we are ambassadors for Christ, as though God were entreating through us;
we beg you on behalf of Christ, be reconciled to God.” The standard
assumption is that the you refers to the lost who need to be reconciled to
God. When the teacher of the Sunday School class finally paused and
asked if there were any comments, I posed a simple question: “To whom
does the you refer?” Who is Paul addressing when he says, “We beg you
on behalf of Christ, be reconciled to God”? Who is being begged to be
reconciled?

believers (4 times), not unbelievers (8 times). In total, Paul assures the
readers that they are genuine believers over a 100 times. If Paul has failed
to convince modern theologians of the Corinthians’ salvation, it is not due
to ambiguity on his part.

      Exhortations
       Based on the broad context, Deffinbaugh’s assumption concerning
2Cor 13:5 is immediately thrown into question. How can Paul use their
behavior to call their salvation into question when he has been affirming
their salvation despite their behavior? To base their salvation on their
behavior would destroy the Pauline basis for behavior modification. In
other words, it would put the cart before the horse. Not only does
Deffinbaugh’s assumption result in a faulty doctrine of assurance; it also

results in a false doctrine of salvation. If your behavior determines whether
you are saved, then your behavior determines whether you will be saved.
Salvation ultimately becomes dependent on works in Calvinism.

        Specific Passages

        2Cor 13:5
       An examination of the specific details of 2Cor 13:5 also causes me
to reject Deffinbaugh’s assumption. Paul calls upon the Corinthians to test
and examine themselves in this verse, but nowhere else in these epistles
does Paul use these words to denote or determine whether a person is a
believer: “Test yourselves to see if you are in the faith; examine
yourselves! Or do you not recognize this about yourselves, that Jesus
Christ is in you—unless indeed you fail the test?”3
       The issue was spirituality, not spiritual birth. Paul already had given
them ample reasons for concluding that they were regenerate but
disqualified from being considered spiritual. He had claimed that he and
his associates (fellow apostles) were spiritual and had spiritual thoughts
and words (1Cor 2:13-16). This plural reference to those associated with
his apostolic ministry is known as an apostolic we.* Next, he immediately
contrasts his spirituality, and that of his fellow workers, with the
Corinthian believers. He denies that the Corinthians are spiritual, asserting
that they are carnal instead (1Cor 3:1-3).
       The expression in you must be understood in terms of both the
immediate and broad context. Paul has been defending his ministry
throughout these letters to these readers. They want proof of his
apostleship (e.g., 1Cor 9:1ff; 2Cor 12:11ff), that is, proof that Christ was
working and speaking in him (1Cor 9:3). Paul, in turn, turns the tables on
them and wants proof that Christ was working and speaking in them. They
mistakenly had assumed that because they were exercising spiritual gifts
they were spiritual (1Cor 14:37). However, carnal Christians can exercise
spiritual gifts, but this does not make them spiritual. Paul wants real proof
of their supposed spirituality. He was not asking for proof of their
regeneration. As to the expression in the faith, when it is used elsewhere
in these epistles (or the NT for that matter), it always refers to the quality
of one’s experience in the faith, not to whether or not one simply has faith
(1Cor 16:13; 2Cor 8:7).

       Exhortation
       The broad context, immediate context, and clear implication of
2Cor 5:20 are typically ignored because of the nature of the exhortation.
Those addressed as you are being urged to be reconciled to God. So it is
assumed that they must be lost. After all, verse 19 talks about the
reconciliation of the whole world and defines this reconciliation in terms
of forgiveness of sins. Thus, it would be natural to assume that those who
need to be reconciled are lost people in the world, who according to verse
21 need to have righteousness imputed to them. Romans 5:10-11 makes
an excellent cross-reference for this point of view since, according to
Paul’s statement there, all believers already have been reconciled, and this
reconciliation is equated with the justification of all believers. Likewise,
in Col 1:22 Paul used a compound form of this Greek word to say that all
believers are reconciled.

      Three types of Reconciliation
       To explain how we can be both—reconciled before we became
believers and even more so after we become believers—I adopt the
following explanation: Christ’s death has reconciled everyone
potentially; it has reconciled carnal believers positionally; it has
reconciled spiritual believers practically. Paul makes it clear in the
Colossian context that being reconciled potentially and even positionally
did not inevitably mean that the believer would be able to come before the
Lord holy, blameless, or complete (Col 1:22,28).* Being able to come
before the Lord in this manner would be dependent on their performance
(Col 1:23). Paul does not take it for granted that all believers would
inevitably be presented in this manner. On the contrary, Paul diligently
works for this goal (Col 1:28). The ultimate goal of Christ’s reconciliation
is maturity in holiness. Such experiential reconciliation is dependent on
our practice; it is conditional, not inevitable.
       Jesus speaks of the need for brother to be reconciled with brother
(Mt 5:24). In 1Cor 7:11, Paul uses the same Greek word for reconciliation
that he uses in 2Cor 5:20, but in 1Cor 7:11 he instructs Christian wives
regarding being reconciled to their husbands. If Paul can exhort believers
to be reconciled to their spouses in First Corinthians, then why can he not
exhort believers to be reconciled to their Father in Second Corinthians?
Relationship does not guarantee fellowship. The lost need to be reconciled
to their Creator; the saved need to be reconciled to their Father. Just as the
NT can exhort both the lost and saved to repent and thus get right with
God, it can exhort both the lost and saved to be reconciled to God.

      Imperative
       Just as context determines whether the exhortation to repent is
addressed to believers or unbelievers, so the context of the Corinthians
epistles must be consulted to determine whether believers are being
exhorted to be reconciled to God. This exhortation is an imperative. An
examination of the approximately 140 occurrences of the imperative in
these two epistles reveals that none of them are addressed to unbelievers.
Since all other exhortations in these epistles are addressed to believers, the
usage argues overwhelmingly that this exhortation to be reconciled to God
is likewise addressed to believers.

      Summation
       At the conclusion of the Sunday School class, the author was
surprised to find that a number of its members were able to see his point.
Not all believers are ambassadors according to 2Cor 5:20. This verse
clearly pictures one group of believers urging another group of believers
to be reconciled to God. The remaining members of that Sunday School,
who were determined to hang on to their traditional understanding
regardless of the biblical evidence, were left with the following question.
“Is every American citizen an American ambassador?” Those in the class
who had been persuaded by the context immediately answered, “No.” The
obvious summation from this analogy was then given: “Nor is every
citizen of Heaven an ambassador of Heaven.”

      Analogy
       Carnal believers cannot be ambassadors for Heaven any more than
American kindergarten children can be ambassadors for America. We look
to our graduate schools rather than our grade schools for ambassadors to
represent us. We do not look to our prisons for our ambassadors. Nor
should we look to those who are under church discipline as ambassadors
to represent the church.

      Carnality
       It should come as no surprise that these Corinthian believers needed
to be reconciled to God. Paul had said these believers were carnal,
arrogant, boastful, wicked, suing one another, suing one another, sinning
against one another, having sex with prostitutes, depriving their spouses
of sex, abusing the Lord’s Supper, dying prematurely under God’s
discipline, foolishly denying the physical resurrection of believers,
embracing false apostles, being skeptical of Paul’s apostleship, engulfed
in divisions and factions, in danger of receiving no rewards and of being
saved through fire. Paul did not hesitate to confront these believers with
their sin, and he did not shun his responsibility to say they were not
reconciled in terms of their behavior. Judicial reconciliation had not
resulted in familial reconciliation. But in spite of all their faults, Paul
affirms their salvation over a hundred times and never calls it into
question.

      Prodigal Brother
       In this very passage, Paul affirms that, as a result of their being in
Christ, they are positionally and ontologically new creatures. Every
Page 50                                                      Carnal Corinth
believer is in Christ; every believer is a new creature. These are absolute
statements. You are either in Christ, or you are not. You are either a new
creature, or you are not. Unfortunately, being a new creature in Christ had
not resulted in an experiential confirmation. Paul begs these new creatures
to be reconciled to God, to become in practice what they are in position
and by new birth.
       When we have a prodigal brother who has gone into the far country
and chosen to dine with the swine, we are not to be self-righteous
Arminians who stay at home and tell our Father, “He is no longer worthy
to be called your son.” Nor are we to be self-righteous Calvinists who tell
our Father, “He is no brother of mine. He was never your son to start with.
If he really was your son, then he would have stayed home.” If we are
going to be ambassadors of God’s grace, we must get up out of our
theological easy chair, go into the far country, and beg our brother, “Come,
home. Be reconciled with Dad.”

       Interpretation #3. Saved People in the Church

herein is a synthesis of the previous two explanations and understands Paul

to be reconciled experientially with God. My position is that experiential
reconciliation is the best interpretation.

      Experiential Righteousness
       As believers we already have peace, justification, and
reconciliation. These synonymous terms refer to our soteriological
position. However, this does not mean Paul cannot use these terms to refer
to our experience. Paul says we are justified (dikaioo)—declared
righteous. Since believers are already positionally righteous, one would
assume this means that they cannot be exhorted to be righteous or pursue
righteousness. Yet this is far from the case, as a cursory examination of its
cognates demonstrates. Paul uses the nominal cognate (dikaiosune) to
exhort believers to be righteous (Rom 6:13,16-19; 14:16-17; Eph 4:24;
5:8-9) and pursue righteousness (1Tim 6:11; 2Tim 2:22). You are
righteous so be righteous, is Paul’s point. Be what you are, in other words.
Paul uses the adjectival cognate (dikaios) soteriologically to describe
being made righteous by Christ’s obedience (Rom 5:19). But he also uses
it to describe our experiential righteousness based on our obedience
(Eph 6:1; Tit 1:8). And I already have presented evidence elsewhere that,
although Abraham’s simple faith was soteriologically imputed to him as
righteousness in Rom 4:1-11, it was his mature faith that was

dualistic righteousness is available to those who believe (Rom 4:24).†

      Before God
        The supposed dichotomy between James and Paul on the matter of
justification by works would be largely resolved by the simple recognition
that justification/righteousness can be used either positionally or
experientially and hence soteriologically or misthologically. James uses
the verb to refer to experiential righteousness. Although Paul prefers to
use the verb to refer to positional righteousness, he nevertheless allows in
Rom 4:2 that it can refer to experiential righteousness. What Paul denies
in that context is that this practical righteousness by works results in
positional righteousness. And in this particular verse he denies that either
those justified by works or by faith have a right to boast before God: “For
if Abraham was justified by works, he has something to boast about; but
not before God.” The indication is that if Abraham was justified by works,
he would have something to boast about. But even then, he would not be
able to boast before God (at least not in this sense).
        The simplistic assumption that there is no sense in which we are
justified by works is ruled out by James. James is clear that there is a sense
in which believers can be justified by works. And the corresponding notion
that there is no sense in which we can be justified by works before God is
a premature assessment implicitly rejected by Paul in this context and
explicitly so in other contexts.‡ Even if Abraham was justified by works,
he would still not be able to boast before God in the sense intended in this
passage. But this is not to say that if Abraham were justified by works he
could not boast in some other sense of the word, as Paul implies when he
states: “For by grace you have been saved through faith; and that not of
yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, that no one
should boast” (Eph 2:8-9). The inverse implication of Paul’s statement
would be that if the benefit were a result of works, one could boast.

       Regardless as to whether the justification is soteriological or

       Discussions of reconciliation generally stress that God is the

      Tense
        A further point of interest concerning these three exhortations is
they all use the same tense (aorist imperative). Any assumption that the
aorist imperative must indicate a simple punctiliar action is mistaken.16 In
1Cor 7:11, the reconciliation may involve a process. In Mt 5:23-24 it
involves a double process. Being reconciled to God requires not only
presenting the sacrifice but also going and doing what is necessary to be
reconciled with one’s brother as well. Thus, the fact that the aorist
infinitive in 2Cor 5:21 is punctiliar does not prove the aorist imperative in
2Cor 5:20 is punctiliar. Both the nature of the verb and its tense
demonstrate it may refer to linear reconciliation.
        Since the reconciliation in Mt 5:23-24 intertwines the horizontal
and vertical dimensions, it demonstrates the reality of both practical and
theological reconciliation. This theological reconciliation is linear rather
than punctiliar.* Admittedly, this is not a Pauline example of
reconciliation; however, its related compound apallasso is used
synonymously by Paul’s traveling companion in Lk 12:58. And the
relationship between Mt 5:23-25 and this passage is strong.

       In short, when in 2Cor 8:18 Paul says that God reconciled (1) us to
Himself and gave (2) us the ministry of reconciliation, the first us refers to
all believers, the second us does not. Since the ministry of reconciliation
was given to Paul and his associates rather than believers in general, not
all believers are ambassadors of God’s reconciliation. If Paul does not
need to distinguish an inclusive we from an exclusive we grammatically in
v. 18, certainly he does not need to be redundant in distinguishing between
you and we grammatically in v. 20. The pronoun in 6:1 is necessary to
denote the subject of the infinitive. A pronoun is not necessary in v. 20,
since the second-person-plural verb clearly denotes second-person-plural
reference. Paul does not need to say you with a pronoun when he already
has said you with the verb.
       This is not to say that Paul never uses this pronoun-verb
combination in the manner Wilkin describes. Indeed, Paul does use it in
three places in these epistles (1Cor 10:15; 16:1; 2Cor 6:13).21 However,
Paul uses this verb tense (2nd pl. imperative) 70 times in these epistles. This
means that the remaining 67 times Paul uses this verb tense, he does not
use this pronoun to state the you, since it already is understood from the
verb. In every one of these other passages where Paul uses this verb tense
without the pronoun, he is addressing the readers. Since the absence of the
pronoun does not call the identity of those being addressed into question
anywhere else, Wilkin’s FG interpretation is guilty of special pleading.
Everywhere else this tense is used in the Corinthian epistles, it is used to
address the readers. Thus, the LS interpretation is correct in understanding
that the exhortation is addressed to the readers. However, the LS
interpretation errs when it assumes this means that the readers Paul is
addressing are lost. As was demonstrated above, Paul never calls the
salvation of the readers into question. In contrast to the special pleading
employed by either the first or second interpretation, the third
interpretation is built upon Paul’s well-established usage of exclusively
employing this verb tense to address saved readers in the Corinthian
epistles.

      Conclusion
       The first interpretation argues the exhortation is addressed to the
lost within the church. The second interpretation maintains it is addressed
to the lost outside the church. Both interpretations are guilty of special
pleading since everywhere else in these epistles the exhortations are
addressed to the saved within the church. Thus, the third interpretation is
the only one not guilty of special pleading.
       Admittedly, on the other few remaining occasions when Paul refers
to theological reconciliation, he proclaims a soteriological reconciliation.
But 2Cor 5:20 is an exhortation rather than proclamation, so it does not
really match the soteriological parallels. Also, soteriological reconciliation
already has been accomplished for both the lost and the saved, which again
is not the case in 2Cor 5:20. As a result, this verse is being used in a
distinctively different manner than the few other isolated instances Paul
uses it. The third interpretation correctly recognizes that 2Cor 5:20 is a
special Pauline case and yet does so without employing special pleading
since it interprets the you in a manner consistent with Pauline usage. This
alternative explanation interprets the exhortation to be theologically
reconciled in a manner consistent with NT usage. And it interprets the
exhortation to be generically reconciled in a manner consistent with
Pauline usage.
       Since Paul states that both the lost and saved are already
theologically reconciled in these extraneous passages, it would be
impossible to be dogmatic in determining from those passages who is
being exhorted to be reconciled in 5:20. The fact that he exhorts believers
to be reconciled in a non-theological context may incline us to suspect that
he could likewise urge believers to be reconciled in a theological context
as well. This inclination is supported by the additional observation that
exhortations for theological reconciliation are directed toward believers
elsewhere in the NT. But what clinches the argument for the third
interpretation is that every second person plural exhortation in the
Corinthian epistles is addressed to readers who already are saved. This
passage is no exception.
       The difference between believers and unbelievers is that believers
believe they have been reconciled; unbelievers do not believe they have
been reconciled: believers believe; unbelievers do not believe. Such a
conclusion is rather intuitive. As to how reconciliation can be
accomplished before faith occurs, we observed that as a result of the
believer’s belief, the judicial reconciliation/forgiveness (which was the
Page 74                                                     Carnal Corinth
believer’s potentially and provisionally before he or she became a
believer) has now become his or her positionally once he or she becomes
a believer. When believers live consistent with their position (i.e., walk in
the light), they have experiential reconciliation/forgiveness with God (e.g.,
1Jn 1:7-9). In answer to the question, “Who needs reconciliation?” it must
be concluded that both nonbelievers and believers need reconciliation. But
if the question be further refined to, “Who needs reconciliation in
2Cor 5:20?” the answer must be that it is believers who need reconciliation
in this passage. This exhortation to be reconciled is addressed to believers.
As believers, they already have moved from potential to positional
reconciliation. Now, they need to move on to practical reconciliation by
becoming obedient believers.

                             Introduction
       As described previously, there are two basic ways to approach the
context of this passage.* Basically, the question revolves around to whom
does Paul refer as being spiritual in the context of 1Cor 2:13. Some in LS
may see justification for concluding that all believers are spiritual. In
contrast, those within the FG camp may find it more reasonable to
conclude that not all believers are spiritual in the sense described in this
passage. Whether the we in this passage is inclusive or exclusive is a major
consideration and has been examined already on the broad scale.† Before
commencing a more detailed examination of the passage in general, and
of 1Cor 2:13 in particular, it will be helpful to consider the evidence that
Paul along with other NT authors considered their writings to be Scripture.
       In 2Pet 3:16, Peter classifies Paul’s epistles as Scripture. Carson
surmises, “This is an indication that very early in the history of the church
Paul’s epistles were considered to be God’s written word in the same sense
as the Old Testament texts.”1 In 1Tim 5:18 Paul apparently quotes from
the Gospel of Luke (10:7) and refers to it as Scripture. Paul and Luke were
friends, and Luke had been with Paul during his first imprisonment in
Rome. According to Hendriksen, “It is not impossible that Luke’s Gospel
had just been brought to completion. Hence, if that be true, the apostle was
able to quote from it.”2 This latter statement by Hendriksen is all the more
likely in view of the evidence for an early date for the writing of Luke.

       With this background in mind, one can properly assess Paul’s
statements in chapter 7 of First Corinthians. Some would say that Paul’s
statement in 7:6 shows that Paul’s instruction only has the authority of an
opinion: “But this I say by way of concession, not of command.” To the
contrary, Lenski’s remark is accurate: “Exceptions are properly called
concessions.”5 Everyone is familiar with the famous exception clause
Jesus gives concerning divorce in Mt 5:32. Whether it is a temporary
concession to the human culture or a more permanent concession to the
human condition, the point still remains that concessions can be an
expression of God’s permissive will. A concession does not prove lack of
authority. To label Paul’s concession as a mere human opinion is an
imposition.
       In 1Cor 7:10, Paul has a command from Jesus’ earthly ministry,
which is why he says, “I give instructions, not I, but the Lord, that the
wife should not leave her husband.” But in 1Cor 7:12 he does not have a
command from Jesus’ earthly ministry so he says, “But to the rest I say,
not the Lord, that if any brother has a wife who is an unbeliever, and she
consents to live with him, let him not send her away.” He is clearly not
disclaiming the Lord’s authority for what he says, as is apparent from what
he wrote later in the epistle: “The things which I write to you are the Lord’s
commandment” (1Cor 14:37). Paul’s command has the same authority as
that of the Lord Jesus. Paul’s statement is not a disclaimer of revelation
but an indicator of distinction within the revelation given to him between
what is original with him versus simply repeated by him.
       Some would understand Paul in 1Cor 7:25 as merely giving a
human opinion: “Now concerning virgins I have no command of the Lord,
but I give an opinion as one who by the mercy of the Lord is trustworthy.”
But as Gruden states: “By this statement he seemingly implies that his
considered judgments were able to be placed on the same authoritative
level as the words of Jesus.”6 Erickson takes the same position and
considers it probable that “Paul is stating that the Lord is indeed speaking
(or writing) through him even though the tradition has not preserved any
words which the Lord himself spoke on this matter.”7 In short, Paul
explicitly states that even his opinion, by the Lord’s mercy, is completely
trustworthy. And then Paul uses irony in 1Cor 7:40 to drive the same point
home. There he asserts that his opinion is trustworthy because he has the
Spirit of God! Warfield points out that Paul’s effective understatement in
7:40 is not based on “modesty or doubt.” “Paul claims here a direct divine
inspiration in the expression of even his ‘opinion’ in his letters.” Consider
the “very emphatic ‘I’ staring us in the face, drawing the contrast so
sharply between Paul and the impugners of his authority.”8 Therefore,

        Before scaling to the summit of another peak that soars up from
First Corinthians, a brief survey of the terrain is in order. Paul is a Jew who
shares the beliefs of his countrymen in the inspiration of the OT. He claims
to have had an encounter with the risen Christ, and he makes emphatic
claims to have been commissioned as an apostle by this Christ. His claims
are attested by miracles as legitimate. His apostleship is accepted by other
apostles, and Peter acknowledges that Paul’s writings are Scripture. Paul
himself makes claims in both the Thessalonian epistles that strongly
indicate that he considers his own writings to be Scripture. Indeed, it
would have been very surprising if he had thought otherwise. Would he
think that the NT writings concerning the Messiah, His death, and the
theological and ethical ramification of these events to be inferior to the OT
writings which pointed forward to these events? Surely the writings which
looked back on these events would not be considered less authoritative
than those writings which only foreshadowed these events. Paul had been
appointed as an apostle by the Messiah Himself. Consequently, in 1Cor 7
when Paul presents directions with no force to demand compliance other
than the fact that they represented his expressed written opinions, these
were to be obeyed as a command from the risen Lord (1Cor 14:37-38).
Paul’s writings are recognized by himself and those who were spiritual
(1Cor 14:37, which included Peter) as inspired. This explanation is also in
perfect agreement with the promises which Jesus had made before and
after His resurrection to the disciples concerning authority and guidance
(Mt 28:18-20; Mk 13:11; Lk 10:16; Jn 14:26; 15:20; 16:13; Acts 1:8). One
would expect the promises of Christ, made effective through the Holy
Spirit, to apply to Paul, whom He called to the same apostolic office, and
such seems to be the undeniable conclusion.

       Primary Questions Concerning 1Cor 2:13
       This profile of Paul’s view of the inspiration regarding his own
writings should guide us in interpreting 1Cor 2:13. “We also speak, not in
words taught by human wisdom, but in those taught by the Spirit,
combining spiritual thoughts with spiritual words.” Who does the “we”
refer to? The options before us are to take it as referring (1) to Paul, (2) to
Paul and his companion(s), (3) to Paul and his fellow workers, (4) to Paul
and the other apostles, (5) to Paul and mature Christians, or (6) to Paul and
general believers. The second question to consider is, “Should
pneumatikois (the second spiritual) be understood as masculine or neuter
(as referring to men, or things and thus words)?”

point, Paul had contrasted the wisdom of men (particularly that of the
Greeks, 1Cor 1:22) with the wisdom of God, which appears to be foolish.
But in verse 6a he emphatically claims: “Yet we do speak wisdom among
those who are mature.” Although some of the Corinthians may have been
more impressed with the rhetorical abilities of Apollos (1Cor 3:4) than the
simplicity of Paul, they would be mistaken to attribute Paul’s simplicity to
be inability to speak at a deeper level. The mature does not refer to all
believers. Paul refers to the mature again in 3:1, with the designation of
spiritual in contrast to the babes. He contrasts the carnal, immature
believers in the Corinthian church with those who are spiritually mature
when he says that he cannot speak to those in the Corinthian church “as to
spiritual men, but as to men of flesh, as to babes.” He then tells them that
he cannot “even now” speak to them as spiritual men since they were “still
fleshly” (1Cor 3:2-3). Thereafter, Paul never refers to those in the
Corinthian church as spiritual (pneumatikos, except for a very
questionable reference in 1Cor 14:37*). The only other time in the letter
where he may conceivably refer to them as mature (teleios) is in
1Cor 14:20, when he says, “Stop being children . . . become mature”
(TM). Maturity for believers was not something out of reach in Paul’s
mind (Phil. 3:15), but it was obviously a level to which the Corinthian
believers had not yet obtained.
        Barrett (HNTC) attempts to counter this conclusion partially by
appealing to 1Cor 12:8. His arguments are basically (1) that only the
mature have this gift of speaking wisdom; (2) since some of the
Corinthians had it, some were mature; and (3) all the mature speak wisdom
“among” themselves (1Cor 2:6).10 Even if Barrett’s full conclusion is
granted, the we of verse 6 still would not refer to general believers—only
the mature. But are Barrett’s premises acceptable? In the context of
chapter 12, one gets the distinct impression that the presence of gifts does
not show maturity. As Radmacher remarks, “Even the most worldly
church in the New Testament, the church at Corinth did not lack spiritual
gifts (1Cor. 1:7), but they failed to exercise them properly.”11

                            1Cor 2:6b-9, Wisdom
       In 1Cor 2:6b-9, Paul expounds on the wisdom of 1Cor 2:6a. It is a
wisdom not (1) of this age, meaning it does not originate from this age nor
belong to it; instead, it is evaluated as foolish in this age. It is not (2) of the
rulers of this age, that is, it is not attributable to human leaders due to their
education. Rather, it is (3) God’s wisdom, denoting it as divine, having its
origin is God. (4) The fact that it is a mystery does not mean it is something
mysterious or a truth humans cannot grasp. Rather, mystery refers to a truth
which humans cannot discover on their own but which they can understand
once God reveals it to them through His prophets or apostles. (5) Being
formerly hidden designates it as a secret that is now revealed (cp. Rom
16:25). (6) Predestined refers to it as an eternal decree. (7) Not being
understood by the world’s highest representatives, civil and religious,
Roman and Jewish, does not mean that it is not understandable. (8) This
wisdom being undiscoverable by the eyes, ears, or heart, means that it is
not discovered empirically, nor through tradition, nor through logic and
intuition.
       Paul contrasts the divine and human wisdom in writing to Corinth,
a Greek city. In this regard, Wuest makes an astute observation that the
Greeks were “the most intellectual of all peoples…The sole instrument
which they used in their attempt to pierce through the mysteries of
existence was human reason.”13 Paul shows that human reason is an
inadequate means of reaching God. He had been trained in Greek schools.
“He was a native of Tarsus, a city where Greek culture predominated. The

“come with superiority of speech or of wisdom” in the “wisdom of men”

for in this verse he combines three terms—eye, ear, and heart—which are
three terms of natural knowing. In verse 9 Paul claims that the wisdom of
which he speaks did not come from any conceivable human source. In

could not come from any human source. “Paul by the use of pure logic
proves to these Greeks the impossibility of discovering God’s Word

      in His own heart. Therefore, if man finds it impossible

       Paul refutes rationalism, not rational thought. He uses logic to prove
the necessity of revelation (just as inerrantists use logic to prove the fact
of revelation).

with the first person verbal plural (we speak) in vv. 6,7,13. Verses 10-12
describe the means by which revelation is received, and verse 13 the means
by which it is declared or spoken. Thus, in these verses he develops
another word from 6a, speak. Kaiser believes that since revealed (v. 10) is

      that God has given a revelation to the ordinary believer in

       The original revelation given to the apostles is not the same thing as
the illumination given to believers. The original revelation guaranteed
inerrancy; illumination does not. The doctrine of inerrancy applies only to
the original writings (the autographs as they are called), not to the copies
of those writings. Nor does it apply to our understanding of those writings.
God evidently allows errors in copies and in our understanding to keep us
dependent upon the Spirit, upon each other, and upon our own diligence
in study for a proper interpretation of His revelation.
       Searches (v. 10) is present tense, evidently denoting continuous
action. Even the inerrant revelation which Paul received did not make him
omniscient. He still had to rely on the Holy Spirit (not his spirit, otherwise
the analogy with verse 11 breaks down); otherwise, continuous searching
would not be needed, and partial knowledge would be impossible.
       As the Spirit searched the depths (essence, attributes, decrees) of
God, He would continue to reveal wisdom to Paul so that he might know
(v. 12) this wisdom more fully (cf. 1Cor 13:10-12). Paul begins verse 12
with an emphatic but we, just as he did in verse 10, making the connection
clear. Paul had received the Spirit (not referring to regeneration) in order
that he might know the things [wisdom] freely given to him by God.
Commentators prefer to think of the last phrase of verse 12 as referring to
general believers: “We [general believers] might know the things freely
given [justification, etc.] to us [general believers] by God.”
       However, even if the concession were made that we in v. 12 is
inclusive, verse 13 would still refer to Paul, not general believers. (1) The
ha (things) of v. 13 evidently refers back to the ha (things) in the last
phrase of verse 9. This phrase is in the wisdom theme section (vv. 6-9).
These things must be revealed by God in order for a person to know them,

secondary revelation of his readers. (2) The laloumen (we speak) refers
back to verses 6 and 7, where it does not refer to general believers.

general believers.

        The kai (also) of v. 13 links it with the last phrase of v. 12 and
indicates that the things spoken are the things freely given. Consequently,
it is best to understand the last phrase of v. 12 concerning the things freely
given to us as referring to Paul, not general believers. Granted, there may
be application that can be drawn from these verses that would be
applicable to believers in a secondary sense, but application should not be
confused with interpretation. The we and us to whom Paul refers is not to
believers in general. It refers to those who receive the relation in v. 10 and
who do the speaking in vv. 6-7,13. It is an exclusive we. The things spoken
in v. 13 are the things freely given (v. 12), which are the things received
by revelation (v. 10). It is the wisdom of God (note the double ha in v. 9,
cf. v. 13). Thus, in verses 10-13 Paul is expounding on the word speak
from v. 6a. The kai of verse 13 connects the revelation of verses 10-12
with the inspiration of verse 13.
        Technically, this inspiration is probably not that of 2Tim 3:16,
where Paul says, “All Scripture is inspired by God and profitable for
teaching.” This refers to inspiration of the writings. The inspiration Paul
describes in v. 13 is more like 2Pet 1:21, where Peter said, “No prophecy
was ever made by an act of human will, but men moved by the Holy Spirit
spoke from God.” This refers to the inspiration of the prophets. The
prophets were moved and taught by the Spirit. The writings were inspired.
In the case of the prophets, God saw to it that what He wanted said was
said. In the case of the writings, God saw to it that what He wanted written
was written. “The same Divine breath which lifted the veil to reveal, takes
possession also of the mouth of the interpreter when it is to speak.
Inspiration is, as it were, the language of revelation.”17 As God
communicated Himself to Paul by revelation, He communicated Himself
through Paul by inspiration. As Kaiser surmises,

      Objection
       It could be protested that some of the Corinthians had revelations
(14:26,30) and were spiritual (14:37), and thus Paul may have been
referring to the Corinthians. If so, then in addition to the comments made
above, let it be further noted that 1Cor 14 itself indicates their immaturity.
Paul says, “Brethren, stop being children in your understanding…become
mature” (14:20; TM). In this chapter, Paul has to tell those who receive
the revelations how to behave when prophesying (14:30ff). He then
concludes, “If anyone thinks he is a prophet or spiritual, let him recognize
that the things I write to you are the Lord’s command. But if anyone does
not recognize this, he is not recognized” (14:37-38). Anyone who claimed
to be spiritual or to have a revelation was subject to what Paul spoke or
wrote. Thus, chapter 14 strengthens, not weakens, the view that Paul does
not consider his readers mature.
       Blum may be correct when he says that 1Cor 2:13 “probably does
not refer directly to Paul’s writing ministry but rather to his preaching and
teaching.”26 On the other hand, Ryrie makes a credible observation,

            The fact that Paul says he spoke in words does not
      mean that he is not referring to his writings. Notice that Peter
      said Paul ‘spoke’ in his writings (2 Peter 3:16). So ‘speaking
      in words’ can certainly refer to Paul’s written letters.”27

        This position is of course strengthened by 1Cor 14:37, where Paul
refers to his writing. Both Peter and Paul use laleo, which makes the
assertion by Robertson all the more applicable “Laleo in the N.T. as here
is used of the highest and holiest speech. Undoubtedly, Paul employs the
word purposely for the utterance of the revelation which he has
understood.”28
       In any event, since Paul uses laleo instead of kerusso or kataggello,
he is not talking about preachers in general. Paul is defending his own
preaching (1Cor 2:1-5), not that of others. Kaiser concludes, “Paul is
mainly referring to himself in this type of editorial ‘we’ or ‘us’ reference
and perhaps in a derivative sense, those fellow-teachers who labored with
him.”29 This editorial we is not an inclusive we that includes the readers. It
is an exclusive we that excludes them. They were carnal, not spiritual. It is
an apostolic we with primary focus on Paul himself.

      Conclusion
       An exegetical examination of the context of 1Cor 2:13 leads to the
conclusion that Paul is using an exclusive we in this passage that excludes
the Corinthian readers from being part of the company being described as
spiritual. Consequently, the LS argument that all believers are spiritual in
the sense being described in this text is hermeneutically faulty and results
in a soteriological fallacy in assuming that the carnal Corinthians were not
carnal believers. This church was composed of carnal believers.
Categorization of some believers as carnal is biblically necessary.
Compartmentalization of such believers to the outer darkness as those
saved by fire, as described in 1Cor 3:15, is to be expected.

      Introduction
        Olson declares: “True believers will persevere.”1 His Calvinistic
proof-text is the ever-popular 1Jn 2:19. “They went out from us, but they
were not really of us; for if they had been of us, they would have remained
with us; but they went out, in order that it might be shown that they all are
not of us.” Supposedly, this verse not only proves the perseverance of the
saints but also gives us the means by which to determine who is really
saved on the basis of the way they live. If a person does not persevere, this
verse shows, so the Calvinist assumes, that he or she was never really of
us and thus never really saved to begin with. As in the previous discussion,
it will be necessary to consider the possibility that an apostolic we, or in
this case an apostolic us, is intended. Is us in 1Jn 2:19 apostolic or
Calvinistic?
        FG exegesis argues for an apostolic understanding. These false
teachers went out from the apostolic community and thereby showed that
they did not share the apostolic outlook and were not operating under
apostolic authority. In its historical context, this verse says nothing about
true believers persevering in the faith. To the contrary, it implies just the
opposite. The concern in the historical context is that these false teachers
would subvert the faith of genuine believers. This text implicitly affirms
the possibility that a believer’s faith may be destroyed.

      Tentative Definition of Apostolic We
       Since John wrote this epistle, it may be best to begin our
examination of the historical context with his gospel. To whom does the
we of Jn 1:14,16 refer in GJ? “And the Word became flesh, and dwelt
among us, and we beheld His glory” (Jn 1:14). This introduction to his
gospel corresponds to the prelude of his epistle: “What was from the
beginning, what we have heard, what we have seen with our eyes, what we
beheld and our hands handled, concerning the Word of Life—and the life
was manifested, and we have seen and bear witness and proclaim to you
the eternal life, which was with the Father and was manifested to us”
(1Jn 1:1-2). The first person plural designation refers to John and the other
eyewitnesses within the apostolic community.
       My tentative thesis, accordingly, is that the apostolic we in the
general epistles was used by an apostolic writer. I will further postulate
that these epistles were written while the apostolic writer was in Jerusalem
so that what he wrote had the authority of that council and church so that
Page 94                                                      Carnal Corinth
it conveyed the meaning: “I and the other apostolic leaders of the apostolic
council here in the Jerusalem church.” If so, one could fill in the italicized
text above with this expression: “What was from the beginning, what I and
the other apostolic leaders of the apostolic council here in the Jerusalem
church have heard, what I and the other apostolic leaders of the apostolic
council here in the Jerusalem church have seen with our eyes, what I and
the other apostolic leaders of the apostolic council here in the Jerusalem
church have beheld and our hands handled, concerning the Word of Life—
and the life was manifested, and I and the other apostolic leaders of the
apostolic council here in the Jerusalem church have seen and bear witness
and proclaim to you the eternal life, which was with the Father and was
manifested to us” (1Jn 1:1-2).
       The FG defense of the apostolic position does not necessitate this
level of exactitude; nevertheless, it is a plausible position. It is possible
that Jude, 2Pet, 1Jn, 2Jn, and 3Jn were all written from the same place
(Jerusalem) to the same place (Asia Minor) for the same reason (the
problem of false teachers who crept into the Hellenistic churches) about
the same time A.D. 61-65.

      False Teachers Still in the Churches
        These false teachers were among the Hellenistic churches to which
the epistles were written (cf. Jude 1:12—in your love feasts). Thus, by the
time John wrote his first epistle (A.D. 63), these Jewish Gnostics had left
the Jerusalem church (us in 1Jn 2:19 is thus an apostolic us). The us of
1Jn 2:19 cannot be an epistolary us since the other contemporary writings
(external evidence) indicates that these false teachers were in the churches.
Jude 1:4 says they have crept in, not that they had crept out of the church.
But which church? The NIV, BBE, NLT, and NJB insert the phrase,
“among you,” thereby indicating that they had crept into the church to
which Jude was writing. The Greek text, however, does not make this
specification. The NLT also says that they cause divisions among you in
Jude 1:19, and the NIV says that they divide you. Again, the Greek text
does not specify. Even the more literal translations frequently add with you
in Jude 1:12, but again the Greek text does not explicitly make this spatial
connection. On the other hand, it is readily apparent in this particular verse
that this elliptical addition is justified on the basis of the immediate
context, since the Greek text does in fact specify that they were in your
love feasts in Jude 1:12. Consequently, the broad context requires that Jude
1:4 and 1:19 be understood similarly, and the addition of among you by
various translations is thus contextually justifiable. These false teachers
already had crept in among the Hellenistic believers’ love feast and were
still there causing divisions among those believers.

      Futuristic Tense but Present Danger
       Peter prophesies in 2Pet 2:1 and 3:3 that the false prophets will be
among the people (the Hellenistic churches). Albeit, the use of the future
tense does not mean they were not yet among the believers. The present
imperative of 2Pet 3:17 contextually indicates a present danger. These
false prophets were already in the church. Peter uses a present imperative
rather than a future imperative (i.e., an imperatival future) when he urges
the believers to be on your guard. The vividness of the context stresses the
immediacy of the concern. Likewise, the use of the future in the parallel
passage of Jude 1:18 does not mean the prophesied event had not yet
occurred but that the present event had been prophesied. The prophesied
mockers were present; their mocking had been prophesied. Therefore, 1Jn
2:19 and 4:1 do not mean false teachers were leaving the churches to go
out into the world. Rather, they were infiltrating the churches throughout
the world. The epistles are expressing this warning about such false
teachers to these infiltrated churches. The problem with false teachers is
not that they leave the church; on the contrary, the major problem caused
by false teachers is that they do not leave the church. They had not gone
out of the churches in general. Instead, they had gone out of one church
into another—out of the Jerusalem church into the Hellenistic churches
scattered throughout the world.

      Internal Evidence
       The internal evidence also confirms the conclusion that the first-
person plural is apostolic since the we-you contrast, which already has
been made clear in 1Jn 1:1-5, is similarly contrasted in 2:19ff. Also, in the
only other passage in 1Jn where the we-you-they contrast is made so clear
(1Jn 4:4-6), the first-person plural is apostolic: “You are from God, little
children, and have overcome them; because greater is He who is in you
than he who is in the world. They are from the world; therefore they speak
as from the world, and the world listens to them. We [I and the other
apostolic leaders of the apostolic council here in the Jerusalem church]
are from God; he who knows God listens to us; he who is not from God
does not listen to us. By this we know the spirit of truth and the spirit of
error.”

      Historical Parallel
      A historical parallel also points to this scenario as probable. In
Acts 15 Jewish legalists from Judea went out of that church into other
churches and were disturbing them by saying that faith in Christ was not
enough for salvation, that certain kinds of works were also required in
Page 96                                                      Carnal Corinth
addition to faith in order to be saved. As a result, James, Peter, and John
had to convene a council (Gal 2:9) to deal with the problem. These false,
legalistic brethren had sneaked into the Jerusalem church (Gal 2:4). They
then went out from the apostolic community at the Jerusalem church into
the Hellenistic churches. On that occasion, there was also a contrast
between the apostolic we (defined in Acts 15:23) and those who went out
from them:
      23
        They wrote this letter by them: The apostles, the elders,
      and the brethren, to the brethren who are of the Gentiles in
      Antioch, Syria, and Cilicia: Greetings. 24 “Since we have

       The apostolic we/us is defined for us in this passage, and history is
seen repeating itself a few years later:

      They went out from us, but they were not of us; for if they
      had been of us, they would have continued with us; but they
      went out that they might be made manifest, that none of them
      were of us. (1Jn 2:19; NKJ)

       The same phraseology is used to describe both schisms from the
apostolic circle. It is not surprising that the apostolic council in Jerusalem
would deal with a similar threat thirteen years later in a similar fashion,
again warning the churches about another group of false teachers that went
out from their church. The situation was similar. This time it was Jewish
Gnostics who had gone out from Judea into other churches to disturb them.
They had left the church at Jerusalem and crept into the Hellenistic
churches (Jude 1:4). These Gnostics had gone out into the world of the
Hellenistic churches of Asia Minor (1Jn 4:1).

      Writing about the Problem
       Paul also wrote about this same problem to the same area at this
same time: Colossians (A.D. 60), 2 Timothy (A.D. 67). The expression
that John used in 1Jn 1:4, “these things we write,” refers to a plural number
of writers and writings. The we does not refer to plural authorship (i.e.,
a plural number of writers), such as a Johannine community (suggested by
liberals). The epistle of 1 John was written by John, not by a community
of his followers. In these affirmations of plural authorship, John notifies
Appendix 2. The Apostolic We of 1Jn 2:19                               Page 97
his readership that he is (1) writing as a representative of the entire
apostolic circle, (2) is giving the purpose statement of his entire epistle,
and (3) is including this epistle in the body of literature written by the other
apostles. This assessment is confirmed by the fact that everywhere else
John uses the verb “write” he says, “I write,” not “we write” (Jn 21:25;
1Jn 2:1,7-8,12-14,21,26; 5:13; 2Jn 1:5,12; 3Jn 1:9,13,14). Every time John
exclusively refers to (a portion of) his writing of 1John, he does so in the
singular, saying I write rather than in the plural we write. This distinction
shows that “these things we write” is not merely this epistle; rather, these
things also includes other writings—it at least includes Jude and 2Peter.
        Moreover, the purpose statement for this particular epistle is found
in this introduction in 1:4 rather than in its conclusion. Hence, the attempt
by LS to turn this epistle into a soteriological litmus test by appealing to
5:13 as the purpose statement for the entire epistle is faulty. The purpose
of this opening phrase in 1:4 is not only to place this epistle within this
broader apostolic corpus, but also to give the purpose for this particular
letter which takes its place among that corpus.
        This opening phraseology refers to any gospel or epistle written
(1) by an apostolic leader of the apostolic council, (2) while in Jerusalem
as a representative of that council and church, and (3) with that council
and church bearing witness to the truthfulness of that gospel or epistle (cf.
Acts 15:23). Thus, Jn 21:24 means: “I and the other leaders of the church
where I am writing know that my testimony is true.” From where were
these apostolic writers writing? Jerusalem. Consequently, I would date and
locate 1John as being written from Jerusalem before the apostolic circle
was disbanded in A.D. 68. Although Paul uses the apostolic we while
traveling on the road in his ministry to the Gentiles, and thus while away
from Jerusalem, he still uses this apostolic we to claim apostolic authority
and solidarity with the apostolic circle at Jerusalem, who formerly and
formally had extended the “right hand of fellowship” to him and their
support for his departure to “go to the Gentiles” (Gal 2:9). They sanctioned
his going out from them to the Hellenistic churches, but they did not
sanction this other gnostic group’s departure for the Hellenistic churches.

       Brief Conjectural Bibliographical Outline
      The relation of John’s writings in regard to the writings of the
general epistles may be helpful to identify the usage of the apostolic we.
John, the son of Zebedee (Mk 1:20) and younger brother of James
(Mt 4:1), was born perhaps five years after Jesus, in Bethsaida (cp.
Lk 5:10; Jn 1:44) or its neighboring city Capernaum (Mk 1:21,29), which
were located in Galilee—at least this was the place of John’s residence
when he was called by Jesus. It seems rather certain that John the Apostle
Page 98                                                    Carnal Corinth
was a disciple of John the Baptist before he became a disciple of Jesus (cp.
Mk 1:16-20,29; with Jn 1:35-40).
        John appears to also have had a home in Jerusalem (cp.
Mt 5:17&20; cp. Jn 19:27 & Lk 18:28; cf. Jn 18:15-16). John’s family was
well-to-do, since the fishing business was very lucrative at that time and
area. As a result, his father had hired hands (Mk 1:20). His mother, Salome
(cp. Mt 27:56 & Mk 15:40) was one of the women who followed Jesus
and supported Him financially (cp. Mk 15:41 & Lk 8:3). After Jesus’

mother (Jn 19:26,27) and was a leader in the Jerusalem church (Gal 2:9).
        When the persecution broke out in Jerusalem with the stoning of
Stephen and persecution by Saul in (A.D. 33-34), John, as well as the other
apostles, remained in Jerusalem. However, the persecution resulted in the
dispersion (diesparesan) of the Jewish believers (Acts 8:1). James, Jesus’
half-brother, wrote his epistle from Jerusalem to the Jewish believers of
Asia Minor (A.D. 34-35). James addresses his epistle to these dispersed
(diaspora) Jewish believers (Jam 1:1). The conversion of the Gentiles did
not begin until the conversion of Cornelius in Acts 10 (A.D. 40-41). James,
John’s brother, was martyred in Jerusalem in A.D. 44 (Acts 12:1-2). Mary
died in A.D. 48, which released John from his obligation to stay in
Jerusalem where he was taking care of her. At the council of Jerusalem in
A.D. 49 (Acts 15; Gal 2:9), nevertheless, John was present to meet with
Paul in Jerusalem.
        At this council it is decided that Paul should continue to go to the
Gentiles in his missionary endeavors, while Peter (1Pet 1:1), and James
(Jesus’ half-brother, Jam 1:1), and John (Gal 2:9) would make it their
policy to go to the Jews of the dispersion (i.e., to Greek speaking Jews who
lived outside of Palestine who were called Hellenistic Jews, Jn 7:35). John,
who had been giving his gospel orally to the Jews in Palestine (with the
aid of notes which he had written), now began to give it in the same manner
to the Jews of the dispersion in Asia Minor. John was probably away in
Asia Minor when Paul returned to Jerusalem in A.D. 57 and thus is not
mentioned in Acts 21:18.
        John returned to Jerusalem and wrote GJ (chs. 1-20) for the Jews of
Asia Minor—(A.D. 58-60). This explains the presence of the apostolic we
in Jn 1:14,16. Jude wrote from Jerusalem to the Jews of Asia Minor (A.D.
61-62) before the death of his brother James in (A.D. 62; cp. Jude 1). He
writes before the writing of 2Peter. Would Jude have bothered to write his
epistle after 2Peter already had been written and covered the same material
(cp. Jude with 2Pet 2:1-3:2ff)? Or, at the very least, would Jude not have
felt free to write about salvation (Jude 1:4) instead of covering what Peter
Appendix 2. The Apostolic We of 1Jn 2:19                            Page 99
already had covered if 2Peter already had been written? Therefore, I
believe Jude was written before 2Peter.
        James, Jesus’ half-brother, is martyred in A.D. 62. After hearing of
James’ death, Peter writes 1Peter from Rome (1Pet 5:13) to the Jews of
Asia Minor (A.D. 62-63) in order to urge submission to governmental
authority despite governmental hostility. Peter returns to Jerusalem and
writes 2Peter to the Jews of Asia Minor (A.D. 63), after being informed
by Jude of the situation there and of the letter which Jude had written to
them. Peter uses the apostolic we to urge that they pay attention to what
Jude had written and to what Peter himself is now writing—which
elaborates upon what Jude had written (2Pet 1:16-19).
        John writes 1John from Jerusalem to the Jews of Asia Minor (A.D.
63). The apostolic we used by John in 1Jn 1:4 refers to Jude, Peter, and
John and their writings (Jude, 2Pet, 1Jn). He deals with the same problem
addressed by these writers when he implements another apostolic we later
in his epistle (1Jn 2:19). John then writes 2John from Jerusalem to the Jews
of Asia Minor (A.D. 64). In response to 2John not being obeyed (cf.
3Jn 1:9), John next writes 3John from Jerusalem to the Jews of Asia Minor
(A.D. 65).
        Peter leaves from Jerusalem for Rome (A.D. 65) and is martyred
there (A.D. 66). John adds ch. 21 to his gospel while in Jerusalem in
response to Peter’s death (A.D. 66). He uses the apostolic we in Jn 21:24.
After the Jewish revolt breaks out in A.D. 66, John leaves for Ephesus in
Asia Minor (A.D. 67). John publishes his entire Gospel (chs. 1-21) when
he gets to Ephesus (A.D. 67-70) but before the destruction of Jerusalem
(note the time of the tense in Jn 5:2).

      Conclusion
       It is quite possible that the apostles who had their ministry
headquartered in Jerusalem restricted their usage of the apostolic we to
those times when they were actually in Jerusalem and thus representing
the Jerusalem church of which they were pillars. Since Paul’s ministry was
not centered in Jerusalem, he did not feel the same compulsion to refrain
from using the apostolic we when he was writing while on the mission
field. There is no need to postulate that the apostolic we was used in a
postmortem time period after the apostolic circle was disbanded. A
historical-grammatical hermeneutic is most dissuasive of a Calvinistic
explanation of 1Jn 2:19. This verse is dealing with a group of people who
had defected from the faith and posed a serious threat to infect others as
well because they were infiltrating the churches.
       Whether the defectors who had turned into infectors were merely
unregenerate professors of the faith rather than actual possessors of eternal
Page 100                                                      Carnal Corinth
life is not certain. It would seem probable that at least some of them were
regenerate since the apostolic concern was that they would infect genuine
believers, and one would think that genuine believers who had been
infected would be a source of infection to others. In any event, it is likely
that their ability to subvert genuine believers had already been
demonstrated locally. Regardless, 1Jn 2:19 is not providing a
soteriological litmus test by which we can determine who is a genuine
believer.
        Quite to the contrary, the supposition that this group of defectors
was composed exclusively of unbelievers is rather unlikely. But even if
this group were composed exclusively of false brethren (which may have
been the case in the historical parallel, Gal 2:4), the point of the passage is
not that theological, much less moral, defection from the faith proves that
the defector is lost. Rather, defection shows rejection of the apostolic
witness and writings. And genuine believers are not immune to infection
from this defection. Even Barnabas had been initially carried away
(sunapago) by the hypocrisy of the original infectors in the historical
parallel (Gal 2:13), and other believers were persuaded to some extent by
the claims of these theological apostates (Acts 15:5).2 Unrepentant moral
and theological defectors from the faith who have experienced saving faith
will be subject to punishment in the outer darkness.

                     Blameless in 1Cor 1:8
     Whether the Corinthians are assured of being soteriologically
blameless or misthologically blameless is a matter of translation and
interpretation in 1Cor 1:8. My statement in the body of the present book
that the Corinthians are assured of being confirmed blameless is
purposefully ambiguous and optimistic so as to allow either possibility.
Regardless of which approach is taken, however, this verse confirms the
regenerate status of the readership. Only believers are unconditionally
guaranteed soteriological blamelessness and have the potential for
misthological blamelessness.
     As explained in Fallen from Grace, a very optimistic misthological
understanding of passages such as 1Cor 1:8 is certainly compatible with
FG theology. Yet the misthological interpretation of 1Cor 1:8 does not
assume an ironclad guarantee that all believers will be misthologically
blameless (c.f., 1Cor 3:15). Notwithstanding my misthological preference
regarding 1Cor 1:8, a properly nuanced soteriological interpretation of
1Cor 1:8 is possible. The following discussion will present four basic
interpretive options for this verse: the first three of which are
misthological.
                    Four Basic Interpretations for 1Cor 1:8
                             Option 1       Option 2       Option 3      Option 4
  Theological Persuasion    Free Grace     Reformed       Arminian      Free Grace
  Translation of bebaioo      secures      strengthens    strengthens   strengthens
  Contingent on Free Will       no              no            yes            yes
  Sovereignly bebaioo           yes            yes             no            no
  Sovereignly Blameless     yes (result)   yes (result)   no (intent)    no (intent)
  Unconditional Security      explicit          no             no         implicit
  Soteric or Mistholic        Soteric         Soteric        Soteric     Mistholic

                                     Option 1
     Paul could be understood as saying that God “will beyond doubt
unconditionally guarantee you until the end, [with the result that you will
be] blameless in the day of our Lord Jesus Christ” (TM). To guarantee
beyond doubt is within the semantic range of bebaioo (see BDAG), and
many translations give the impression in the second half of the verse that
being blameless is the result (rather than purpose). In fact, being sealed by
the Spirit until the day of redemption in Eph 4:30 would make an
interesting conceptual parallel to being blameless until the day of the Lord
in 1Cor 1:8.
     This parallel is not farfetched in that bebaioo also occurs in the
opening chapter of the follow-up epistle to the Corinthians and is enjoined
Page 102                                                     Carnal Corinth
with being sealed: “Now He who guarantees us with you in Christ and
anointed us is God, who also sealed us and gave us the Spirit in our hearts
as a guarantee” (2Cor 1:21-22; TM). Accordingly, VGNT says that Paul,
by associating bebaioo (guarantee) with arrabon (guarantee) to denote
“the guaranteeing the delivery of something of which the earnest has
already been paid, can describe the relation of God to believers.” God
guarantees (bebaioo) believers with this guarantee (arrabon). On this
occasion, two different words are used to convey the guarantee. VNGT
cites a parallel where bebaioo is repeated to mean “will guarantee the sale
with every guarantee.” By having unconditionally anointed us, sealed us,
and given us the Spirit, God provides us an ongoing guarantee that we
have an unconditional relationship with Him.
      The use of the present participle for bebaioo in conjunction with
aorist participles for the other three verbs 2Cor 1:21-22 does not
necessarily rule out a dynamic affirmation of static unconditional security.
By means of the work that God has completed for us at the point of
conversion, He still assures us that we are secure in Christ. For this reason,
the definition of secure for bebaioo supplied by L&S is plausible. Indeed,
the translation provided by the NAB is credible: “The one who gives us
security with you in Christ and who anointed us is God.” Perhaps, a
smoother translation fitting would be: “Now He who secures us with you
in Christ and anointed us is God, who also sealed us and gave us the Spirit
in our hearts as a security” (2Cor 1:21-22; TM).
      Just as the present tense for bebaioo does not rule out an affirmation
of unconditional security in 2Cor 1:21, the future tense does not rule out a
similar understanding in 1Cor 1:8. According to Lenski, the future tense
in this verse “has the force of a divine promise” (Lenski, First Corinthians,
34). As an Arminian conditionalist, Lenski assumes that eternal security is
at stake because he regards salvation from Hell to be unfinished business
for the believer and to be dependent upon believers availing themselves of
God’s strengthening. As a Reformed conditionalist, Garland makes much
the same assumption: “The future tense of bebaiw,sei, however, implies
that God’s saving work is not yet finished” (1 Corinthians, Exegetical
Commentary on the New Testament, 35). God has to keep making them
strong so that they will stay saved.
      Securitists, on the other hand, conclude that when the Lord said, “It
is finished” (Jn 19:30), He had completed everything necessary to keep a
believer saved. God continues to keep us saved by means of what He
already has done for us. In 2Cor 1:21-22, for example, God continues to
secure us (present tense) by means of what He has completed for us (aorist
tense) when He anointed, sealed, and gave us the Spirit as our security. Or
to use the illustration provided by the Lord in Jn 10:28-29, as a result of
Appendix 3. Blameless in 1Cor 1:8                                  Page 103
having taken us into His hand and having given us eternal life, God
continues to keep us safe in His hand. Combining future tense 1Cor 1:8
with present participle of 2Cor 1:21-22 would give this understanding:
God presently is guaranteeing us (because of what He already has done for
us) that He futuristically will secure us to the end with the result that,
soteriologically, we undoubtedly will be unconditionally blameless in the
day of our Lord Jesus Christ.
      Bebaioo is also used in 1Cor 1:6, and the adjective (bebaios) in
2Cor 1:6. VGNT insists upon the sense of guaranteed security in the latter
verse. The NJB translates it accordingly: “Our hope for you is secure.”
Paul guarantees his hope for them. One might even perceive something of
the same sense in 1Cor 1:6. God secured the gospel in the Corinthian
congregation corporately by securing the witness of the gospel within each
believer internally. “The witness [marturion] of Christ was secured in [en]
you” (TM). John put it this way: “The one who believes in the Son of God
has the witness in [marturia en] himself” (1Jn 5:10). An explicit
confirmation of unconditional security is theologically and lexically
possible.
                                      Option 2
      Schreiner and Caneday, however, use the NIV translation to pose
conditional security instead: “He will keep you strong to the end, so that
you will be blameless on the day of our Lord Jesus Christ” (1Cor 1:8). God
keeps you strong to the end (i.e., makes you endure to the end) so that you
can be sufficiently blameless to enter Heaven. How blameless? Not
sinlessly blameless, just sufficiently blameless. According to Schreiner
and Caneday, “being blameless on the day of the Lord does not suggest
sinless perfection in this life.” Thomas R. Schreiner and Ardel B. Caneday,
The Race Set Before Us: A Biblical Theology of Perseverance &
Assurance (Nottingham: Inter-Varsity Press, 2001), 264.
      Their suggesting that God keeps us relatively sinless in the present so
that we will be absolutely blameless in the next life (and thus qualify to
enter Heaven) seems to be a rather peculiar interpretation. More
importantly for present purposes, however, note that Schreiner, Caneday,
and the NIV interpret both halves of the verse to denote a guaranteed
result: what will happen, not merely what may happen. You will be strong
in this life; you will be saved in the next life as a result.
      The problem with this second option is not that it perceives a
guaranteed result. Indeed, first option does also. However, these two
interpretations come to two different conclusions regarding unconditional
security. Option 1 affirms it; option 2 denies it. The primary problem with
option two is that it conditions the believer’s security on the believer’s
performance. The NIV’s excessively positive rendition of 2Cor 1:21
Page 104                                                     Carnal Corinth
seems harmless enough at first glance: “Now it is God who makes both us
and you stand firm in Christ.” But when seen from the Reformed
perspective, which is the perspective from which Schreiner and Caneday
see the issue and from which the NIV translates both set of verses, the
pieces give this composite picture: God will make the elect strong/firm to
the end, so that the elect will qualify to enter Heaven as a result. The elect
are allowed into Heaven because they persevere. God has guaranteed that
the elect will persevere and reach Heaven as a result. The elect have no
choice to the contrary in this matter. The elect will persevere; they cannot
do otherwise. God conditions His children remaining His children on their
service to Him. As if that were not bad enough, the nonelect have no choice
in the matter either. God never intended to give the nonelect strength
enough to persevere and thus make it to Heaven. The nonelect cannot do
otherwise. Free will is not truly a factor.
                                      Option 3
     Arminians rightly champion free will, not for the sake of free will,
but for the sake of God’s character which is besmirched by option 2.
Rather than concluding that God limits His saving strengthening to elect
believers, so as to make them irresistibly persevere and damning nonelect
believers to Hell because they do not persevere (even though they cannot
do otherwise), Arminians understand these verses as saying that God
provides His strengthening to all believers. The Lord makes it possible for
all believers to reach Heaven. Furthermore, all who hear the gospel may
respond to it in saving faith. Such an approach is to be commended in
defending free will in defense of God’s character on both accounts. Yet
even the Arminian position fails to go far enough in defending God’s
character against the slanderous implications of option 2. The Arminian
position still wrongly portrays God conditioning His relationship with His
children on their performance.
     Option 1 rightly would insist that God does not condition our
remaining His child on our ongoing obedience to God but on the fact that
He permanently has sealed and secured us. Our security is conditioned on
God having sealed us, not on God strengthening us. Once we become
secured, our free will is not a factor as to whether we will remain secure.
Although we may not persevere, we will go to Heaven. We cannot do
otherwise. We must go to Heaven because God has unconditionally
promised us that we will go to Heaven. He has not made our remaining
His child contingent on our performance. Making God’s keeping us
contingent on our free will would, in fact, be an attack against His
character. So just as Arminians justly champion free will pertaining to
becoming a child of God in order to defend God’s character against the
slanderous implications of option 2, securitists properly deny free will
Appendix 3. Blameless in 1Cor 1:8                                 Page 105
pertaining to remaining a child of God in order to defend God’s character
against the slanderous implications of both options 2 and 3. The Arminian
option (# 3) is better than the Reformed option (# 2) but inferior to the
securitist option (# 1).
                                      Option 4
     Despite the attractiveness of option 1, a better option is available to
securitists. Bebaioo is frequently translated as strengthen in 1Cor 1:8
(BDAG). Misthologists, therefore, are inclined to agree with conditional
securitists that the verse is discussing a blamelessness that is conditioned
on our standing firm. We need God’s strengthening so that we may stand
firm and thus be blameless on that day. Misthologists, however,
understand the verse as holding forth the possibility of misthological
blamelessness. Being misthologically blameless is possible but only if we
diligently persevere. Paul uses this same adjective for being
misthologically blameless (anegkletos) in Col 1:22-23. Indeed, on all other
occasions he uses this Greek adjective, Paul refers to a blamelessness that
is contingent upon performance (1Tim 3:10; Tit 1:6-7). Paul uses amomos
synonymously in Col 1:22. On the other two occasions where he uses
amomos, he likewise refers to believers being misthologically blameless
(Eph 1:4; 5:27). Although some misthologists might contest this latter
statement, it is nonetheless true that there is not a single uncontested
Pauline usage where amomos refers to unconditional security. Indeed,
Jude’s use of amomos in 1:24 sounds very much like what Paul is saying
in 1Cor 1:8. God is able to keep believers from stumbling, that is, God is
able to make them firm and strong, so that they will be blameless at the
Bema.
     That God provides believers with the strength they need does not
prove that believers will avail themselves of that strength, however.
Provisional strengthening does not guarantee the outcome. Failure is
possible. Advocates of option 2 completely overstate their case. True, God
strengthens us, but this does not mean that we will be strong, only that we
have the potential to be strong. For example, just because God will provide
a way of escape for believers when they encounter temptation does not
mean that all believers will take that escape route and thus endure. Even
the NIV gets this verse right: “When you are tempted, he will also provide
a way out so that you can stand up under it” (1Cor 10:13). Not even the
NIV translates this verse as saying, “When you are tempted, he will also
provide a way out so that you will stand up under it.”
     Just because believers can stand does not prove that they will stand.
Similarly, just because God strengthens them does not prove that they will
be strong. Whether they flee the temptation and stand up under trial is
contingent upon their freewill response. Correspondingly, a verse like
Page 106                                                  Carnal Corinth
1Cor 1:8 should be translated so as to show intent rather than result: “He
will also strengthen you to the end, so that you may be blameless on the
day of our Lord Jesus Christ” (NRS). You may be; you can be. But this
does not necessarily mean that you will be. Numerous translations
correctly render this verse as showing intention rather than result.
Moreover, even if one insists upon a translation that conveys a sense of
result, the result need not necessarily be guaranteed unconditionally.
Rather, the potential result is merely being expressed in a very positive
manner. See Fallen from Grace for discussion related to latter perspective
and related texts (e.g., Rom 14:4).
       Regardless as to whether being misthological blameless is seen as
the provisional intention or contingent result, what is at stake for the
Corinthian readers is how well they will do at the Bema. Consequently,
their soteriological security is assumed in that being soteriologically
blameless is not at stake. Unconditional soteriological security is still
affirmed implicitly even if one interprets the passage misthologically.

      Stegall has accused GES of promoting a crossless gospel in which

has always been faith alone, but the CONTENT of that faith has changed”
(pt. 2, 11; emphasis his). By his own acknowledgment, Stegall believes
that the saving content of the gospel has changed from the OT to the NT.
Therefore, his position accordingly may be dubbed as the changed gospel
(CG) position.
       Advocates of CG use 1Cor 15:1-4 to prove that we are saved from
Hell by the gospel proclamation of the cross and resurrection. Indeed, in
the portion of this present book to which this endnote refers, I entertain the
soteriological interpretation of this passage. Although I state my
preference in my book, Salvation, for an alternative understanding of the
passage, my allowance herein of a possible soteriological understanding
of 1Cor 15:1-4 might unnecessarily be thought to indicate an endorsement
of CG.
       To clarify, I would be inclined to agree with CG advocates that the
message of the gospel includes soteric content. It might also be admitted
in 1Cor 1:18 that those who are perishing soteriologically have rejected
the gospel message (which includes the message of the cross) and that the
reaction to the NT preaching of the cross separates the lost from the saved
in this context. Such concessions, however, provide an insufficient basis
from which to derive the CG conclusion that the cross is the saving object
of faith (or even that justification salvation is in view in 1Cor 15:2).
       Not only does CG logically fail to follow as a necessary conclusion,
the CG dichotomy between condition and content is not logically coherent.
One cannot change the objective content of the gospel without changing
the condition of the gospel. The objective content is equivalent to the
consequence. (For symmetry between condition and consequence, see
“Symmetry” in the concluding chapter of The Outer Darkness.) For
example, according to GES, we must believe that Christ gives eternal life
to those who simply believe in Him for eternal life. The condition is that
we believe. The content of the proclamation to be believed is that Christ
gives eternal life to those who simply believe in Him for it. If we change
Page 108                                                      Carnal Corinth
the proposition to be believed, then, obviously, we have changed the
condition also. The condition is not simply that we believe; it is that we
believe the stated proposition. Consequently, CG, by its own
acknowledgement, is arguing that the gospel message has changed from
the OT to the NT. And I would further point out that, in doing so, it
necessarily changes the condition for regeneration. CG advocates two
different gospels: one for OT believers, another for NT believers.
        In contrast, as one can see from Stegall’s criticism, GES is
attempting to maintain a singular gospel message spanning the OT and
NT. I will therefore dub the GES position accordingly as the unchanged
gospel (UG) position. This is not to say that the gospel does not entail more
information in the NT. Those of us who hold the UG position affirm
progressive revelation, of course. However, our agreement with CG that
progressive revelation has an impact upon the content of the gospel does
not require our changing the saving object of faith. In agreement with UG,
we affirm that believers in both the OT and NT are saved by trusting in the
promised Messiah for eternal life. So in what sense has progressive
revelation impacted the content of the gospel if it has not changed the
content of the proposition that must be believed in order to experience
regeneration?
        Our answer entails acknowledging that the gospel is not limited to
soteric content, much less to objective soteric content. For example, the
gospel message also includes instrumental material. If the message of the
cross is the instrument being used to draw men to faith in Christ for eternal
life, as seems to be the case (Jn 12:32), then to reject the instrumental
message would naturally entail the rejection of the objective message to
which that instrumental message points. The objective message being
pointed to is the proposition that men receive eternal life by simply
believing in Christ alone for eternal life. If this is the case, then the
objective content of the gospel has not been changed.
        Accordingly, if the message of the cross is the instrumental
component of the gospel message that Paul is using to bring men to trust
in Christ alone for eternal life, then naturally one would be saved by
accepting that instrumental message as a psychological means of reaching
the theological requirement that one believe in Christ alone for eternal life.
The cross appears to be the means of lifting up the object of faith, which
is Christ. Paul is using the cross to lift up Christ as the object of faith, is
he not? This would be in accordance with Jesus’ prophecy in Jn 12:32.
        CG needs to prove that the cross is either the co-object of soteric
faith or the necessary theological means of coming to soteric faith. If the
message of the cross is only a psychological necessity, though, it may only
be so in certain circumstances for certain people. (Of course, no one could
Appendix 4. What is the Gospel?                                    Page 109
be saved if Christ had not died on the cross. The question is, “Must a
person recognize the significance of this necessity?”) A mere
psychological necessity (in terms of what must be believed) would not
suffice for the point CG is seeking to make. For example, when Paul
proclaims the message of the cross as an instrumental means of drawing
people to faith in Christ, it is quite possible that it was psychologically
impossible for them to believe in Christ apart from believing in the
message of the cross. But before the event of the cross at least, the message
of the cross does not appear to be a necessary psychological instrument for
people to come to saving faith. Therefore, I am skeptical that the object of
soteric faith has changed. The object of our faith remains that we believe
the proposition that whoever believes in Christ alone for eternal life has
it. But the primary instrumental means of bringing people to that
conclusion has changed from miracles to the miracle of the resurrection.
      The soteric content of the gospel would include: instrumental,
objective, explanatory, and supplementary material. Has the condition for
regeneration changed so that the gospel now requires that the lost believe
all this information in order to experience regeneration? Certainly not.
Hopefully, we will come to an agreement regarding the objective material,
but it may be impossible to definitely categorize the rest of the material. If
GES is correct (as I believe) that OSAS composes the necessary objective
content, then it may be that what is instrumental material for one person
would be supplementary for another. For example, NOSAS proponents
who believe in the cross for forgiveness of their sins are not saved because
they have not met the condition of believing in Christ for OSAS. For such
people, it could be that the message of our being sealed in Christ is what
would bring some of them to trust in Christ alone for eternal security. In
such a case, the sealing becomes the instrument message that brings such
persons to accept the objective message.
       For the NOSAS proponent, I might mention that the soteriological
objective content of the gospel need not be explicitly identified in the NT
with the word gospel as such but only implicitly referenced by inference.
Therefore, the soteric identification of our sealing with the gospel of our
salvation in Eph 1:13 need not exclude GJ as collaborative material even
though the word gospel does not occur in GJ. The soteric objective content
of the gospel need not be defined in such a way as to exclude Jn 6:47 and
1Tim 1:16. We believe in Christ for eternal life. This is true for pre-cross
and post-cross believers. Yet where is this objective content identified as
such with the word gospel? By this absence are we to assume that the
proclamation of the gospel did not contain this objective content? Contrary
to some of my kingdom friends who limit the gospel to the misthological
Page 110                                                    Carnal Corinth
message of the kingdom, I believe that GES has correctly identified the
heart of the soteric message of the Bible with its motto.
       The objective content did not change with the advent of the cross,
but the principle means of bringing people to Christ certainly changed with
the cross and resurrection. Must a person believe the gospel to be saved?
Yes. If I am allowed to identify the inferred objective soteriological
content of the gospel as I have done above, then I respond affirmatively.
But must a person believe all the gospel to be saved? Not hardly. There
are parts of the gospel that we are still discovering. Yet I have encountered
CG arguments that use 2Thess 1:8 to imply that the lost must believe the
entire gospel message in order to escape Hell. Not only is that a big
requirement, perhaps even an impossible one, but it potentially leads to a
false gospel. Paul refers to the gospel of grace in Acts 20:24, and Jesus
refers to the gospel of the kingdom (Mt 24:14). If we are not allowed to
differentiate the different elements of the more comprehensive term
gospel, then we are left with the false gospel of salvation by works,
specifically enduring to the end (cp. Mt 24:13). Undiscerning CG leads to
an accursed gospel.
       Even aside from this potential pitfall, my preference is to regard the
message of reviviscency (i.e., the resurrection and corresponding cross)
along with deity and exclusivity as truths which are used evangelistically
to bring us to saving faith. But they do not compete as multiple objects
which must be believed as theologically necessities in order for our faith
to be saving.
                              Epilogue
         I have had this book in an almost completed, albeit rough draft,
format on my computer for quite a number of years. As I look back and
recall what I have placed herein regarding my sons, Jeremy and Jonathan,
the years seem to have passed quickly. Both of my sons are attending and
teaching various Bible studies. The churches in our community are still
teaching LS theology from the pulpits. Their pastors have not proven to be
willing to discuss the issues. Nevertheless, I am happy to report that my
sons are promoting FG theology as they have the opportunity, and my
brother Philip is teaching a FG Sunday School class.
         My toleration for LS theology has reached zero over the years, so
rather than remain in Sylva where I would have to listen to LS sermons
from a pastor who will not listen, I have been driving fifty minutes to
Asheville where I can hear my friend, Scott Crawford, teach a FG Sunday
School class with a strong kingdom emphasis. Well worth the drive! And
the preaching at that church is at least rather neutral on the matters of LS
versus FG, so I do not have to get up and walk out—at least not very often!
         I have a number of books in semi-finished form. The occasion
causing me to release this particular book at this time is that my father, Al
Cauley, asked me to speak at his funeral if he should die before the Lord
returns. Consequently, I am producing a little booklet, A Tribute to Grace,
to summarize, at the lay level, both his and my transition from carnality to
spirituality. I wished to cite a scholastic work defending the existence of
carnal Christians for the sake of any LS pastors in our community who
may attend the funeral, so I have dusted the cobwebs off the present
manuscript to meet that need.
         Originally, this discussion was intended to be part of my book,
The Outer Darkness. But I had to remove about a third of the material from
that book to get it down to two volumes. This material was part of that
original discussion. Nevertheless, I deemed it to be suitable as a standalone
release and have done so in this book. I might add that the type of carnality
evidenced at Corinth is not the only type of carnality prevalent in our
society. Many believers trying to avail themselves of the Spirit-filled life
while maintaining a LS premise will probably find that most, if not all, of
their works, relative to inheriting the kingdom, will go up in smoke at the
Bema. They will weep in the outer darkness as a result. To a considerable
extent, accepting the reality of the carnality of the believers at Corinth
could have saved them from this tragedy. Therefore, this book is offered
in anticipated conjunction with my little booklet, A Tribute to Grace, to
make escape from the outer darkness possible to those believers who will
believe.
