                                                 Key Terms
        Anamartetology is derived from the Greek word sinless (anamartetos) used by John in Jn 8:7 and is employed
herein to refer to the doctrine of sinlessness.
        Aphesisology is a coined term derived from the Greek words aphesis (forgiveness) and logos (teaching) and
refers to the doctrine of forgiveness.
        Bema is a transliteration of the Greek word used in 2Cor 5:10 for the Judgment Seat of Christ and is used as an
abbreviation for the judgment of believers. The unqualified use of Bema will be understood in the typical
dispensational manner as referring to the Christian Bema—the judgment of church-age believers following the rapture.
Jewish Bema will refer to a similar judgment of Jewish believers following the tribulation. Gentile Bema (also known
as the Judgment of the Sheep and the Goats) would be used for the judgment of Gentile believers following the
tribulation (after the Jewish Bema). Unbelievers are judged 1000 years after the tribulation at the Great White Throne
(thronos) judgment of Rev 20:11.
        Marrowism is the present author’s coined term for describing his mediating non-Calvinistic Free Grace
theology, which is positioned between Arminianism (PEARS) and Calvinism (TULIP) and summarized herein by the
author’s acronym GRAPE. Historically, this movement was expressed in seminal form by the Marrow Controversy. In
a number of key areas, the present author may be considered a modern-day Marrowist. The present book is an
explanation of Marrowism as the present author has come to refine and define it. As used by the author, Marrowism
refers to a defense of unconditional security consisting of soteriological and misthological consistency. Marrowism is
summarized by the acronym GRAPE:
•   Gift is Free—means that the gift of eternal life is offered as a free gift and must be passively received as such.
•   Rewards are Earned—means that the distinction between unmerited gifts and merited rewards must not be
    blurred.
•   Absolute Assurance—means that personal assurance of one’s salvation from eternal damnation is the essence of
    saving faith.
•   Passive Persuasion—means that faith is a passive persuasion, not an active commitment.
•   Eternal Security—means that the person who trusts in Christ alone for eternal life immediately and eternally
    thereafter has eternal life and cannot experience eternal damnation. This doctrine of eternal security is known
    popularly as OSAS (once saved always saved) and is consistently and emphatically affirmed by GRAPE, in
    contrast to the conditional denunciation this doctrine receives in TULIP and PEARS. The affirmation of eternal
    security (E) is placed strategically at the end of the GRAPE acronym in order to contrast the most distinctive aspect
    of Marrowistic theology from the other two acronyms. Unlike Arminianism and Calvinism, Marrowism
    emphatically does not condition salvation from hell on the believer’s perseverance. GRAPE affirms true eternal
    security, not some Arminian or Calvinistic imitation of security. Marrowistic eternal security is unconditional
    security.
       Misthology is a coined term derived from the Greek words misthos (reward) and logos (teaching). It and its
derivatives will be used to refer to matters pertaining to the doctrine of rewards.
       Misthologist: refers to an advocate of unconditional soteriological security who has a sufficient degree of
misthological competency so as to recognize that the biblical teaching regarding the outer darkness pertains to
misthological themes so that salvation from the outer darkness depicts conditional misthological security.
       Mistholic is an abbreviation for misthological (that has been shortened syllabically to match its counterpart
soteric, which, in turn, is the abbreviation for soteriological).
       Soteriology is based on two biblical Greek words, soteria (salvation) and logos (teaching), and correspondingly
refers to biblical teaching about salvation. Although the biblical concept of salvation includes many dimensions other
than deliverance from hell, it is this aspect of salvation to which writers refer frequently when they use the word
soteriology. Thus, salvation from hell (or more specifically, from eternal damnation in the Lake of Fire) will be referred
to as soteriological salvation.
       Unconditional security is the conviction that once a person believes in Christ for eternal life, that person is
unconditionally secure in Christ and unconditionally assured of reaching heaven unconditionally. Technically, this
articulation is superior to saying that believers are unconditionally assured of reaching heaven. In Calvinism, God
unconditionally elects certain individuals to reach heaven through means of their perseverance. The elect not only will
persevere, they must persevere in order to reach heaven. Within Calvinism, reaching heaven is conditioned on the
perseverance of the elect. In contradistinction, unconditional security does not condition reaching heaven upon the
believer’s perseverance. Accordingly, unconditional election is not to be confused with unconditional security.

                                                    Introduction
       “To fear or not to fear?” is the question many would ask in regard to the Judgment Seat of Christ, otherwise
known as the Bema.1 For reasons explained in Mere Christianity and Moral Christianity, the mascot for the
Marrowistic school of thought is the meerkat because it fearlessly devours the arguments of those presenting poisonous
forms of the gospel of grace. Thus, the meerkat was pitted against various scorpions in that book. An ostrich rather than
a scorpion serves better for the opposing mascot in the present book, however, because many of those holding the
opposing point of view regarding the Bema correctly understand the gospel of grace. Nevertheless, they tend not to fear
the Bema because they bury their heads in the sand when regarding its logical and biblical implications regarding the
gospel of the kingdom.

Not every ostrich will offer all these excuses not to fear the Bema. Some may only offer one or two such explanations.
Regardless, none of these reasons are legitimate reasons not to fear the Bema. Nevertheless, there are various degrees
of truth to each of these misperceptions. The kernel of truth is wrapped in deception, nonetheless, when such positions
are used to justify the supposition that believers do not need to fear the Bema.

abbreviation for the judgment of believers. The unqualified use of Bema will be understood in the typical
dispensational manner as referring to the Christian Bema—the judgment of church-age believers following the rapture.
Jewish Bema will refer to a similar judgment of Jewish believers following the tribulation. Gentile Bema (also known
as the Judgment of the Sheep and the Goats) would be used for the judgment of Gentile believers and living unbelievers
following the tribulation (after the Jewish Bema). Those not judged previously are judged 1000 years after the
tribulation at the Great White Throne (thronos) judgment of Rev 20:11.

        The opening premise herein is that, like Paul, believers should fear the Bema. Paul had a healthy fear of the
Bema. Rather than being paralyzed or demoralized by that fear, he was prompted to appropriate action because of it:
“For we must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ, that each one may receive the things done in the body,
according to what he has done, whether good or bad. Knowing, therefore, the terror [phobos] of the Lord, we persuade
men” (2Cor 5:10-11; NKJ). Paul finds the prospects of the Lord’s Bema terrifying and used that terrifying prospect to
urge the carnal Corinthian believers to become reconciled to God (2Cor 5:20). Naturally, since only believers will stand
before the Judgment Seat of Christ, those whom Paul is contextually urging to be reconciled to God in view of the
coming Bema are believers. They already have been reconciled to God in terms of their position; now they need to be
reconciled to God in terms of their experience: to be what they are.2 Even Paul was motivated by the fear of the Bema.
The Greek word for fear here is phobos, the word from which we get phobia. Ostriches think that fear of the Bema is a
phobia, an irrational fear. To the contrary, although Paul was motivated by phobia, it was a rational rather than
irrational fear.
        Does this mean that meerkats live in fear of the Bema? Paradoxically, the answer to that question is both “Yes”
and “No,” depending upon what is meant by fear. From one point of view, it is appropriate to say that meerkats have no
fear of the Bema. The proper rationale for not fearing the Bema will be provided after we first engage some of the
erroneous reasons held by the opposing school of thought represented by the ostrich.
        Perhaps the most common objection to the assertion that the Bema is punitive is derived from the nature of
soteriological forgiveness.3 Hence this response is listed at the top of the list of erroneous reasons provided by the
ostrich for not fearing the Bema. Supposedly, this judicial forgiveness level of sins is so complete that it renders
judicial punishment of sin impossible. Some passages commonly cited in this capacity include: Ps 103:12; Is 38:17;
44:22; Micah 7:19; Col 2:13; Heb 8:12. Since the forgiveness that saves us from Hell is judicial in nature, those of the
ostrich persuasion insist we cannot need that same type of forgiveness in other areas, such as the Bema. Because God
already has forgiven us judicially at the cross, we cannot be judged judicially at the Bema. Otherwise, double jeopardy
would be involved. So they argue that after Christ already has paid the price for our judicial forgiveness, a judicial view
of the Bema wrongly would have us paying for our sins in this same judicial capacity yet again. Thus a judicial
understanding of the Bema is impossible in their opinion. The judgment of the believer cannot include the judgment of
his or her sins. We cannot be punished for our sin, so we cannot be punished for our sinful works. Forgiveness and
chastisement are familial rather than judicial (cp. 1Jn 1:9; Heb 12:6). Judicial forgiveness must be limited to the
soteriological dimension. Furthermore, since our sin and sin nature are removed completely in heaven, no need for
forgiveness or chastisement remains at the Bema. Like the rewards dispensed at the Olympian Games, which supplies
the historical backdrop, the Bema must exclusively be an awards podium, not a judgment seat where punishments are
rendered. Thus this position can be appropriately labeled the awards-podium (AP) position.
                                      Level One—Soteriological Forgiveness
       The appeal to such passages as those listed above actually backfires, when many of the passages used in this
capacity are examined in context. Some of these passages are not used in a soteriological capacity, or at least not
exclusively or even primarily in that capacity. But if they describe judicial forgiveness outside of the soteriological
sphere, then obviously judicial forgiveness cannot be limited to the soteriological dimension. Before proceeding to
those verses used outside of the soteriological arena, laying a correct scriptural foundation for a soteriological
understanding would be prudent. After laying that foundation, we will then proceed up the strata or vertical planes of
the various types of forgiveness available to believers until we arrive at last at Bema forgiveness—misthological
forgiveness.4

     As we move up these stratified planes and interact with the data, we will notice certain characteristics of these
strata, so as to formulate the Principle of Stratification to summarize certain key truths about how these strata build
upon and interact with one another. In any field of science, one seeks to observe and classify the data. Geologists, for
example, observe various layers in the Earth’s crust and formulate theories to explain the sequence and interaction of
these layers. Theology is a science which explores doctrine. Therefore, as misthologists5 examine the data pertaining to
the doctrine of forgiveness (aphesisology6), we must, as good scientists, form hypotheses to explain why these layers of
forgiveness form observable strata of forgiveness. Additionally, we must supply suitable theories as to the rules
governing how these levels interact with one another. Some misthologists have only distinguished two such levels of
forgiveness. A more precise scientific inquiry into the data will reveal four such sequential strata in relation to the
levels of forgiveness available to God’s children by their heavenly Father. Because this study is focused on the
misthological forgiveness available to God’s children and presumes a soteriological foundation, it will not interact with
data that appears to be inclusive of those outside that frame of reference. For instance, when the Bible urges
unbelievers to repent for the forgiveness of sins (Mk 1:4; Lk 3:3), such forgiveness might not presume a soteriological
foundation. In such a case, the forgiveness granted would not be that found within pre-existing a familial relationship
so as to be offering familial fellowship. Thus, even though the forgiveness offered to the unregenerate might include a
degree of fellowship, it would not be a familial fellowship built upon a family relationship and hence is not part of the
subject matter of this present study. The focus of this study presumes a soteriological foundation and consequently is
limited to the study of God’s forgiveness in relation to His children.

       The stratum into which some of these passages should be placed is questionable.7 In fact, some passages are
intended to function dualistically, that is, in more than one stratum. For example, Acts 10:43 attributes a soteriological
understanding of forgiveness to all the OT prophets: “Of Him all the prophets bear witness that through His name
everyone who believes in Him receives forgiveness of sins.” Even if an OT passage was not used in a strictly or
exclusively soteric manner in its original context, the NT appears to expect us to see how it might have soteriological
depth. The Principle of Duality (PD) allows that possibility.8 The famous passage regarding the bronze serpent
provides an unquestionable OT example of this phenomenon.

7
  Previously in my writings I referred to the bottom stratum as judicial forgiveness. However, when I then went on to
explain misthological forgiveness, I had to explain that it too was judicial, just not judicial in the soteriological sense.
Rather than use this cumbersome approach in the present book, the term soteriological forgiveness is implemented as
opposed to misthological forgiveness. Both soteriological and misthological forgiveness pertain to judicial (i.e.,
forensic) issues in their own respective compartments or fields of reference. In a court of law, the death penalty is the
severest penalty because of its permanency. If need be, terminal and misthological penalties may also invoke the death
penalty, thus bearing full witness to their judicial/forensic nature. So one aspect of the Principle of Stratification, as it is
being developed herein, is to note that just because forgiveness is granted in a judicial sense in one stratum or
compartment, this does not prevent it from being granted again, even in a judicial sense, in another stratum or
compartment. The law of non-contradiction is not violated because the sin is not considered forgiven yet unforgiven in

        God has promised to forget our sins. Since God cannot lie, He must be able to forget our sins. On the other hand,
since God is omniscient, He cannot forget our sins—or so it would seem. After all, being omniscient means that He
must know everything. So how can an omniscient God forget something? On the other hand, if God is all powerful,
does He not have the power to forget our sins? Does an insurmountable tension exist between God’s omnipotence and
His omniscience? The key to resolving this paradox, according to Murphree, is found in understanding the difference
between God’s power and ability.11 Yes, God is all-powerful. The Bible repeatedly refers to Him as Almighty. One of
the words so translated is pantokrator. This Greek word is used of God in a number of passages. One such passage is
Rev 19:6, where some translations render it as Almighty, others as Omnipotent (KJV, NKJ, MRD, PNT, RWB, TNT,
WEB), or as All-Powerful (NET). Our Lord God Almighty certainly is omnipotent and all-powerful. He has the
might/power to do anything that is the function of might/power. His might/power is infinite. Nothing is too hard for
God (Jer 32:17). Nothing is impossible for God (Mt 1:37). All things are possible for God (Mt 19:26; Mk 10:27;
14:36). So can God do anything? No. According to the Bible, there are a number of things that God does not have the
ability to do.
        Morally, God cannot look favorably upon evil (Hab 1:13); He cannot deny Himself (2Tim 2:13); He cannot lie
(Tit 1:2; Heb 6:18); He cannot be tempted by evil (Jam 1:13). He is unable to lie in that His nature prevents Him from
doing so. He is incapable of lying, not because He is impotent in the sense of lacking the power to do so but because He
lacks the ability to do so. We might summarize in terms of two equations:

           •   Power ≠ ability
           •   Power > ability.

       Logically, we may note that God’s power to do anything (that He morally would be able to do) does not mean
that He can do non-things (i.e., nothings). He cannot do things that are logically contradictory. Two examples will
suffice:

           •   God cannot make round squares.
           •   God cannot make a rock too large for Him to lift.12

        Autonomously, God has power over His power. He has the power and ability to limit the exercise of His power
and ability. God has the ability to forget sin, for example. But how can God forget something without ceasing to be
omniscient? Does this not involve a logical contradiction, which is something God cannot do? Since He knows
everything, how can He forget anything? How can God know all things, as 1Jn 3:20 affirms, if He has forgotten some
things? The distinction between knowledge and consciousness provides Murphree with the appropriate tools to resolve
this dilemma. Summarization with two equations is again possible:

           •   Knowledge ≠ consciousness
           •   Knowledge > consciousness

       God’s chastisement of His children, therefore, may be clarified as occurring in four possible forms: (1) internal,
(2) external, (3) terminal, or (4) misthological. The first three are temporal (i.e., experienced in time) and are thus
temporary. The last one is eschatological and thus eternal. God initially disciplines every believer temporally (Heb
12:6-8), and ultimately He will discipline them misthologically (Rev 3:19), but He may cease to discipline a believer
temporally.

                                           Internal and External Discipline
       The mildest form of God’s discipline is conviction and instruction through His word and the believer’s
biblically-informed conscience, pictured by the Bible in the above illustration. If believers resist or prove insensitive to
that form of discipline, then God may move to external forms of correction. When the author’s children were very
young, he would keep a little switch or fly swat handy in each room of the house so that he could render external
discipline as necessary, being a firm believer in the proverb: spare the rod, spoil the child. Biblical discipline is an
expression of Fatherly love (Prov 13:24; Heb 12:6). Such discipline can be administered in various degrees of severity.
Some would describe this type of discipline as being taken to God’s woodshed. It may be experienced as sickness or
various degrees and forms of calamity.

                                          Terminal and Misthological Discipline
        Should God’s children prove to be rebellious when submitted to this form of reproof, God may progress to
terminal discipline by one of two courses of action. He may chastise His children with premature death. Considering
the alternative, in certain cases this might even be considered, at least in part, to be a mercy killing. In mercy God may
kill a disobedient believer before such a believer can do even more damage to his or her testimony, which in turn would
result in an even worse degree of misthological punishment at the JSC.33 On the other hand, premature death is more
likely than not, in most cases at least, probably part of God’s terminal discipline. But terminal discipline does not
necessarily require premature death. God may decide to allow believers to persist in their error, and swear in His wrath
that such believers will not inherit the misthological throne. From the perspective of eternity, this latter alternative
frequently could be much more severe than premature death. Regardless, since terminal discipline is not limited to
premature death, terminal discipline might more broadly be defined as the death of hope—misthological hope. There is
no longer any hope of ruling with Christ in heaven in any capacity for those subjected to this form of discipline. As a
result, they will be subjected to the maximum form of chastisement—misthological exclusion from rulership.
        Temporal discipline may be resisted and temporally cease. Believers may persist in sin to such a degree or
duration that they become without discipline (Heb 12:8). Simon Magus was warned of the possibility of a premature
death (Acts 8:20). He was spared premature death and incurred something much worse. Although Magus’ temporal

forgiveness. Sin is not something to be trifled with. It hurts. Forgiveness does not necessarily erase all the pain that God
may inflict because of one’s sin.
        In regard to the Principle of Stratification being developed herein, it must be observed from the data that
forgiveness in a lower plane or compartment does not ensure forgiveness in a higher plane. This is a rock solid
deduction. In the present case, God compartmentalized part of His forgiveness so that it only applied to stratum two.
The Lord immediately forgave David’s confessed sin in regards to restored fellowship, but He did not completely
forgive David’s confessed sin in regard to not punishing him for his sin. God withheld forgiveness at this upper
stratum. Upon David’s confession God immediately granted him level-two forgiveness but withheld level-three
forgiveness. In accord with other observations being noted herein, however, the hypothesis will be advanced that since
David will inherit the kingdom, God’s temporally withholding a level-three forgiveness from David does not prevent
God from granting a level-four forgiveness to David eternally regarding this sin. This observation either annuls my
hypothesis that an upper level of forgiveness necessarily entails forgiveness for that sin in all the lower planes, so that
forgiveness can skip one plane in its vertical assent, or it requires that we allow the possibility that God forgave
David’s sin at the level-three stratum after He disciplined him at the level-three stratum.
        In other words, we know that forgiveness is stratified, no doubt about it. What we do not know for certain is if
one or more intermediate strata might be missing in some instances. In the geological column, for example, not all
possible layers of soil are found in all geological digs. Sometimes, the expected intervening soils types are missing.
Sometimes the sequence of the geological layers is inverted. Various theories are then advanced to explain these
anomalies. I am suggesting that aphesisological layers do not contain such anomalies, although they contain anomalies
of their own. The layers of forgiveness are always found in the expected sequence in the aphesisological column, not
inverted or mixed. But in order to maintain this rule, implementing the Principle of Duality is necessary.
        As has been demonstrated on more than one occasion already, this caveat must be added: A passage can be used
in separate capacities or implement a principle that has application to more than one layer. The question at hand in the
present passage is not, “Can the sequence be inverted?” It cannot be. Rather, the real question is, “Can some
intermediate levels be skipped?” I am skeptical that an intermediate layer can be skipped or missing in the
misthological column, at least not under normal circumstances. Third-level forgiveness presupposes second-level
forgiveness, as the intervening prerequisite forgiveness after first-level forgiveness has been forgiven. One would not
forgive another of the temporal consequences of a sin without forgiving in terms of fellowship. Fourth-level
forgiveness likewise presumes third and second-level forgiveness. Intermediate levels cannot be skipped. The

        My hypothesis that an expected layer cannot be skipped encounters a difficulty in 2Sam 12:10, however.
Although not mentioned in this context, we know that since David will rule in the millennial kingdom, David
ultimately is granted level-four forgiveness. So how could he be granted level-four forgiveness without first being
granted level-three forgiveness for this sin? The verse states: “Now therefore, the sword shall never depart from your
house, because you have despised Me and have taken the wife of Uriah the Hittite to be your wife” (2Sam 12:10). If
God never forgave David for this sin during David’s lifetime, then (1) I would have to amend my Principle of
Stratification so as to allow the possibility that forgiveness can jump a level, or (2) I would have to allow that once
God’s discipline had run its course at level three, then level-three forgiveness was granted, even though it was at the
end of David’s life. If I had to choose between the two options, I would lean toward the latter. In that case, God forgave
David before he died, so level-three forgiveness was not skipped, just delayed until the end of David’s life. The
Principle of Stratification would not need to be amended to allow skips in the strata in this case.
        However, the more straightforward proposal, derived directly from the data as it is presented in this context, is
readily at hand. A third option cries for attention. Note carefully that God does not say in this verse, “I will cause the
sword never to depart from your house.” Whereas God takes ownership for personal involvement in taking David’s
child and wives away from him, the Lord does not take personal ownership for the sword never departing from David’s
house. This detail is exegetically significant. My hypothesis is that God is not taking personal ownership for the sword
because He plans to grant David level-three forgiveness immediately after taking David’s child and wives away from
him. At that point, God will cease His active role in David’s discipline. After that point, God will take a passive role
and simply allow the Law of the Harvest (LH), also known as the Law of Sowing and Reaping, to follow its natural
course. This law functions in various capacities in Scripture (covering anything from Lk 6:38 and 2Cor 9:6 to Gal 6:7-
8). Adopting this proposal as my working hypothesis, the Principle of Stratification needs this clarification: The
forgiveness of sin in the temporal stratum does not necessarily nullify the Law of the Harvest in that stratum.
        This proposition is entirely reasonable and is commonly observed by others. For example, new believers do not
necessarily have immediate temporal deliverance from the negative habits and addictions in their lives, even though
they are forgiven of those sins at the point of conversion. God does not necessarily remove the sexually transmitted
diseases that new believers’ have as a result of their preconversional behavior just because they became believers.
Consequently, 2Sam 12:10 is best considered an example of the Law of the Harvest rather than the Principle of
Stratification. For this reason, verse 10 is not included in the passage quoted in Illustration 4. Four Types of Vertical
Forgiveness. The range of this passage, as it pertains to the Principle of Stratification, is 2Sam 12:11-18. Verse 10 is
excluded because it pertains to the Law of the Harvest, a supplemental role to the topic at hand. As a result, the
Principle of Stratification does not need modification, only this clarification: Level-three forgiveness removes God’s
active role in the discipline but does not necessarily remove His passive role in allowing the Law of the Harvest to
come to full fruition and play a supplemental role.

into eternity future, as David will show us, is to obey God fearfully.

        The soteriological interpretation, which I originally held, assumes that the continuity of the parable
indiscriminately uses the same word doulas (slave/servant) for the person in question throughout the parable. As a
result, he is called a slave both before and after his conversion. Therefore, the term slave could be used to describe
someone who is not yet a believer, since the individual in this story is commonly thought to be an unsaved slave at the
beginning of the parable. According to this popular perception, the forgiveness in Mt 18:27 would be soteric
forgiveness. Indeed, the parable can be interpreted in this manner without necessarily causing any problems for FG
theology. Jesus simply is using the word slave to describe someone who experiences salvation but who subsequently
fails to demonstrate the reality of that salvation in his life, and this subsequent failure has misthological repercussions,
but the misthological consequences are not as severe as the original soteriological pronouncements in that his property,
wife, and children are not sold. As a result of his initial soteric forgiveness (picturing salvation by grace apart from
works), the slave’s subsequent failure to demonstrate his lord’s merciful nature is not dealt with as harshly as originally
specified.47 Thus a soteric view that is compatible with FG theology could be adopted theologically; nevertheless, any
such soteriological interpretation must be rejected exegetically. Therefore, if soteriological applicability is allowed, it is
only done secondarily through implementation of the Principle of Duality, which, as demonstrated in the present study,
allows a non-soteriological text to be given a secondary soteriological applicability. In any event, the forgiveness
pictured in v. 27 is a familial forgiveness, which according to the rules governing the strata being examined herein,
must presume soteriological forgiveness. One cannot have familial forgiveness if one is not part of the family.48

                                                    Mistholic Interpretation
         A straightforward exegesis of the passage strongly indicates that the slave already was saved before his sins
were forgiven in Mt18:27. Contextually, the parable is not dealing with someone who becomes a believer/slave but
someone who already is a believer/slave. The debt incurred to him by his fellow slave (picturing the debt between
believing brethren) occurred before he was forgiven in v. 27. In other words, the debt of a hundred denarii between
these two slaves occurred while the wicked slave was incurring his debt of ten thousand talents, not afterwards.
The significance of this fact should not be ignored. This smaller debt which pictures the debt between two believing
brethren was incurred before the forgiveness pronounced in v. 27. Thus, the forgiveness pictured in v. 27 is not the
forgiveness that results in one becoming a believing brother. The wicked slave already is a believing brother before he
receives the forgiveness pictured in v. 27. The most reasonable deduction from the sequence (and from the notification
that what is being described is forgiveness among brethren) is that the wicked slave already was saved before he was
granted the forgiveness pictured in v. 27. The forgiveness pictured in this verse must be the temporal forgiveness of an
already saved slave. This verse is not depicting the soteric forgiveness that we get when we first become
believers/slaves (at least not exclusively or even primarily) but the forgiveness that we experience as believers/slaves
after we already have become believers/slaves. Our performance as believers, not as unbelievers, is under review.
         The misthological forgiveness of our postconversional sins is conditioned, in part, on our dealings with our
fellow believing brethren. Jesus goes on to portray this reality by describing what happens to the slave in v. 34 after his
postconversional temporal forgiveness in v. 27. The wicked slave incurs his debt in v. 27 while he is a slave in service
to his master. Similarly, after we become believers, we incur a large sin debt (Mt 6:12) while we are working in the
Lord’s service. Nevertheless, as we confess our sins, the Lord forgives us (cp. 1Jn 1:9). By confessing our sins, we can
avoid the premature death that otherwise could have a devastating financial and emotional impact on our wife and
children. Nevertheless, our misthological forgiveness of those postconversional, confessed sins is contingent on our
forgiving our brothers and sisters in Christ. If we do not forgive them, then our postconversional sins, which were
forgiven temporally, will be taken into account misthologically at the Bema, and we will have to pay the consequences
personally. Thankfully, our family will not have to pay the consequences along with us at that time. Therefore, not only
does the forgiven-yet-unforgiven slave in this parable represent a believer, but he is a believer from the very beginning
of the parable. There is no contextual justification for regarding v. 27 as a picture of justification salvation or for
thinking that the slave’s initial forgiveness pictures the conversion of the unsaved.
         We are not literal slaves, we are not given ten thousand literal talents, and we are not literally handed over to
literal torturers. The parable is using pictorial language. Nevertheless, the picture it presents is that the consequences of
not forgiving others will be severe. If we are unforgiving in our dealings with our believing brethren, we can expect a
similar outcome. Sins which are forgiven in one sense may still be punished in another sense. James also describes this
same truth didactically rather than parabolically in Jam 2:13. He urges believers to show mercy because judgment will
be merciless to those believers who show no mercy. Believers are not automatically merciful as is seen by the need for
the warning. The fact that James is talking about the judgment of believers is stated again in Jam 3:1, where he warns
that Christian teachers will receive a stricter judgment. He makes it clear that some believers will experience stricter
judgment. Also, compare his comments in Jam 5:9,12. The people whom James is warning about the seriousness of
judgment are not the lost but the saved, the brethren. Believers are in danger of the merciless judgment in Jam 2:13.
Although this judgment has its temporal dimension, the primary and ultimate expression of this judgment is its
eschatological dimension as is seen in Jam 5:8-9. The judgment that believers will experience when the Lord comes
again is entailed. Thus, this judgment takes place at the time of the rapture and at the Judgment Seat of Christ. James
understood Jesus’ parable; so should we.

                                                  Conclusion
       Picturing the four basic types of forgiveness as parallel planes allows the summarization that all of a believer’s
sins have been forgiven soteriologically: past, present and future. There is no sin that a child of God can commit that
will undo his or her relationship with God. The forgiveness granted at that level cannot be undone at that level.
Forgiveness of a person’s sin is irrevocable at the level at which it is given. Children of God remain children of God
regardless of what happens. But just because all of a believer’s sins have been forgiven soteriologically does not mean
that those sins have been forgiven familially. To experience fellowship with God, believers must confess their known
sins. But even when God’s children confess their sins and experience fellowship with God as a result, their Father still
may decide to discipline them nonetheless. Granting forgiveness in terms of fellowship does not necessarily mean that
God has forgiven His child in the sense of not punishing His child temporally. Moreover, even in those cases where the
Lord decides to withhold temporal punishment, this does not necessarily ensure that He will withhold misthological
punishment. In fact, one reason God may dispense with temporal judgment on occasion is because He is going to deal
with the issue misthologically.
       When my son, Jonathan, was very young, he misbehaved badly, while my wife and I were in a public setting
that would not be conducive to the type of discipline I would have been inclined to inflict had we been at home.
Perhaps Jonathan thought that he would be able to get away with such behavior since we were in public. He was
mistaken. When we got back in the car to take the twenty minute drive back home, I simply said, “I will deal with you
when we get home.” In short order Jonathan apologized for his behavior and asked for forgiveness. Forgiveness was
granted, immediately. I was no longer angry with him. Cognizant of this fact, Jonathan then asked, “Will I still be
disciplined when we get home?” My response was, “Yes.” The discipline would no longer be an expression of my
fatherly emotional anger, but it would still be administered as a demonstration of my fatherly wrath (i.e., my volitional
displeasure with my son’s previous behavior).
       Believers who have been forgiven of all their sins soteriologically will find that they still need to be forgiven
familially in order to have fellowship with their Father temporally. Even when those sins have been forgiven familially
and are no longer a barrier emotionally, they may still be dealt with eschatologically. Once God swears in His wrath
(orge) that His children will not enter into the rest of millennial rulership (Heb 3:11; 4:3), they can be certain that
misthological forgiveness of such behavior, in terms of being allowed to rule in the kingdom, is no longer an option.
Misthological discipline will inevitably come. It is God’s way of saying, “I will deal with you when you get home.”

practicing unrighteousness. Paul is saying that anyone who degrades himself or herself by participation in such
unrighteous behavior will not inherit the kingdom. Everyone who lives like this, even if they are believers, will not be
entitled to rule the future kingdom. Believers guilty of such behavior may enter the kingdom, but they will not own the
place.
        Verse 8 specifies that the unrighteous people, to whom Paul is addressing the warning, are the Corinthian
readers and thus genuine believers. Paul does not question the genuineness of their conversion; he confirms it and
appeals to it in verse 11. Soteriologically, they were (1) forgiven, (2) sanctified, and (3) justified—even in regard to
these very sins they are now committing. For this reason, they should stop committing these sins. It is another way of
Paul urging, Be what you have become rather than what you used to be. You are saved, so start acting like it!
        To be sure, unbelievers are referred to as the unrighteous (hoi adikoi) in 1Cor 6:1. However, this would be an
unlikely antecedent for verse 9 because (1) unrighteous is not a technical term reserved for the lost (cp. Lk 16:10-11).
(2) The verbal form (adikeo) in verse 8 is the closer antecedent, and for this reason some commentators believe that
verse 9 is referring back to the unrighteous behavior of verse 8 rather than the unrighteous group of verse 1. (3) The
grammar itself would suggest that the articular form in verse 1 refers to the classification of a group as being
unrighteous; whereas, the anarthrous form in verse 9 is describing the character of being unrighteous. In other words,
the fact that these believers act like the lost in their character does not demonstrate that they are classified with the lost,
but it does show that they express the same behavior as the lost. (4) The warning about not inheriting is addressed to
genuine believers.
        The link between hoi adikoi (the unrighteous) in verse 1 and Paul’s similar description for the behavior of the
saved in verses 1 and 8 is not accidental. This description shows that those who are saved may express the same
character as those who are lost. Paul reminds his readers that the unjust judges of the world will not judge the world to
come. Unbelievers will not inherit the kingdom because of their lack of righteousness. Believers need righteousness,
Excursus on being Washed Clean                                                                                    Page 57
both imputed and imparted, to inherit the kingdom and judge the world. Using the Principle of Correlativity, Paul
purposefully ties the believer’s potential lack of inheritance together with the definite lack thereof on the part of
unbelievers. Not all believers will inherit the kingdom. Those believers who live like unbelievers certainly will not
inherit the kingdom.
        In summary, the articular hoi adikoi (the unrighteous) is used in offsetting parallelism with hoi hagioi (the
righteous; i.e., the saints) to differentiate the lost from the saved as a class. However, within the classification of the
saved, there are those who behave as if they were lost. They are not holy (hagios) in their behavior. Instead, Paul
accuses these believers of behaving unrighteously (adikeo), and he proves that this is the case with his law-court
indictment against them. He then uses an anarthrous adikoi, which is the plural form of unrighteous (adikos), to link
their behavior characteristically with those who will not inherit the kingdom categorically. Believers who live
unrighteously are placing themselves characteristically in the same misthologically disqualified set as those who
categorically will not inherit the kingdom. Yet such believers are not categorized with the lost. Carnal believers do not
lose their salvation or become a part of the articular hoi adikoi. Although all saints potentially are qualified to render
judgment in the church and rule the world categorically, only some saints actually are qualified to do so
characteristically. Other saints will be disqualified characteristically. Not all saints will rule.
                                             Unrighteous versus Immoral
      Paul started off his vice list with fornicators: “Do not be deceived; neither characteristic fornicators
[pornoi], nor idolaters, nor adulterers, nor effeminate, nor homosexuals, nor thieves, nor the covetous,
nor drunkards, nor revilers, nor swindlers, will inherit the kingdom of God” (1Cor 6:9-10). The lexical
form of this word is pornos (immorality), the Greek word from which we get pornography: immoral
writings. This word is also featured in two of the Johannine vice lists. The Greek word that Paul uses for
idolaters (eidololatres) is also repeated in these same two vice lists. Yet simply using pornoi will suffice
as a visual reminder of the connection between the Pauline and Johannine vice lists for sake of
simplicity.
Johannine Vice Lists
      For sake of completeness, the additional parallel Johannine vice list is included in the list below. These verses
have been translated in a manner consistent with the Pauline renditions above:

    •   “But as for the categorically cowardly and unbelieving and abominable and murderers and fornicators
        [pornoi] and sorcerers and idolaters and all the categorical liars, their portion will be in the lake
        that burns with fire and brimstone, which is the second death” (Rev 21:8; TM).

    •   “But nothing unclean will enter it [the Heavenly City], nor the [?] one who categorically commits
        abomination or falsehood, but only those whose names are written in the Lamb’s book of life” (Rev 21:27;
        TM).

    •   “The categorical dogs and the categorical sorcerers and the categorical fornicators [pornoi] and the
        categorical murderers and the categorical idolaters are outside [the Heavenly City], and each of the
        ones who categorically love and practice lying” (Rev 22:15; TM).
Corresponding Johannine Promises
        Those who fall into the above category will have their portion in the Lake of Fire, which is the second death.
These passages are describing what takes place during the eternal state. They are recapitulations of what John had just
described as happing to unbelievers at the end of the millennial kingdom, and thus at the commencement of the eternal
state: “And death and Hades were thrown into the lake of fire. This is the second death, the lake of fire. And if
anyone’s name was not found written in the book of life, he was thrown into the lake of fire” (Rev 20:14-15). These
passages describe the fate of unbelievers. For sake of comparison, two related promises to overcomers are included:

    •   “The one who categorically overcomes will not be hurt by the second death” (Rev 2:11; TM).

    •   “Blessed and holy is the one who categorically has a portion in the first resurrection; the second death
        has no power over such ones; but they will be priests of God and of Christ and will rule with Him
        throughout the millennium” (Rev 20:6).

       Whereas Paul envisions exclusion from the reward of inheriting the kingdom, John envisions exclusion from the
Reward City. The perspectives are very similar and in complete harmony. What Paul makes explicit in the vice-list

behavior indulged in by those are categorically excluded from the reward, unfaithful believers will share in the
exclusion from the reward and be hurt in a correlative but not identical manner by the second death.

        Although both unbelievers and unfaithful believers are both excluded, recognizing that the exclusion of the
unfaithful believers happens at different strata is crucially important for both writers. For the Pauline stratification, the
soteriological versus misthological plane has been implemented in the illustration. For the Johannine stratification, a
playpen (serving as a sheep pen) has been juxtaposed with the literal fires of the Lake of Fire. The playpen will not be
in the fire! Neither soteriology nor misthology have any excuse for confusing God’s playpen for His immature children
with His punishment of the unregenerate in the Lake of Fire. Both the Lake of Fire and the playpen are outside the city,
but that does not mean that the playpen is inside the Lake of Fire.
