                                                     Introduction
       In some churches, presenting more than one point of view is considered disadvantageous. Doing so might
cause divisions. Some might take a point of view different from the one taken by the leadership. Such concerns are
valid—to a point. One wonders, though, if trying to protect congregants from everything does not, at times, stunt
their growth. Is it not akin to giving them only milk to drink? Can they really be given meat very effectively if they
are limited to one point of view? The writer of Hebrews chastised his readers, saying, “For everyone who partakes
only of milk is not accustomed to the word of righteousness, for he is a babe. But solid food is for the mature, who
because of practice have their senses trained to discern good and evil” (Heb 5:13-14). How can a congregation be
trained to discern good from evil without practice in discerning good interpretations from bad ones? Or how can
iron sharpen iron if only one point of view is allowed to be considered? The Bible advises: “Iron sharpens iron, so
one man sharpens another” (Prov 27:17). In some churches, it would seem, such a biblical practice is despised rather
than encouraged. The biblical scrutiny demonstrated in the Berean spirit of Acts 17:11 shows an openness to biblical
investigation, not a censorship that precludes the necessity of a Berean examination. Paul likewise urged the
Thessalonians, “Examine everything carefully; hold fast to that which is good” (1Thess 5:21).1
       Some may think that limiting oneself to one point of view will make one stronger in that point of view rather
than an investigation that is open to consideration of the evidence from multiple points of view. To a degree this is
true. Those with limited interest, time, or resources to consider the pros and cons of a point of view should probably
be given the predigested assessment provided by the church leadership. The congregation can just sit back and be
spoonfed. If this is the only diet to which congregants are being exposed, however, can we really be surprised if
they show an inability to make their own discernments?
       On the other hand, holding one’s position from an informed point of view can certainly strengthen and deepen
one’s convictions at times as well. For example, as I have spent many years studying the outer darkness from
multiple points of view, my own views on the outer darkness have grown much stronger and deeper. Stepping
outside of my own paradigm to look at the doctrine of the outer darkness through the eyes of those holding
conflicting or competing points of view has had an immeasurable impact on my own perception. Ironically, it has
made me more tolerant and intolerant. Bad teaching regarding this doctrine tends to be very annoying to me. Yet I
have assisted others in developing competing models to my own model, and some of these models are extremely
attractive. I not only tolerate some of these competing models but find them very appealing, so much so that I am
entertaining a modification of my original model in this series.
       An additional benefit that comes from looking at Scripture from multiple vantage points is that, in a number
of cases, it helps one see Scripture as it itself intends to be seen—from multiple vantage points.2 Teaching a
congregation to see every passage from a singular point of view is very disadvantageous to a proper study of
Scripture when the Scripture itself teaches us to look at it from multiple vantage points in order to appreciate and
understand its multifaceted beauty. Is not part of the beauty of a diamond its multifaceted sparkle? Furthermore, not
being able to perceive the multidimensional nature of the Bible can be downright deadly at times. Many, even most,
have failed to perceive the basic difference between a gift and reward in the Bible for the simple reason that they
cannot see the multidimensional nature of some key concepts. Confusing the gift of eternal life with the reward of
eternal life causes many from obtaining either form of eternal life. The purpose of this chapter, then, is to bring

                                            Brief Examples of Duality
       Before surveying some of the more involved examples of duality discussed in detailed elsewhere in my
writings, a few classic examples will suffice to illustrate the legitimacy of this approach. On the one hand, we are
told that nothing can separate us from God’s love (Rom 8:38-39). On the other hand, we must keep ourselves in
God’s love (Jn 15:10; Jude 1:21). We are forgiven (Col 2:13-14), yet we still need forgiveness (1Jn 1:9). We are
promised that we will not come into judgment (Jn 5:24), yet we are assured that we will be judged (2Cor 5:10). We
are told that God may forsake us (2Chron 15:2), yet we are told that He will never forsake us (Heb 13:5). Such
tensions can be resolved by postulating that we are unconditionally loved, forgiven, spared from judgment, and
unforsaken at one level but not at another level. How about the rapture? Is the rapture imminent or not—or both?
Stearman acknowledges the difficulty in answering that question:

             The Rapture will happen without any preceding sign or warning.
             However, the doctrine of imminence remains among the most difficult in the Christian faith.
      The problem is simple: On one hand, believers are urged to be aware of various signs that suggest the
      end of the Church Age....
             But on the other hand, Christians are cautioned not to set dates on the basis of visible geopolitical
      developments....
             The faithful have a challenge. We sometimes feel like a living dichotomy—looking for signs,
      excited by developments on the world stage, but constantly reminding ourselves that the Rapture is a
      signless event.3

                                               Illustration 2. Fig Hinge




       A similar situation might exist regarding the fig tree (Mt 24:32-35). The parable of the fig tree might be a
hinge, applying to both the beginning and end of the tribulation. The preceding context is certainly talking about
the tribulation (Mt 24:1-31), and the proceeding context is talking apparently about the rapture (Mt 24:36-51). At
the most basic level, the figure is a simple everyday illustration from nature being used to make a very simplistic
point. At a deeper level, the fig tree might signify the nation of Israel. If the fig tree is a hinge passage, then “this
generation” that “will not pass away until all these things take place” would be a hinge generation, and “all these
things” would, naturally enough, encompass both the events of the rapture and tribulation. The Lord’s being “right
at the door” (Lk 24:33) would be a hinge verse, having application to the present, just as James insists (Jam 5:9).
But if this is the case, then, as a hinge passage, the fig tree would indicate the approach not only of the end of the
tribulation but its beginning as well, thus the time of the rapture. This hypothesis accords well with the simplistic
understanding as well, in which this simple figure from nature is being used to picture the coming judgment. Since
this judgment is to occur both at the end of the church age and the end of the tribulation, posing that it anticipates
both is natural enough. In that case, “this generation” would not merely be the Jews alive at the time the tribulation
occurs but also, more explicitly, the Jewish generation alive when the nation of Israel budded (either in 1948 or
1967). They will see all these things, including the start of the millennial kingdom (Lk 21:31). In conjunction with
Ps 90:10, one popular computation would be to say that 1948 + 80 – 10 = 2018 as the latest date possible for the
rapture. Of course, it could come have come before then—we do not know the day or hour.4

                                                       Basileia
       All believers will be with the Lord in His kingdom (basileia) . In fact, positionally we are already with Him
in His heavenly kingdom (Col 1:13). Indeed, we are, even now, seated with Him in the heavenly places (Eph 2:6).
As I type this sentence, seated here at my computer in my earthly home, I am, from an earthly point of view, absent
from the Lord. Nevertheless, by faith I acknowledge that I am simultaneously seated with Christ in the heavenly
places from a heavenly point of view. Yet, I have no trouble relating to Paul’s words, as I type these words, that I
long to go home and be at home with the Lord (2Cor 5:8). I am at home and yet so far from home that I get homesick.
So much so that tears role down my face as I type. We know we are not home yet. Our hearts yearn to go home.
We want to be with the Lord so much that it hurts. We are not yet with the Lord in the sense that we will be when
Paul says that at the time of the rapture we will always be with the Lord (1Thess 4:17). Uppy Daddy, Uppy!
Nevertheless, when we are raptured and are with the Lord in His heavenly city (per my TOD model) or with the
Lord in the clouds (per Crawford’s model), we will not yet be in His earthly kingdom. We will still have to wait
seven to ten years, depending on what model you prefer, before the Lord sets up His kingdom on Earth. Then all
believers will be with Him in this earthly kingdom (per my TOD model), or some believers may be cast back into
the heavenly extension of that kingdom (per Crawford’s model). In any event, when His kingdom comes, we will
be there with Him, somewhere in His kingdom. He will be in the millennial kingdom. We will be with Him.

Aion and Aionios
       As another example of duality, consider the Greek adjective used for eternal life, which is aionios. TOD
provides an extended lexical discussion as to why aionios should be interpreted to mean eternal within the NT.
Some misthologists disagree with that assessment. For example, in Darkness (pp. 521-522) I pointed out that
Whipple is inconsistent in acknowledging that rewards are eternal and then attempting to limit the duration of a
believer’s negative reward. For example, in 2Pet 1:11, Whipple correctly acknowledges that aionios is used to talk
about our abundant misthological entrance into the eternal kingdom. Splendid! Yet he limits the fires of Gehenna
to 1000 years by translating aionios as age lasting in Mt 18:8-90. But if rewards are eternal, as Whipple and I agree,
then it makes no sense for him to try to limit negative rewards to 1000 years. Thus, I dismissed his misthological
interpretation of Gehenna on the grounds of his inconsistency.
       Chitwood, on the other hand, has a way around that inconsistency. He rejects the interpretation that 2Pet 1:11
is talking about the eternal kingdom. Instead, he takes it as talking about the age-lasting kingdom. In short, an
abundant misthological entrance into the millennial kingdom is at stake according to Chitwood, but entrance into
the eternal kingdom is a level playing field. By limiting all rewards to the millennial kingdom, he has a
hermeneutical sword by which to rightly divide, in his opinion, soteriological texts from misthological texts. Thus,
if a passage is talking about receiving aionios life as a free gift by faith, then it must be referring to eternal life. If
the passage is talking about winning, earning, or hoping for aionios life, then it must be referring to millennial life.13
       Accordingly, in Chitwoodian misthology, if a passage is talking about rewards and uses aionios, then aionios
must be translated as age-lasting rather than as eternal in that instance because rewards are not eternal. Context is
king, and it must determine if aionios means eternal or age-lasting in any given instance. Secondly, the only way
the Greek NT can express the concept of eternal apart from contextual considerations is with the plural from of
aion (age). Aion is the nominal form of aionios. Thus, unless the Greek writer uses the plural noun form for ages,
one must consult the context to know if the concept conveyed is millennial or eternal. And because Chitwood has
concluded beforehand that rewards are not eternal, he is being consistent, within his hermeneutical approach, to

Riddle
    The heart of Chitwood’s argument against eternal life being considered a reward may be reduced to the

Bridal Righteousness in Isaiah 61:10
       Many interpreters try to shirk away from the misthopological overtones of the passage by making a pure
assumption that the wedding garments refer to imputed righteousness, as if the garments were provided freely by
the host (Is 61:10). However, Isaiah merely affirms: “I will rejoice greatly in the Lord, my soul will exult in my
God; for He has clothed me with garments of salvation, He has wrapped me with a robe of righteousness, as a
bridegroom decks himself with a garland, and as a bride adorns herself with her jewels” (Is 61:10). Did you see the
word freely in the biblical text? Neither did I. Such interpreters interject it. Martin appropriately rejects inserting
the word freely (BKC). The OT context is decisively in favor of Martin’s rejection.
       To be sure, a favorite OT text found chapters away affirms: “All our righteous deeds are like a filthy garment”
(Is 64:6). Fair enough. This text establishes the need for imputed righteousness. Still, it does not nullify the need of
imparted righteousness for misthological themes. For those who have been made imputationally righteous, the
possibility of imparted righteousness becomes a valid possibility. In the context of Is 61:10, imputational
righteousness is not the theme.
       Jesus is the speaker in this chapter from Isaiah 61: “The Spirit of the Lord God is upon me, because the Lord
has anointed me to bring good news to the afflicted; He has sent me to bind up the brokenhearted, to proclaim

and righteousness is Jesus and His Bride the church. In Isaiah, Jesus is the Bridegroom putting on the robe. The

righteousness you can see, and praise you can hear. The next two verses confirm this point: “For Zion's sake I will
not keep silent, and for Jerusalem’s sake I will not keep quiet, until her righteousness goes forth like brightness, and
her salvation like a torch that is burning. And the nations will see your righteousness, and all kings your glory”

this passage.

      In addition to the millennial kingdom, Johnson affirms a minimum of three kingdom concepts: the spiritual
form of the kingdom into which believers are born, and the mystery form into which tares are sowed, and the future

                One must remember that there are at least three kingdom concepts in the New Testament. The
         word kingdom may refer to the eternal invisible kingdom, that kingdom into which all believers are
         born (cf. John 3:3–5; Acts 8:12; Rom. 14:7). It may also refer to the earthly mediatorial kingdom of
         our Lord Jesus Christ, commonly referred to as the millennial kingdom (cf. Matt. 4:17; 10:2; Mark
         1:15). And also the term may refer to the present mystery form of the kingdom (cf. Matt. 13:34, 35,
         41–43). (Emphasis his.)86

Even though he is a dispensationalist, Johnson links the new birth with the eternal invisible kingdom. Apparently,
he poses that, at the point of new birth, believers are born into a spiritual form of the kingdom, and he regards this
spiritual form of the kingdom into which they are spiritually born to be the universal kingdom. Ryrie does better by
distinguishing the spiritual form of the kingdom associated with spiritual birth from the universal kingdom, thus
listing four kingdom concepts:

                 1. The universal kingdom. The Scriptures reveal God as Ruler of the whole world
         (1 Chron. 9:11; Ps. 145:13). As such He exercises jurisdiction over the nations of the world,
         appointing rulers of His choosing and judging the world (Ps. 96:13; Dan. 2:37). In Jewish thought this
         concept of the kingdom began with Adam, was disfigured when sin entered, yet continued on until
         Abraham, who recalled people to the kingdom with only partial success (witness the rebellion of
         Sodom and Gomorrah). However, when Israel accepted the Mosaic Law, this kingdom was
         reestablished, though rebellion erupted almost immediately (with the golden calf) and repeatedly
         throughout Israel’s history. Only the godly remnant revived the kingdom. Only Messiah would bring
         the full realization of this kingdom.
                 Christian theology acknowledges this concept of a universal kingdom (though usually including
         angels in it, which Judaism did not). God is Ruler of the nations (Rev. 15:3), and ultimately they will
         answer to Him when He judges them (Ps. 110:6).
                 In summary, in the universal kingdom God is the Ruler; He rules over all; and He does it in all
         time and eternity.
                 2. The Davidic/messianic kingdom. Both Judaism and premillennial Christian theology give a
         major place to this concept of kingdom. It is Davidic in that the promises concerning the kingdom
         were made in the great covenant with David (2 Sam. 7:12–16). It is messianic since Messiah will be
         the Ruler. It will be realized at the second advent of Christ when He will establish His kingdom and
         fulfill those promises made to David. (See further discussion under eschatology, beginning with
         chapter 77.)
                 In summary, in the Davidic messianic kingdom Christ is the Ruler; He will rule over the earth
         and its inhabitants during the one thousand years that follow His second coming.
                 3. The mystery form of the kingdom. In Matthew 13 Christ revealed mysteries concerning the
         concept of the kingdom (v. 11). In accord with the meaning of “mystery,” this means He told the
         disciples some things about the kingdom that were previously unknown. This idea of the kingdom,
         then, began when the Lord was teaching and will end at His second advent (vv. 39–40) . In other
         words, it is the concept of kingdom used to encompass the period between the two advents of Christ.
         The Ruler is God. The ruled are people on the earth who have related themselves in a positive, neutral,
         or negative way to “Christendom” (including true believers, professing people, rejecters, and even
         opponents). The time is the period between His comings.

second translation is preferable. It is imperative in function even if it is indicative in form. Alternately, perhaps, one

readers be found to have lived holy lives when their lives are assessed at the future judgment? The fact that this
holiness is commanded rather than promised leaves the matter unsettled. Each reader must answer that question for
him or herself by the life that he or she lives.

        The multidimensional duality of this verse will be summarized using the following translation, “But you are
(to be) a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, God’s special treasure” (1Pet 2:9; TM). They already are
chosen soteriologically so that they might be chosen misthologically. They already are royal provisionally so that
they might become royal misthologically. They already are holy, so they are to be holy. They already are God’s
special treasure soteriologically, but they can become God’s special treasure misthologically. The duality is
multidimensional: (1) already versus not yet, (2) soteriological versus misthological, (3) potential versus actual.
Because of this duality, my use of 1Pet 2:9 (within Sealed and Secure) in defense of unconditional security is
justified. Yet misthological conditionality is also part of the scope as demonstrated here.
        The affirmation of the already-not-yet dimensions of this verse naturally refute the attempts of those who
attempt to deny the already-not-yet dimensions of the kingdom. Believers presently function in a limited capacity
as a kingdom of priest. Because believers enter this role at the point of spiritual birth, we may affirm that in a limited
mystical capacity they enter the spiritual kingdom soteriologically when they become believers.

      Two twin objections frequently made regarding rewards in general are particularly applicable to the
discussion of bridal misthology. The first objection might be expressed syllogistically:

      1. The desire to be the Bride, thus better off in Heaven by being better than others, is selfish.

      3. Therefore, the Bride cannot be composed of those who wanted to be better off than others by being better

      2. There is no envy in Heaven.
      3. Therefore, the Bride cannot be composed of only faithful believers.

                                                      Conclusion
       The twin arguments of selfishness and envy cannot be legitimately maintained against biblical bridal
misthology. The primary premise of the syllogism using selfishness against bridal misthology is in error. The desire
to be the Bride is not selfish. This desire for personal gain is not at the expense of others. We do not step on others
in our endeavor to become the Bride, we stoop to serve others, elevating them above ourselves. The Lord was not
being selfish when He created a world in which evil would come about, resulting in the creation of a dragon that
would have to be slain at the cost of His own life, to procure a Bride who would share His nature and display His
beauty.
       The syllogistic objection based on envy is shortsighted in its second premise, which assumes that no envy
exists in Heaven. A godly envy is permissible, even to be expected, in Heaven. Unfaithful believers will have a
longing aroused by the beauty of the Bride. They will wish that they had followed the Lord, thus qualifying for such
beauty themselves, and will regret that they did not do so. However, they will admire rather than resent the beauty
of those who rightfully qualified for such beauty. They will not wish that they could take such beauty from those
who rightfully qualify for it for themselves.
